## Part 1. Why Is Historiography Not a List of Historians and Schools?

### What does `historiography` study when it studies history-writing?

`Historiography` is often introduced as “the history of historical writing.” The phrase is useful, but it becomes misleading if it turns the subject into a sequence of authors, books, schools, and doctrines. A more exact definition would begin elsewhere: historiography studies how the past has been made into historical knowledge. It asks what different ages believed the past to be, what kinds of traces they trusted, how they distinguished evidence from rumor, how they explained change, and why they thought history should be written at all.

A chronicle, a saint’s life, a humanist edition of an ancient text, a national history, a diplomatic monograph, a Marxist social history, an Annales study of long-term structures, a microhistory of one miller, a gender history of domestic life, a postcolonial reading of an imperial archive, and a digital reconstruction of migration all belong to the broad world of historical writing. They do not differ only in topic. They differ in their assumptions about time, causality, evidence, authority, narrative, and the relation between past and present. A history of historiography must therefore ask more than “who wrote what?” It must ask how the standards of credible historical knowledge changed.

**Definition:** `Historiography` has two closely related meanings. It can mean the body of historical writing produced by a culture, period, school, or scholar. It can also mean the reflective study of that writing: its sources, methods, concepts, narrative forms, institutional settings, and public uses. In this course, the second meaning controls the first. Historical works matter not only because they contain information about the past, but because they show how different societies learned to organize the past into intelligible form.

This approach changes the shape of the subject. **Herodotus** is not important merely because he stands near the beginning of Western historical writing, but because his work shows an early effort to separate inquiry from inherited story. **Augustine** matters not only as a Christian thinker, but because he transformed historical time into a drama of creation, fall, redemption, and judgment. **Lorenzo Valla** matters because philological analysis could become an instrument of historical criticism. **Ranke** matters because the archive, the seminar, the footnote, and the trained reading of documents became central to professional historical knowledge. The **Annales School** matters because it shifted historical attention from events and rulers to structures, geography, economy, mentality, and long duration. Later cultural, gender, postcolonial, and memory studies matter because they asked what earlier methods had made invisible.

A list of historians cannot show this movement. It can name figures, but it cannot explain why certain forms of evidence became persuasive, why certain subjects became worthy of study, why some kinds of writing were accepted as scholarly and others excluded, or why the meaning of historical truth changed. Historiography becomes serious only when it treats historical writing as an evolving practice of knowledge.

### Why is the past not identical with history?

The past happened, but history is written. This distinction is simple enough to sound obvious, yet almost every difficulty in historical method follows from it. Events do not survive as complete objects. They leave traces: documents, memories, ruins, images, legal records, official reports, private letters, account books, oral testimony, statistical series, landscapes, bodies, institutions, rituals, names, silences. Historical knowledge begins when those traces are selected, criticized, compared, interpreted, and narrated.

A lost poem can be discovered. A wall can be excavated. A charter can be edited. But “the fall of Rome,” “the Reformation,” “the French Revolution,” “industrialization,” “the making of the modern state,” or “the rise of capitalism” is not discovered in that same direct way. Such objects are constructed by historical inquiry. They are not invented without evidence, but they do not exist as ready-made units waiting in the archive. A historian decides which events belong together, where the temporal boundary falls, which actors matter, which causes are relevant, and what kind of explanation is being attempted.

This does not reduce history to fiction. The past constrains historical writing through surviving evidence, chronology, material conditions, and the possibility of contradiction. A historian cannot responsibly claim that an event occurred before the institutions that made it possible, or that a person wrote a document after death, or that a law had effects in a place where it was never applied. Yet constraint is not the same as automatic meaning. Evidence can limit interpretation without generating interpretation by itself.

The difference between past and history also explains why every historical object depends on scale. A famine may be written as a climatic event, a failure of state capacity, a crisis of markets, a breakdown of local charity, an experience of hunger, a political memory, or a moral catastrophe. The object shifts with the question. The same traces can be arranged into different histories, not because anything goes, but because historical reality is layered. A change in scale changes what counts as relevant evidence.

### What turns a trace into a `source`?

A `source` is not simply an old thing. It is a trace of the past made usable by a historical question. A coin, a law, a diary, a ruin, a census table, a newspaper article, a photograph, a song, a map, or a court record becomes a source only when it is asked to bear evidence for something. The same material can serve different inquiries. A coin may be evidence for trade, state authority, imperial imagery, monetary policy, metallurgy, religious symbolism, or the circulation of political messages. A court record may be evidence for law, violence, gender, village conflict, administrative language, popular belief, or the power of the state to classify persons.

**Definition:** A `source` (*source*) is any surviving trace that can be used as evidence in relation to a historical question. Its evidentiary value is not fixed once and for all. It depends on provenance, form, transmission, context, comparison, and the specific claim being made.

The older distinction between `primary sources` and `secondary sources` remains useful, but only if treated as relational rather than mechanical. A primary source is usually a source produced within or near the historical process being studied; a secondary source is a later interpretation based on other evidence. Yet the same text can shift category. **Thucydides’** history may be a secondary account of the Peloponnesian War if the question concerns the war’s sequence of events, but it becomes a primary source if the question concerns classical Greek political thought, elite rhetoric, or ancient standards of historical explanation. A nineteenth-century national history may be secondary in relation to the medieval kingdom it describes, but primary in relation to nineteenth-century nationalism.

This relational view prevents a common error: treating primary sources as automatically more truthful than later scholarship. A diplomatic letter written during a crisis is primary, but it may conceal motives, distort events, or speak in formulaic language. A later scholarly article is secondary, but it may compare dozens of primary sources, correct earlier errors, and identify the limits of each document. Historical research depends on the interplay between both. Primary sources provide traces; secondary scholarship provides accumulated criticism, context, and debate.

### Why are sources both the foundation and the prison of historical knowledge?

Historical knowledge cannot do without sources, yet sources never provide full access to the past. They are partial, situated, mediated, and often damaged by survival itself. Some were produced by institutions of power; some by people trying to persuade, excuse, command, remember, accuse, justify, or deceive; some by administrative routines that never intended to preserve lived experience; some by hostile observers; some by later copyists, editors, translators, and archivists. The historian begins with materials that are necessary and insufficient at the same time.

This is the basic paradox of historical knowledge. Without sources, there is no scholarly history. With sources alone, there is still no history, because sources do not explain themselves. They must be decoded. Their form must be understood. Their silence must be noticed. Their claims must be compared with other claims. Their categories must be treated as historical facts in their own right, not as neutral containers.

A medieval miracle story, for instance, may be weak evidence for the physical occurrence it describes, yet strong evidence for local sanctity, institutional competition, devotional expectation, or the persuasive strategies of a monastery. A colonial census may give numbers, but those numbers may be organized by racial, ethnic, legal, or administrative categories produced by colonial rule. A revolutionary manifesto may claim to speak for “the people,” but the historian must ask who authored it, who circulated it, who could read it, and which groups were included or excluded by its language.

**Key judgment:** The reliability of a source is not an all-or-nothing property. A source is reliable only in relation to a question. It may be unreliable for one claim and indispensable for another.

This is why `source criticism` cannot be reduced to exposing lies. It asks what a source is, how it came into being, what it can prove, what it cannot prove, and what kind of reality it constructs. A forged charter cannot prove the legal transaction it pretends to record, but it may reveal later institutional ambition, property conflict, or the prestige attached to ancient rights. A biased chronicle may misrepresent an enemy, but it can show how enmity was imagined and justified. Historical criticism does not merely discard flawed sources; it learns how to use them within their limits.

### What does `source criticism` actually involve?

`Source criticism` (*source criticism*) is the disciplined examination of a source’s origin, form, transmission, perspective, and usable range. It has an external and an internal dimension, though in practice the two constantly interact.

`External criticism` asks questions about identity and transmission. Who produced the source? When and where was it made? Is it an original, a copy, an edition, a translation, a later reconstruction, or a fragment? What material form does it have? Does the handwriting, vocabulary, formula, seal, paper, ink, layout, or institutional style fit the date it claims? Has it been altered, abbreviated, interpolated, damaged, or preserved in a selective archive? These questions became especially important in traditions of philology, paleography, diplomatics, archival science, and textual editing.

`Internal criticism` asks questions about meaning and credibility. What did the author or producer intend? What could the observer actually know? Was the observer present, informed, competent, pressured, partisan, afraid, dependent, or writing for a patron? What genre shaped the statement? What did the source assume as obvious? What was left unsaid because it was forbidden, irrelevant, shameful, routine, or outside the author’s categories of perception?

A source can pass external criticism and still require internal suspicion. An authentic police report may be genuine as a document and still distort the actions of those being policed. A correctly dated newspaper report may reproduce rumor. A private diary may be intimate without being transparent. A statistical table may look precise while resting on changing categories, uneven reporting, or administrative pressure. The first question is whether the source is what it claims to be. The second is what kind of knowledge it can sustain.

### How does a historical `fact` come into being?

A historical `fact` (*fact*) is not a loose piece of information waiting inside a document. It is a claim stabilized by evidence. A date, a name, a price, a military order, a law, a demographic figure, or a witness statement becomes a historical fact only after the historian has established that the source is usable for that claim and that competing evidence does not defeat it.

This process can be simple or complex. The date of a treaty may be established with high confidence if official copies, diplomatic correspondence, seals, and subsequent implementation converge. The causes of that treaty require a different order of argument. They demand attention to diplomacy, military pressure, fiscal crisis, ideology, personal rivalry, public opinion, and the expectations of the parties involved. A fact about occurrence is not the same as an explanation of significance.

The term `evidentiary satisfaction` can help clarify this difference. Historical proof is rarely absolute. It usually means that the evidence is sufficient for the claim being made, that alternative explanations have been considered, that counterevidence has been addressed, and that the limits of certainty are visible. A narrow claim needs one kind of evidence; a broad claim about social transformation, religious change, class formation, national identity, or colonial power needs a wider evidentiary architecture.

**Example:** A set of parish registers may support a claim about baptism, marriage, and burial patterns. To turn those records into an argument about family structure, demographic change, religious practice, or social discipline, the historian must ask how complete the registers are, who maintained them, what population they exclude, whether categories changed, and how they compare with wills, tax lists, court records, letters, or local customs. The document provides data; historical explanation requires a chain of criticism.

The fact, then, is neither raw data nor arbitrary construction. It is a disciplined achievement. It rests on materials that existed before the historian, but it takes form through questions, criticism, comparison, and argument.

### Why does historical explanation change over time?

Historians do not only disagree about facts. They disagree about what kind of explanation is appropriate. Ancient historians often explained events through character, prudence, fortune, political error, and the fate of cities. Christian historians organized events through providence and sacred time. Enlightenment historians emphasized civilization, manners, commerce, law, reason, and comparative progress. Romantic historians looked for spirit, individuality, peoplehood, and national character. Nineteenth-century professionals emphasized documentary reconstruction and the individuality of historical contexts. Positivists and social theorists searched for laws, structures, and causal regularities. Marxist historians placed production, class, and material conflict at the center. The Annales tradition turned toward geography, economy, mentalities, and long duration. Cultural historians later examined meaning, language, representation, and practice.

These changes are not decorative. They reshape research. A historian looking for providence reads events differently from a historian looking for constitutional development. A historian of diplomacy privileges different materials from a historian of family life. A historian of class conflict asks different questions from a historian of memory. Every explanatory model makes certain connections visible and others less visible.

`Causality` is therefore one of the hardest problems in historical writing. Historical events rarely have one cause. They usually emerge from interacting conditions: institutions, material resources, beliefs, technologies, political decisions, social structures, emotions, accidents, and inherited constraints. A serious explanation must avoid both single-cause reduction and empty complexity. It must show how factors relate, not merely list them.

### Why are historical methods also political choices?

Historical method is political not because every historian writes propaganda, but because methods decide what can be seen. The choice of scale, source, category, and explanation distributes attention. A history based on state archives will more easily recover officials, laws, diplomacy, administration, taxation, war, and institutional memory. It will not automatically recover the experiences of the illiterate, the enslaved, the colonized, the poor, women, migrants, children, or defeated groups. Those lives may appear only through court records, police reports, missionary writings, estate accounts, petitions, oral memory, material culture, or the silences of the archive.

A national history may create continuity where the past was fragmented. A class analysis may reveal exploitation while flattening cultural difference. A cultural history may reveal meaning while losing sight of material constraint. A quantitative study may discover large-scale patterns while obscuring individual experience. A microhistory may recover texture and agency while risking overinterpretation of an exceptional case. None of these methods is simply wrong. Each has power and cost.

**Definition:** `Archive silence` refers to the absence, distortion, or indirect presence of people, events, and experiences in surviving records. It is not merely a lack of documents. It is often the result of power: who could write, who was recorded, whose records were preserved, and which institutions decided what mattered.

The political dimension of method becomes clear when new historical subjects appear. Women’s history, labor history, African American history, colonial and postcolonial history, queer history, oral history, and memory studies did not merely add neglected topics to an existing framework. They often required new ways of reading. Hostile records had to be read against the grain. Formulaic language had to be treated as evidence of institutional classification. Silence became meaningful. Experience had to be reconstructed through fragments.

This is why historiography must include method. A change in subject matter without a change in method may leave old exclusions intact. To ask new historical questions often means learning to read old materials differently, to search for new materials, or to admit that certain kinds of knowledge can only be partial.

### What kind of `objectivity` can historical knowledge still claim?

The ideal of `objectivity` (*objectivity*) has never disappeared from historical scholarship, but it cannot mean a view from nowhere. Every historian writes from within language, training, institutions, archives, categories, and present concerns. The historian chooses a topic, frames a problem, selects sources, and writes a narrative. There is no historical knowledge without perspective.

Yet the existence of perspective does not make all interpretations equal. Historical objectivity is best understood as procedural and public rather than absolute. It depends on whether the historian identifies sources, explains method, handles contrary evidence, distinguishes evidence from inference, avoids claims stronger than the materials permit, and allows other researchers to test the argument. Objectivity is not the absence of interpretation. It is interpretation made accountable.

This view avoids two weak positions. One imagines that facts speak for themselves once sources are properly collected. The other imagines that because all history is mediated by language and perspective, historical writing is only narrative invention. Historical practice works between these extremes. Sources are not transparent windows, but they are not meaningless. Narratives are constructed, but not unconstrained. Some claims are better supported, more coherent, more careful with evidence, and more open to correction than others.

**Key judgment:** Historical truth is not the same as certainty. It is a disciplined form of knowledge produced under conditions of incompleteness.

This understanding also clarifies why later critiques of objectivity do not abolish historical method. They make it more demanding. A historian must ask not only whether a document is authentic, but what kind of reality it constructs; not only whether a category appears in the source, but who produced that category; not only whether a statement is supported, but what the archive makes difficult to know. The critique of certainty does not end historical research. It forces historical research to become more self-aware.

### Why must the history of historiography include institutions?

Historical writing is shaped not only by ideas, but by institutions. Temples, courts, monasteries, city governments, royal administrations, churches, universities, archives, learned societies, museums, journals, presses, research seminars, funding bodies, and digital databases all influence what historians can know and how they write.

In ancient Greece, the historian worked within traditions of oral report, political memory, travel, civic identity, and literary performance. In the Christian Middle Ages, monasteries, bishoprics, courts, and scriptoria preserved and organized historical memory. Renaissance humanists depended on manuscripts, patronage, libraries, and the recovery of classical texts. Reformation historians wrote in the context of confessional conflict. Nineteenth-century professional historians depended on universities, state archives, seminars, and scholarly journals. Twentieth-century social historians relied on expanding universities, social science methods, demographic records, and political movements that made ordinary people historically visible.

Institutions do not mechanically determine thought, but they create conditions of possibility. They preserve some materials and neglect others. They reward some kinds of writing and marginalize others. They train researchers to recognize certain forms of evidence as serious. They give authority to particular methods. They decide, quietly but powerfully, what counts as historical scholarship.

This institutional dimension prevents historiography from becoming a pure history of ideas. Ideas about evidence, causality, and objectivity become powerful only when embedded in practices: copying manuscripts, editing texts, preserving charters, building archives, teaching seminars, reviewing books, maintaining journals, classifying documents, designing databases. The craft of history is intellectual, but it is also material and social.

### What four questions organize this course?

A coherent study of Western historiography can be organized around four recurring questions.

The first asks: what is the past? Is it a storehouse of examples, a sacred drama, a record of political action, a civilizational process, a national inheritance, a structure of social relations, a field of meanings, a site of trauma, or a contested memory? Every historiographical tradition begins with some answer, explicit or implicit.

The second asks: what counts as a source? Oral testimony, epic, inscription, scripture, chronicle, charter, diplomatic dispatch, tax list, court record, census, image, object, landscape, interview, database, and silence have not always carried the same authority. The history of historiography is partly the history of how new materials became evidence.

The third asks: what makes a claim reliable? Different periods developed different techniques: eyewitness judgment, moral and political prudence, theological authority, philology, chronology, diplomatics, archival method, statistics, social theory, anthropology, discourse analysis, oral history practice, and digital reconstruction. These techniques do not merely support historical writing; they define what historical writing can responsibly claim.

The fourth asks: how is change explained? Historians have explained change through divine providence, moral decline, fortune, political prudence, constitutional development, national spirit, economic structure, class conflict, environment, demography, technology, discourse, emotion, memory, contingency, and agency. Every major historiographical movement can be read as a change in causal imagination.

These questions will guide the sequence that follows. Greek historiography will appear as the emergence of inquiry from myth and memory. Roman historiography will show history as civic memory, moral judgment, and imperial reflection. Christian historiography will transform time and causality through salvation history. Medieval histories will organize peoples, dynasties, crusades, saints, cities, and communities. Renaissance humanism will make textual criticism historically powerful. Reformation polemic and learned scholarship will intensify source criticism. Early modern historians will respond to new states, new worlds, and universal history. Enlightenment historiography will turn history toward civilization, progress, and comparison. Romantic and national historians will give the past emotional and political force. Ranke and historicism will professionalize historical knowledge. Positivism, Marxism, economic history, social history, Annales history, new cultural history, gender history, postcolonial history, memory studies, and postmodern critique will continue to expand and disturb the field.

### Why begin with historiography before beginning research?

Historical research without historiographical awareness tends to mistake inherited categories for natural ones. It may assume that the nation-state is the obvious unit of analysis, that written documents are the most serious sources, that political events are the most important events, that archives are neutral repositories, or that causality is a simple line from cause to effect. Historiography interrupts these habits.

To study historiography is to learn that every research operation has a history. The archive, the footnote, the source edition, the period label, the national narrative, the statistical table, the oral interview, the memory site, and the digital database all carry assumptions about what the past is and how it can be known. They are tools, but they are not innocent tools.

This does not weaken historical research. It makes it more exact. A researcher who understands the history of method can choose tools more deliberately. A source can be read for what it was meant to say, what it accidentally reveals, what it suppresses, and what later institutions made it mean. A narrative can distinguish event, structure, experience, memory, and representation. An explanation can avoid both single-cause reduction and vague complexity. A claim can mark the difference between certainty, probability, inference, and speculation.

Historiography is therefore not an ornamental preface to “real history.” It is the discipline that makes historical research self-conscious. It shows that history is neither mere storytelling nor pure data recovery. It is a structured inquiry into the past through surviving traces, governed by changing but criticizable standards of evidence, method, explanation, and public accountability. The development of Western historiography is the development of those standards: their invention, expansion, misuse, critique, and renewal.
