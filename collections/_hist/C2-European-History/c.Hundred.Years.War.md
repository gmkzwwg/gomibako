## English

* 12th–early 14th century

  * **The feudal problem of English lands in France becomes a structural cause of war**

    * **Background**: The **Hundred Years’ War** was not a single continuous campaign, but an intermittent struggle produced by overlapping disputes over **feudal lordship**, **dynastic succession**, **territorial sovereignty**, and **commercial power**. Its deeper roots lay in the unusual position of the **king of England** as both an independent monarch and, for lands such as **Aquitaine/Guyenne**, a formal vassal of the **king of France**. This created a constitutional contradiction: one king could demand homage from another king, while the other could resist that demand without appearing to surrender royal dignity.
    * **Process**: The conflict developed from centuries of Anglo-French friction after the **Angevin** expansion placed large continental territories under rulers who also held the English crown. French kings gradually recovered much of this territory, but **Gascony** remained economically valuable and politically awkward. It exported wine, connected Atlantic commerce, and gave England a direct stake in southwestern France. The French crown, meanwhile, treated the duchy as a fief subject to royal jurisdiction, appeals, confiscation, and intervention.
    * **Significance**: The war should not be reduced to a simple national conflict between “England” and “France” in the modern sense. It began inside a medieval political order where personal lordship, inheritance law, homage, aristocratic networks, and royal courts still structured sovereignty. The later war helped *harden* more centralized royal identities, but those identities were partly a consequence rather than a starting point.
  * **The Capetian succession crisis turns a feudal dispute into a dynastic claim**

    * **Background**: The direct male line of the **Capetian dynasty** weakened when the sons of **Philip IV** failed to produce surviving male heirs. The succession question became especially dangerous because **Edward III of England** was the grandson of **Philip IV** through his mother, **Isabella of France**, while **Philip VI of Valois** descended through the male line from a collateral branch. The French political elite favored the Valois claim, partly because it avoided placing the French crown under the rule of the English king.
    * **Process**: The French succession settlement rejected inheritance through the female line for the crown, though the legal reasoning later associated with the **Salic Law** was clarified and politicized over time. Edward initially accepted political realities more than he abandoned potential claims. The problem was not simply whether Edward had a theoretical dynastic argument, but whether French magnates would accept a ruler whose power base, fiscal system, and strategic interests lay across the Channel.
    * **Common Pitfall**: It is misleading to imagine the war as beginning because Edward suddenly “wanted France.” The dynastic claim became useful because it interacted with the older dispute over **Aquitaine**, the Anglo-Scottish conflict, Flemish commerce, and the French crown’s capacity to use feudal law against English continental possessions.
* 1328

  * **The death of Charles IV settles the crown on the Valois line but leaves Edward III with a usable grievance**

    * **Background**: **Charles IV of France** died without a surviving son, ending the direct male Capetian line. The French succession crisis did not automatically create war, but it created a dynastic argument that could later be attached to older territorial and feudal disputes.
    * **Process**: French political society accepted **Philip VI of Valois** as king rather than **Edward III**, whose claim came through his mother, **Isabella of France**. This outcome reflected law, precedent, political interest, and the unwillingness of French elites to accept the English king as ruler of France.
    * **Significance**: The settlement of 1328 created the ideological weapon that Edward could later use when the conflict over **Guyenne** escalated. Without the feudal dispute over English lands in France, the succession issue might have remained dormant; without the succession issue, the feudal dispute would have been narrower.
* 1337

  * **May 24, Philip VI’s confiscation of Guyenne gives the conflict its conventional starting point**

    * **Background**: By convention, the war is said to begin when **Philip VI** confiscated the English-held duchy of **Guyenne**, though this act followed earlier disputes over English fiefs in France. The immediate legal act was Philip’s confiscation of Guyenne on **May 24, 1337**, but the deeper quarrel over English-held French fiefs had been building since the twelfth century.
    * **Trigger**: Philip’s action was not merely symbolic. Confiscation meant that the French crown claimed the right to strip **Edward III** of his continental duchy for failures of feudal obedience. Edward could either submit to a status that made his continental power dependent on French royal judgment, or escalate the dispute into a broader war over legitimacy.
    * **Process**: The opening phase did not instantly become a grand war of conquest. It involved diplomacy, alliance-building, maritime preparation, fiscal extraction, and attempts to mobilize support in **Flanders**, **Gascony**, and among opponents of French royal policy. The French crown used legal authority and territorial pressure; the English crown answered by widening the dispute beyond feudal procedure.
    * **Significance**: This event matters because it shows how medieval law could produce war. The legal language of forfeiture and homage did not prevent violence; it gave violence an institutional form. The war began as a conflict over jurisdiction, but jurisdiction in a feudal monarchy meant taxation, courts, lordship, military obligation, and prestige.
  * **The Anglo-Flemish connection turns dynastic war into commercial and diplomatic war**

    * **Background**: **Flanders** depended heavily on the English wool trade, while England depended on access to continental markets and allies. The French crown had influence over Flemish politics, but urban commercial interests often pulled in a different direction. This meant that the war was never purely a noble or dynastic affair; textile towns, merchants, customs revenues, shipping routes, and credit networks mattered from the beginning.
    * **Process**: Edward sought allies who could pressure France from the north and give his claim practical military value. Flemish towns, imperial princes, and border powers entered the conflict because the war overlapped with their own interests. Coalition warfare was expensive, unstable, and dependent on subsidies, but it allowed England to compensate for France’s greater population and resources.
    * **Significance**: The early war reveals a central pattern of the whole conflict: England often tried to offset French demographic and territorial depth through **naval mobility**, **commercial alliances**, **contract armies**, and **political fractures inside France**.
* 1340

  * **June 24, the Battle of Sluys gives England control of the Channel crossing**

    * **Background**: Control of the **English Channel** was essential because English armies could not operate in France without secure maritime movement. French naval pressure threatened invasion, commerce, and communication between England and its continental allies.
    * **Process**: At **Sluys**, an English fleet under **Edward III** attacked a large Franco-Genoese fleet. Medieval naval warfare often resembled land combat transferred to ships: vessels were locked together, archers and men-at-arms fought at close range, and boarding actions decided the issue. The English victory damaged French naval capacity and made large-scale English expeditions more feasible.
    * **Result**: England did not gain permanent mastery of every sea route, but the victory secured the immediate strategic corridor needed for campaigning in northern France. It also protected the political credibility of Edward’s continental strategy.
    * **Significance**: Sluys shows that the Hundred Years’ War was maritime as well as territorial. The Channel was not a boundary separating two worlds; it was a military highway, commercial route, and strategic battlefield.
* 1346

  * **August 26, the Battle of Crécy demonstrates the tactical power of disciplined defensive warfare**

    * **Background**: Edward’s campaign in northern France placed a smaller English army under pressure from a larger French force. The French monarchy possessed more extensive manpower and aristocratic prestige, but battlefield success required coordination among cavalry, infantry, missile troops, and command structures. The English relied on a combination of dismounted men-at-arms, archers, prepared positioning, and tactical discipline.
    * **Process**: At **Crécy**, Edward III halted and made the French attack him rather than continuing a dangerous flight across northern France. The English placed dismounted men-at-arms in the center and archers on the wings, using the slope and field position to turn French momentum against itself. The **Genoese crossbowmen** advanced first but could not break the English line, and the mounted French nobles pressed forward in disorder behind or through them. The attack became a succession of crowded, piecemeal assaults rather than a coordinated blow. The **longbow** mattered, but terrain, fatigue, command disorder, noble impatience, and the English choice to fight defensively mattered just as much.
    * **Result**: The French suffered heavy noble losses, while Edward gained enormous military prestige. The battle exposed the vulnerability of aristocratic cavalry when forced into frontal assaults against prepared infantry and missile formations.
    * **Common Pitfall**: Crécy should not be simplified into “longbows beat knights.” The English system worked because archers, men-at-arms, terrain, command discipline, and defensive posture functioned together. The battle was a tactical event inside a wider strategy of raiding, coercion, and political bargaining.
  * **October 17, Neville’s Cross links the French war to the Anglo-Scottish frontier**

    * **Background**: The war was embedded in a wider diplomatic system. **Scotland**, long hostile to English domination, remained connected to France through the **Auld Alliance**. French support for Scotland could force England to divide attention between the continent and its northern frontier.
    * **Process**: While Edward campaigned in France, **David II of Scotland** invaded northern England. English forces defeated the Scots at **Neville’s Cross** and captured David. This weakened Scotland’s ability to relieve pressure on France and strengthened England’s strategic position.
    * **Significance**: The event shows that the Hundred Years’ War cannot be understood only through battles in France. It involved a northern British front, Channel warfare, Flemish urban politics, papal diplomacy, and Gascon local interests.
* 1347

  * **August, the capture of Calais gives England a fortified bridgehead**

    * **Background**: After Crécy, Edward besieged **Calais**, a port whose location made it strategically valuable for communication, commerce, and future invasion. A port was more useful than a temporary battlefield victory because it could anchor continuing military pressure.
    * **Process**: The siege was long and materially demanding. Calais was isolated until it surrendered. English occupation transformed the town into a fortified enclave, commercial center, and military base. The crown resettled and governed it as an English possession, making it a durable instrument of Channel strategy.
    * **Result**: Calais became England’s most important continental foothold. Even after the later loss of most English possessions in France, Calais remained under English control for generations.
    * **Significance**: Calais demonstrates the institutional side of war. Victory was not only the defeat of armies; it was the acquisition of ports, customs revenues, garrisons, administrative structures, and logistical infrastructure.
* 1348–1351

  * **The Black Death interrupts the war and changes the fiscal and social environment**

    * **Background**: The **Black Death** devastated Europe during the middle of the conflict. Its demographic effects did not end the war, but they transformed the human and fiscal conditions under which war was fought. Labor scarcity, mortality, disrupted agriculture, and social strain reshaped both kingdoms.
    * **Process**: Campaigning slowed as plague struck populations, armies, clergy, towns, and rural communities. Governments still needed revenue, but the taxable population was damaged. Lords faced labor shortages; peasants and workers confronted attempts to regulate wages and obligations; rulers struggled to finance war without provoking unrest.
    * **Significance**: The plague made prolonged war harder to sustain but did not remove the political incentives behind it. Instead, it intensified fiscal pressure and contributed to later social explosions, including revolt and resistance to taxation.
* 1355

  * **Autumn, the Black Prince’s chevauchée in Languedoc weaponizes devastation**

    * **Background**: **Edward of Woodstock**, later known as the **Black Prince**, led a major raid through southern France. English strategy often used the *chevauchée*: a fast-moving mounted raid designed to burn, loot, disrupt tax revenue, undermine royal authority, and force the enemy either to battle or humiliation.
    * **Process**: The raid moved through wealthy regions, attacking the economic base of French royal power. It did not require permanent occupation to be effective. Destruction itself became a political message: the French crown could not protect its subjects, while local elites might reconsider their loyalty or demand concessions.
    * **Result**: The campaign enriched soldiers through plunder, damaged French resources, and raised the reputation of the Black Prince. But it also deepened civilian suffering and made the war socially destructive far beyond formal battlefields.
    * **Significance**: The *chevauchée* shows why the war was catastrophic even during periods without decisive battles. Medieval warfare often targeted the enemy’s capacity to rule by attacking villages, crops, livestock, mills, roads, and taxable communities.
* 1356

  * **September 19, the Battle of Poitiers captures the French king and produces a political crisis**

    * **Background**: **John II of France** attempted to confront the Black Prince after English raiding damaged royal authority. The French crown needed to prove that it could defend the realm. The English, operating with fewer men, again relied on defensive position, archery, and disciplined coordination.
    * **Process**: At **Poitiers**, John II tried to trap the Black Prince’s raiding army before it could return safely to English-held territory. Negotiations and a brief pause failed to prevent battle. The English secured a strong position near broken ground, while French attacks came forward in stages rather than as one coordinated assault. John II fought with personal courage, staying on the field with his household and his young son **Philip**, but he was eventually surrounded and captured. This was a political disaster larger than an ordinary battlefield defeat: the king himself became a prisoner, and the French monarchy had to negotiate under humiliation while governing through the **Dauphin Charles**.
    * **Result**: France faced military defeat, royal captivity, ransom demands, fiscal crisis, and political unrest. England gained leverage far beyond what battlefield occupation alone would have provided.
    * **Significance**: Poitiers intensified the war’s constitutional dimension. The French monarchy had to raise money, bargain with estates, manage urban pressure in Paris, and preserve legitimacy while the king was absent.
* 1357–1358

  * **Parisian reform politics and the Jacquerie expose the social cost of royal failure**

    * **Background**: After Poitiers, the French state needed funds for defense and ransom. The **Estates-General**, Parisian elites, and reformers sought to limit royal officials and impose accountability. **Étienne Marcel**, provost of the merchants of Paris, became a central figure in urban pressure on the Dauphin’s government.
    * **Process**: Political reform demands mixed with factional conflict, fiscal desperation, and distrust of royal administration. In the countryside, the **Jacquerie** erupted as peasants reacted to war devastation, noble failure, taxation, and local violence. The revolt was brutally suppressed by noble forces.
    * **Result**: The Dauphin survived the crisis and later became **Charles V**, but the events revealed that military defeat could destabilize the entire social order. The crown learned that taxation, defense, and legitimacy had to be managed together.
    * **Common Pitfall**: The Jacquerie was not simply irrational peasant violence. It emerged from a world where rural communities bore the costs of raids, ransom, garrisoning, aristocratic warfare, and fiscal extraction while receiving little effective protection.
* 1360

  * **May, the Treaty of Brétigny ends the first phase of the war on terms favorable to England**

    * **Background**: After Poitiers, England attempted to convert military leverage into territorial settlement. The French crown needed the return of its king and relief from military pressure. The **Treaty of Brétigny** ended the first major phase of the war after the Black Prince’s victory and **John II’s** capture, but it did so by postponing several hard questions rather than resolving them permanently.
    * **Process**: The treaty granted Edward expanded territories in southwestern France and ransom arrangements for John II. In principle, it tried to separate the territorial question from the French crown claim: Edward would receive enlarged lands, while renunciations were supposed to settle competing claims. But implementation depended on complex transfers, oaths, payments, and legal formalities.
    * **Result**: England gained an enlarged **Aquitaine** and diplomatic prestige, while France gained temporary relief. Yet the settlement contained weaknesses. The promised renunciations and transfers were difficult to complete, ransom payments strained France, and many local communities and nobles had their own interests. Later disputes over unfulfilled clauses and Gascon appeals helped reopen conflict.
    * **Significance**: Brétigny did not solve the underlying contradiction between sovereignty and lordship. It created a temporary settlement that looked clear on parchment but remained unstable in practice. The war’s first phase ended, but the political structure that produced the war survived.

* 1364

  * **May 16, Cocherel helps Charles V restore royal authority after the crisis of defeat**

    * **Background**: After the **Treaty of Brétigny**, the French monarchy had to recover from royal captivity, fiscal disorder, aristocratic faction, and the political humiliation created by the first phase of the war. **Charles V** inherited not only a military problem but a governmental one: France needed order, revenue, and credible command before it could reverse English gains.
    * **Process**: **Charles the Bad of Navarre**, a dangerous rival with claims, alliances, and territorial ambitions inside the French political world, rebelled again. The French entrusted the operation against him to **Bertrand du Guesclin**, a Breton captain whose career would become central to the French recovery. At **Cocherel**, du Guesclin defeated Charles the Bad’s forces, reducing one of the internal threats that had weakened royal authority after the disasters of Crécy and Poitiers.
    * **Significance**: Cocherel mattered because it marked a shift from spectacular English victories toward French political consolidation. Charles V’s strategy was not built around seeking another Crécy-style set-piece battle. It depended on restoring administrative control, containing rebels, using experienced commanders, and preparing a slower reconquest.
  * **September 29, Auray ends the Breton succession conflict but complicates the wider war**

    * **Background**: **Brittany** had been one of the war’s persistent side-fronts because rival claimants were supported by opposing Anglo-French interests. The duchy was not simply a passive battlefield; its ports, nobles, and local political traditions made it strategically important to both crowns.
    * **Process**: The defeat and death of **Charles of Blois** at **Auray** ended the Breton War of Succession. By the later **Treaty of Guérande**, the Montfort line was recognized in Brittany. This ended one destabilizing conflict, but it did not make Brittany simply an obedient extension of either English or French power.
    * **Significance**: The settlement shows the limited reach of royal power in the fourteenth century. Even when dynastic disputes were settled, duchies, great lords, and regional political communities retained room for maneuver. The Hundred Years’ War therefore operated through layered sovereignties rather than through clean modern state borders.
* 1368

  * **June 30, Gascon appeals reopen the sovereignty problem inside Aquitaine**

    * **Background**: The settlement after Brétigny had enlarged English authority in southwestern France, but legal and political ambiguities remained. **Gascon nobles** could still appeal against their duke, and **Charles V** accepted such an appeal. This was not a minor procedural issue: it challenged whether the English-held duchy was genuinely independent of French royal jurisdiction.
    * **Process**: Charles V’s acceptance of the appeal allowed France to reopen the conflict through law before full-scale war resumed. **Edward III** treated this as a breach of the settlement, because if French courts could hear appeals from Aquitaine, English sovereignty there was incomplete. The renunciation clauses connected with the earlier settlement had not been fully carried out, leaving space for each side to claim that the other had broken faith.
    * **Significance**: The episode shows why the war could not be solved merely by granting territories. The key problem was whether territory meant *sovereign possession* or *feudal tenure under superior royal jurisdiction*. The difference was abstract in legal language but concrete in taxation, justice, allegiance, and military control.
* 1369

  * **Spring–Summer, hostilities resume as England tries to defend the Brétigny settlement**

    * **Background**: Once the legal conflict over Aquitaine escalated, English strategy returned to raiding and pressure. England still possessed important bases and commanders, but France under Charles V had learned from earlier defeats and avoided unnecessary direct battle when the conditions favored English defensive tactics.
    * **Process**: **John of Gaunt**, duke of Lancaster and son of Edward III, landed at **Calais** and led a *chevauchée* into French territory. Such raids were designed to damage revenue, terrorize communities, undermine royal protection, and force the French into battle. Yet the French response increasingly combined defense, attrition, diplomacy, and the gradual detachment of local lords from English allegiance.
    * **Significance**: This phase reverses the earlier pattern. In the first phase, English raids often led to decisive victories or favorable treaties. Under Charles V, the French crown treated English raids as destructive but survivable, refusing to let the enemy dictate the strategic rhythm.
* 1370

  * **December, Pontvallain demonstrates du Guesclin’s strategy of selective engagement**

    * **Background**: English captains such as **Sir Robert Knollys** advanced toward the **Île-de-France**, continuing the older method of destructive cavalry raiding. The danger for France was not only military damage but reputational collapse: if the king could not defend the core lands near Paris, obedience and taxation would suffer.
    * **Process**: **Bertrand du Guesclin**, now central to French military recovery, avoided the large, aristocratic, frontal battle that had favored English tactics earlier in the war. Instead, he struck part of Knollys’s force at **Pontvallain**, destroying a portion of the English army rather than seeking one grand decisive encounter.
    * **Result**: The victory did not end English military power, but it damaged the prestige of English raiding and strengthened confidence in Charles V’s methodical approach.
    * **Significance**: Pontvallain reveals a major military lesson of the war: tactical adaptation mattered more than numerical strength alone. The French recovery depended on refusing the enemy’s preferred battle form, using smaller operations, and converting war from heroic confrontation into administrative and strategic patience.
* 1372

  * **June, La Rochelle breaks English naval security in the southwest**

    * **Background**: **Aquitaine** could not be held securely without maritime connection to England. Ports, shipping, and naval escorts were therefore as important as castles and cavalry. France’s alliance with **Castile** gave Charles V access to naval strength that could contest English movement in the Atlantic.
    * **Process**: A **Castilian** fleet allied with France defeated an English fleet off **La Rochelle**. This was a major operational blow because it disrupted reinforcement, supply, and communications between England and its southwestern possessions. The battle strengthened Charles V’s recovery by making English Aquitaine harder to defend from across the sea.
    * **Result**: After La Rochelle, English control in the southwest became harder to maintain. The French could combine local pressure, diplomacy, bribery, and military operations against territories that had been ceded under the earlier settlement.
    * **Significance**: La Rochelle shows the international character of the war. The conflict was Anglo-French at its core, but its outcome depended on **Castile**, **Flanders**, **Scotland**, **Burgundy**, the papal schism, and maritime logistics.
* 1373

  * **Summer–Winter, John of Gaunt’s great chevauchée exposes the limits of destructive raiding**

    * **Background**: English leaders still used the *chevauchée* as a way to recover initiative. **John of Gaunt** attempted an ambitious march from **Calais** toward **Bordeaux**, crossing large parts of France. The aim was to demonstrate English reach, weaken French authority, and sustain the war through plunder and pressure.
    * **Process**: The campaign was physically punishing and strategically costly. French forces largely avoided the decisive battle that English commanders wanted. Instead, the French allowed distance, supply problems, weather, and attrition to erode the English army. Meanwhile, Charles V’s agents and commanders pursued a “patient reconquest” through warfare, diplomacy, and payments to local lords.
    * **Result**: The raid inflicted damage but failed to restore English strategic dominance. It showed that mobility without secure political conversion could become exhaustion rather than conquest.
    * **Common Pitfall**: A *chevauchée* was not just random pillage, but it was also not the same as occupation. Its success depended on whether destruction forced political results. In this phase, French refusal to fight on English terms reduced its strategic value.
* 1375

  * **June, the Truce of Bruges freezes the war after French recovery**

    * **Background**: By the mid-1370s, France had recovered much of what had been ceded after Brétigny, while England faced financial strain and political fatigue. Neither side had achieved a final settlement, but both needed breathing space.
    * **Process**: A truce was arranged at **Bruges**, lasting into the later 1370s. It did not settle the central disputes over sovereignty, Aquitaine, or the French crown, but it recognized the temporary exhaustion of active campaigning. The truce followed major French recovery and gave both crowns time to manage strained finances, political pressure, and the difficulty of sustaining war across several fronts.
    * **Significance**: The truce marked the end of the most successful phase of Charles V’s counteroffensive. The war did not end because the causes disappeared; it paused because men, money, and political conditions made sustained campaigning difficult.
* 1376–1377

  * **June–June, the deaths of the Black Prince and Edward III produce a succession transition in England**

    * **Background**: The English war effort had depended heavily on the prestige of **Edward III** and the military reputation of **Edward of Woodstock**, the **Black Prince**. Their generation had created the victories of Crécy, Poitiers, and the Brétigny settlement. By the late 1370s, that political-military generation was disappearing.
    * **Process**: The Black Prince died first, on **June 8, 1376**, leaving his young son as heir. Edward III died on **June 21, 1377**, and the English crown passed to **Richard II**, still a child. The succession was legally clear, but political management became harder because the heroic generation of Edwardian warfare had disappeared.
    * **Result**: England entered a period of minority government, factional management, fiscal strain, and reduced continental coherence. The war continued, but with less strategic clarity than under Edward III.
    * **Significance**: The deaths mattered because medieval war was strongly tied to dynastic reputation and personal kingship. A child king did not simply inherit policy; he inherited debts, expectations, factions, military contracts, and propaganda produced by earlier reigns.
* 1380

  * **July–September, the deaths of du Guesclin and Charles V halt the momentum of French recovery**

    * **Background**: **Charles V** and **Bertrand du Guesclin** had formed the political and military partnership most responsible for reversing English gains after Brétigny. Their success was not based on one overwhelming victory, but on disciplined avoidance of English strengths, administrative repair, local negotiation, and steady reconquest.
    * **Process**: Du Guesclin died while campaigning, and Charles V died soon afterward. Their deaths removed the king and commander most associated with the French recovery. Even then, the English had not been completely driven out of France, so the work of reconquest passed to a younger and politically less stable regime.
    * **Result**: France retained many recovered territories, but the crown passed to **Charles VI**, who was young. The continuation of recovery now depended on regents, royal uncles, and court factions rather than on the coherent direction of Charles V.
    * **Significance**: This transition shows the fragility of medieval state-building. A successful strategy could be institutionalized only partially; dynastic minority and elite competition could quickly weaken royal capacity.
* 1381

  * **May–June, the Peasants’ Revolt reveals the domestic cost of war finance in England**

    * **Background**: The English state had financed long war through taxation, customs, credit, and political bargaining. After the **Black Death**, labor shortages and wage regulation through the **Statute of Labourers** intensified social tension. The war’s fiscal burden interacted with class conflict, local grievances, and resentment of government officials.
    * **Process**: A **poll tax** imposed to fund the ongoing war provoked anger among workers and artisans. The revolt began in southeastern England and **East Anglia**, with **Wat Tyler** leading rebels into London. Rebels captured the **Tower of London** and killed officials associated with the hated tax. Richard II met the rebels at **Smithfield**, where Tyler was wounded and then executed; the revolt was suppressed soon afterward, and promised concessions were withdrawn.
    * **Significance**: The revolt demonstrates that the Hundred Years’ War was not only fought in France. Its fiscal machinery reached English villages, towns, labor markets, and households. War taxation helped turn foreign policy into domestic crisis.
    * **Common Pitfall**: The revolt should not be treated as a side anecdote unrelated to the war. It was connected to the costs of prolonged military commitment, even though its causes also included labor regulation, social hierarchy, local abuses, and post-plague economic change.
* 1383–1384

  * **Spring–January, the Flanders question and the failed Norwich expedition show the war’s commercial geography**

    * **Background**: **Flanders** remained central because of its textile economy, political autonomy, and connection to English wool. Control or influence over Flemish towns mattered to both crowns. The **Great Schism** also allowed political-military action to be framed in religious language.
    * **Process**: **Henry Despenser**, bishop of **Norwich**, led an expedition nominally connected to the conflict over the papacy, but it failed around **Dunkirk** and **Ypres**. Soon afterward, the death of **Louis of Mâle**, count of Flanders, brought Flanders into the hands of **Philip the Bold**, duke of **Burgundy** and uncle of the French king.
    * **Significance**: This development increased Burgundian importance in the wider conflict. Burgundy was not yet the later near-independent power of the fifteenth century, but its control of Flanders connected aristocratic politics, urban wealth, diplomacy, and Anglo-French rivalry.
* 1388–1389

  * **November–June, peace factions negotiate the Truce of Leulinghen**

    * **Background**: By the late 1380s, both kingdoms were politically strained. In England, **Richard II** faced magnate pressure from the **Lords Appellant**. In France, the royal council came under the influence of the **Marmousets**, advisers associated with the administrative traditions of Charles V. Groups favoring peace gained influence on both sides.
    * **Process**: Negotiations began at **Leulinghen** in **Picardy**, and a three-year truce was signed. The English were to evacuate some places, and Richard II was to do homage for **Aquitaine**, effectively reopening the older feudal formula that Edward III’s claims had tried to escape. Yet disputes over Aquitaine’s boundaries prevented a final peace.
    * **Significance**: The truce reveals the deep difficulty of ending the war. Diplomats could suspend violence, arrange homage, and discuss evacuation, but the underlying contradiction between French overlordship and English possession still resisted final settlement.
* 1396

  * **March–October, Richard II’s marriage diplomacy extends the truce but fails to end the war**

    * **Background**: **Richard II** personally favored a more pacific policy than many English nobles and knights, who had inherited both the profits and the ideology of Edward III’s war. France, under **Charles VI**, also had reasons to seek stability, though royal illness and factional politics weakened coherent policy.
    * **Process**: Richard II arranged a marriage by proxy with **Isabella of Valois**, daughter of Charles VI. The truce of Leulinghen was extended for a long period, and the two kings met between **Calais** and **Ardres** amid diplomatic ceremony, feasting, gift exchange, and cautious bargaining. The display suggested reconciliation, but no final peace treaty resulted because the older problems of Aquitaine, homage, and English claims remained unresolved.
    * **Result**: The war entered a diplomatic lull. Yet the truce depended heavily on political conditions inside England and France, not on permanent resolution of territorial and dynastic claims.
    * **Significance**: The episode shows that peace was possible but structurally fragile. Nobles accustomed to war profit, English anti-French propaganda, French factionalism, and the unresolved Aquitaine problem all limited Richard’s policy.
* 1399–1400

  * **June–February, Henry Bolingbroke’s seizure of the English crown reverses Richard II’s peace policy**

    * **Background**: Richard II’s authority had been contested by English magnates, and his peace policy was unpopular among many who valued continental war for prestige, profit, and ideological reasons. When **Henry Bolingbroke** returned from exile to claim his inheritance, broader discontent gathered around him.
    * **Process**: Richard II was deposed and imprisoned, and Bolingbroke became **Henry IV**. Although Henry needed first to secure his own position in England, he adopted the French royal claim associated with Edward III rather than continuing Richard’s pacific direction. A king who had taken the throne from another king had strong reasons to speak the language of inherited rights, martial honor, and dynastic restoration.
    * **Significance**: The Lancastrian regime inherited a legitimacy problem. Reviving the French claim could serve domestic politics by connecting the new dynasty to Edward III’s martial prestige. Yet Henry IV’s rebellions at home limited his ability to wage large-scale war abroad.
* 1405–1406

  * **French pressure on Aquitaine resumes but is checked by internal rivalry**

    * **Background**: France had opportunities to exploit Henry IV’s domestic troubles, including Welsh rebellion under **Owain Glyndŵr** and political instability in England. **Louis, duke of Orléans**, pushed a more aggressive anti-English policy and supported pressure across the Channel and in the southwest.
    * **Process**: French forces took towns in **Saintonge** and **Périgord**, while **Bernard VII, count of Armagnac**, threatened **Bordeaux**. But French progress was limited by setbacks, including failure at **Blaye** and a Burgundian failure before **Calais**. More importantly, rivalry between **Louis of Orléans** and **John the Fearless of Burgundy** divided French strategy.
    * **Significance**: This phase shows that the French crown’s greatest weakness was increasingly internal. England was not necessarily strong, but French factional division prevented a coordinated final expulsion of English power.
* 1407

  * **November, the assassination of Louis of Orléans turns faction into civil war**

    * **Background**: The illness of **Charles VI** created a power vacuum at the center of the French monarchy. Royal relatives competed to control the king, the treasury, Paris, patronage, and war policy. **Louis of Orléans** and **John the Fearless** represented rival aristocratic blocs with different strategies toward England and domestic power.
    * **Process**: Louis of Orléans was assassinated at the instigation of John the Fearless. This murder transformed court rivalry into open political rupture. The anti-Burgundian faction later became known as the **Armagnacs**, after **Bernard VII of Armagnac**, who emerged as a leader in the movement to avenge Louis.
    * **Result**: France was divided between **Burgundians** and **Armagnacs**. Both sides would eventually seek English support when useful, creating the conditions for **Henry V** to intervene with devastating effect.
    * **Significance**: This is one of the decisive turning points of the war. English revival under Henry V did not occur only because of English strength; it succeeded because France was politically fractured.
* 1411–1413

  * **Burgundian and Armagnac appeals to England prepare the ground for renewed invasion**

    * **Background**: Civil war made French politics porous. Both factions treated English military power as a tool that could be invited, bargained with, or redirected against internal enemies. This created an opening for England that would have been far harder to exploit under the more unified monarchy of Charles V.
    * **Process**: Henry IV gave limited help to **John the Fearless** by sending a force through **Calais**. The **Armagnacs** then sought Henry’s help, offering restoration of **Aquitaine**. Henry IV died before this could become a full-scale program, and the Parisian Burgundians issued the **Ordonnance Cabochienne**, a reform movement soon annulled by opponents.
    * **Significance**: These negotiations show how civil war converted the English claim from a dormant dynastic ideology into an actionable diplomatic weapon. The next English king would inherit not just a claim, but a divided France into which that claim could be inserted.

* 1413

  * **March 21, Henry V inherits a contested crown and turns foreign war into dynastic consolidation**

    * **Background**: **Henry V** inherited the **Lancastrian** kingship from **Henry IV**, whose seizure of power had left the dynasty vulnerable to accusations of usurpation.
    * **Process**: Henry revived the English claim to the **French crown** not only as a foreign policy but as a domestic legitimacy strategy. A successful war in France could connect the Lancastrian regime to the martial prestige of **Edward III**, redirect aristocratic violence outward, and present the king as the restorer of royal honor. France, meanwhile, was weakened by the mental illness of **Charles VI** and by the rivalry between **Armagnacs** and **Burgundians**, making intervention unusually attractive. Although earlier truces had been designed to last for years, English domestic instability and French political crisis made renewed war possible.
    * **Significance**: The Lancastrian phase of the war was not simply a continuation of Edward III’s earlier claim. Henry V tried to make that claim politically real by conquering territory, forcing dynastic settlement, and imagining a **dual monarchy** in which one ruler would govern both England and France.
* 1414

  * **Negotiations with France fail as Henry converts diplomacy into preparation for invasion**

    * **Background**: Before invading, Henry pursued negotiations that framed his claims in legal and dynastic terms. English demands included restoration of old continental rights, marriage proposals, financial compensation, and recognition of status. French negotiators could not satisfy demands that would have conceded too much sovereignty, especially while the kingdom was internally divided.
    * **Process**: Diplomacy served several purposes. It tested French weakness, built a record of English grievance, and helped Henry present war as lawful recovery rather than naked aggression. In medieval political culture, kingship required *justice* as well as force; therefore, manifestos, embassies, genealogical claims, and treaty language mattered as instruments of mobilization.
    * **Significance**: The failed negotiations show that war was prepared through law and rhetoric before it was fought through siege and battle. Henry’s invasion was not improvised: it was preceded by fiscal preparation, recruitment, naval organization, and political staging.
* 1415

  * **August 11–22, the Southampton Plot exposes the domestic risks behind Henry’s expedition**

    * **Background**: As Henry prepared to cross into France, a conspiracy formed around **Richard, earl of Cambridge**, **Henry, Lord Scrope**, and **Sir Thomas Grey**. The plot aimed to replace or destabilize Henry by invoking rival dynastic claims connected to the **Mortimer** line. This mattered because the Lancastrian dynasty still carried the political memory of Henry IV’s usurpation.
    * **Process**: The plot was discovered shortly before embarkation. Henry moved quickly, placing the accused on trial and executing the central conspirators. The timing was critical: a foreign expedition could not begin with visible division among the political elite.
    * **Result**: The suppression of the conspiracy strengthened Henry’s immediate control and allowed the invasion to proceed. It also showed that the campaign in France was inseparable from the politics of English legitimacy.
    * **Significance**: The episode reveals a central paradox of the Lancastrian war: Henry used foreign conquest to stabilize domestic authority, but the need for such stabilization shows that domestic authority was not secure.
  * **August–September, the siege of Harfleur opens the Lancastrian campaign through logistics and attrition**

    * **Background**: Henry landed in **Normandy** and targeted **Harfleur**, a port near the mouth of the **Seine**. Taking a port was strategically rational: it could become an entry point for further campaigns, a supply base, and a symbol of restored English power in northern France.
    * **Process**: The siege was costly. Harfleur resisted long enough to exhaust English time, manpower, and health. Disease, probably including dysentery, weakened the army severely. Although the town surrendered, the campaign had already become more dangerous than Henry intended. The English army was reduced and had to march through hostile territory toward **Calais** rather than continue easy conquest.
    * **Result**: Harfleur gave Henry a foothold, but the price was high. The victory forced a strategic dilemma: remain in Normandy with a weakened army, or march to Calais and risk interception.
    * **Significance**: Harfleur reminds that medieval war was dominated by sieges, supplies, disease, and garrisons more than by spectacular battles alone. The later fame of **Agincourt** can obscure the logistical strain that made the battle so dangerous for the English.
  * **October 25, Agincourt turns a dangerous retreat into a symbolic and strategic victory**

    * **Background**: Henry’s depleted army marched toward **Calais** while French forces gathered to block it. The English had already lost men to the siege of Harfleur and disease, and the march became a test of endurance as French forces tried to trap them before they reached safety. The French had numerical superiority, though exact figures remain disputed, and they expected to turn Henry’s retreat into a royal disaster.
    * **Process**: Henry positioned his army on a narrow, muddy field bounded by woods. English men-at-arms fought dismounted in the center, while archers deployed on the flanks with stakes to resist cavalry. The French advanced into restricted ground where mud, compression, armor, command disorder, and crowding reduced their advantage. Their nobles did not move as one clean machine: competing commands, eagerness for honor, and the pressure of men behind them turned the advance into congestion. Longbow fire was important, but the decisive effect came from the combined system: defensive positioning, disciplined infantry, missile pressure, terrain, and the inability of French numbers to deploy effectively. During the later confusion, Henry also ordered some prisoners killed when he believed a renewed threat was forming, showing how quickly chivalric ransom norms could give way to battlefield fear.
    * **Result**: The French suffered devastating aristocratic losses. The defeat removed many nobles, shocked French political society, and gave Henry enormous prestige. The English had survived a campaign that could have ended in disaster and transformed it into a claim of divine favor, royal competence, and military legitimacy.
    * **Common Pitfall**: Agincourt should not be explained as “longbows defeated knights” in isolation. The longbow mattered, but so did mud, constricted terrain, exhausted movement, French tactical disorder, English defensive preparation, and the broader failure of French political coordination.
    * **Additional Note**: The battle was dangerous even for monarchs. **Henry V** fought close enough to the crisis of the field that his personal survival mattered politically: the death or capture of the king would have turned a risky campaign into a dynastic emergency.
* 1416

  * **Summer–Autumn, diplomacy and naval pressure prevent Agincourt from remaining an isolated victory**

    * **Background**: A single battlefield victory did not automatically produce conquest. Henry needed money, allies, naval protection, and further campaigns. France still had population, territory, fortresses, and rival factions whose cooperation or neutrality had to be managed.
    * **Process**: Henry worked to strengthen continental alliances and secure the Channel. The war entered a phase in which the English crown had to convert the prestige of Agincourt into durable operational capacity. This required parliamentary taxation, contracts with captains, transport arrangements, garrison planning, and diplomatic outreach to **Burgundy** and other powers.
    * **Significance**: The year after Agincourt matters because it shows that victory required institutional follow-through. Henry’s strength lay not merely in battlefield command but in his ability to organize war as a sustained royal project.
* 1417

  * **August, Henry returns to Normandy and begins systematic conquest rather than raiding**

    * **Background**: Earlier English strategy often relied on *chevauchées*, destructive raids intended to force battle or political concessions. Henry’s later campaign differed: it aimed at territorial occupation, town-by-town submission, and administrative control.
    * **Process**: Henry landed again in Normandy and began taking fortified places. The campaign was slower than a raid but strategically deeper. Each captured town required garrisoning, negotiation, taxation, and legal incorporation. English power in France therefore became an administrative project, not only a military presence.
    * **Significance**: This transition is crucial. The Lancastrian war aimed to make conquest permanent. Henry was no longer only proving a claim; he was building the material infrastructure of rule in northern France.
* 1418–1419

  * **July 31–January 19, the siege of Rouen breaks Normandy’s greatest urban resistance**

    * **Background**: **Rouen** was the leading city of Normandy, economically important and symbolically central. Without Rouen, English control of Normandy would remain incomplete. With Rouen, Henry could claim not only scattered military success but the conquest of the duchy’s principal urban and administrative center.
    * **Process**: Henry besieged the city with systematic pressure from **July 1418** to **January 1419**. Rouen’s defenders tried to hold out behind walls while hunger spread inside the city. Noncombatants became part of the tragedy: some were expelled or trapped between the city and the English lines, where neither side wanted to feed them. Negotiation followed exhaustion. When Rouen surrendered, it did not simply lose a siege; it passed into an English administrative order that demanded oaths, obedience, and cooperation.
    * **Result**: Rouen’s fall gave Henry the core of Normandy. It also allowed English administrators to impose oaths, manage confiscated property, and reorganize local government under Lancastrian authority.
    * **Significance**: Rouen shows the grim mechanics of conquest. Medieval sovereignty was not won only through noble battle; it was imposed through siege starvation, urban submission, garrison placement, oath-taking, fiscal management, and control of courts.
* 1419

  * **September 10, the murder of John the Fearless makes Anglo-Burgundian alignment possible**

    * **Background**: France remained divided between the **Armagnac** faction supporting the **Dauphin Charles** and the **Burgundian** faction led by **John the Fearless**. Burgundy’s position was decisive because it controlled major territories, urban networks, and political influence in northern France.
    * **Process**: John the Fearless was murdered during a meeting with the Dauphin’s party on the bridge at **Montereau**. The meeting was supposed to explore reconciliation, but it took place under heavy suspicion after years of factional murder and counter-murder. In the confrontation, men from the Dauphin’s side killed John. Whether responsibility is assigned personally to the Dauphin or to his entourage, the political effect was catastrophic. The killing made reconciliation between the Dauphin and Burgundy far more difficult and pushed **Philip the Good**, John’s son, toward alliance with Henry V.
    * **Result**: The murder converted French civil conflict into an international turning point. Henry could now present himself not merely as an invader but as an ally of Burgundian revenge and order against Armagnac violence.
    * **Significance**: This event may be as important as Agincourt for explaining Henry’s success. English conquest became viable because French factional division made part of France willing to cooperate with the invader against domestic enemies.
* 1420

  * **May 21, the Treaty of Troyes attempts to replace Valois succession with Lancastrian dual monarchy**

    * **Background**: After Agincourt, the conquest of Normandy, and the Burgundian break with the Dauphin, Henry was strong enough to impose a dynastic settlement. **Charles VI** remained king of France, but his mental illness and factional dependence weakened the authority of his court. **Queen Isabeau**, **Philip the Good**, and Burgundian interests helped produce a settlement excluding the Dauphin.
    * **Process**: The **Treaty of Troyes** recognized **Henry V** as heir to the French throne and arranged his marriage to **Catherine of Valois**, daughter of Charles VI. The Dauphin was effectively disinherited and treated as a rebel rather than the natural successor. The agreement turned a military occupation into a public legal drama: Charles VI’s court, Queen Isabeau, Burgundian power, and Henry’s army all helped stage the claim that the future of France now passed through the English king.
    * **Result**: The treaty gave Henry’s project its highest legal form. It transformed conquest into a claimed constitutional settlement and created the possibility of an Anglo-French monarchy.
    * **Significance**: Troyes was a triumph on paper but fragile in reality. It depended on Burgundian cooperation, English military enforcement, and acceptance by French communities that did not all recognize the disinheritance of the Dauphin. Henry’s project aimed to establish a dual monarchy, but written settlement could not make English authority effective in a hostile and divided France by itself.
    * **Common Pitfall**: The treaty did not make Henry peacefully “king of France.” Charles VI remained alive, the Dauphin continued resistance, and large parts of France did not accept the settlement. Troyes created a legal claim that still had to be enforced by war.
* 1421

  * **March 22, Baugé reveals the vulnerability of the Lancastrian conquest**

    * **Background**: English power in France depended on maintaining commanders, garrisons, finances, and alliances. Henry V could not be everywhere, and his brothers and captains had to hold the military structure together.
    * **Process**: At **Baugé**, a Franco-Scottish force defeated an English army and killed **Thomas, duke of Clarence**, Henry’s brother. The battle did not reverse the whole Lancastrian position, but it shocked English confidence and demonstrated that the anti-English coalition still had military capacity.
    * **Result**: Clarence’s death removed a major royal commander. Henry returned to France to restore the campaign’s momentum.
    * **Significance**: Baugé shows that Lancastrian success was not inevitable after Troyes. The English position remained dependent on manpower, leadership, and fragile political alliances.
* 1422

  * **August 31–October 21, the deaths of Henry V and Charles VI create a child king and a divided France**

    * **Background**: Henry V died before he could inherit the French crown from Charles VI. Charles VI died soon afterward. This sequence transformed the Treaty of Troyes from Henry’s personal project into a regency problem centered on an infant: **Henry VI**.
    * **Process**: Under the terms claimed by the English and Burgundian side, the infant Henry VI became king of England and, in their view, king of France. The Dauphin, however, claimed the French throne as **Charles VII**. France therefore had two competing royal claims: the Lancastrian claim enforced in the north and supported by Burgundy, and the Valois claim maintained from the south and center.
    * **Result**: The war entered a new phase. English policy was directed by regents and commanders, especially **John, duke of Bedford**, while Charles VII struggled with limited resources, damaged legitimacy, and factional suspicion.
    * **Significance**: The deaths exposed the difference between conquest by a charismatic adult king and rule by an infant monarchy. Henry V had fused military command and political legitimacy in his own person; Henry VI inherited claims without personal authority.
* 1423

  * **July 31, Cravant strengthens Anglo-Burgundian control of the northern war zone**

    * **Background**: After Henry V’s death, the English needed to prove that Lancastrian power had not died with him. The **Burgundian alliance** remained central, and the war in the **Loire** and **Yonne** regions became important for controlling movement between northern and central France.
    * **Process**: Anglo-Burgundian forces defeated Franco-Scottish troops at **Cravant**. The presence of Scots mattered because the **Auld Alliance** continued to provide France with external military support against England.
    * **Result**: The victory strengthened the English-Burgundian position and damaged Charles VII’s coalition. It also reinforced Bedford’s reputation as an effective regent and commander.
    * **Significance**: Cravant shows that the post-Henry V English regime was still militarily dangerous. The French recovery was not yet visible; the early 1420s remained a period of Valois weakness.
* 1424

  * **August 17, Verneuil becomes the last great English battlefield triumph of the old pattern**

    * **Background**: **Charles VII** relied on mixed forces, including French, Scottish, and other contingents, while Bedford sought to preserve English military dominance. The encounter at **Verneuil** resembled earlier large battles in which English discipline, archery, and defensive coordination were decisive.
    * **Process**: Bedford’s army defeated a Franco-Scottish force in a hard-fought battle. Scottish losses were severe, and the defeat weakened one of Charles VII’s most important sources of military support.
    * **Result**: The battle confirmed English strength in the short term. Charles VII’s position looked precarious, and the Lancastrian regime retained control over much of northern France.
    * **Significance**: Verneuil was a major English victory, but it did not produce final conquest. The English could win battles and still struggle to impose durable rule across a resistant, fragmented, and geographically large kingdom.
* 1428–1429

  * **October 12–May 8, the siege of Orléans becomes the military and psychological turning point**

    * **Background**: **Orléans** guarded access to the **Loire** and stood as a crucial barrier between the English-held north and the Valois heartland. If the city fell, Charles VII’s position could become strategically desperate. The siege ran from **October 1428** to **May 1429** and became the military and psychological turning point of the war.
    * **Process**: English forces attempted to isolate the city through surrounding strongpoints rather than by a complete encirclement from the start. The defenders held under pressure while Charles VII’s court remained uncertain and politically fragile. After being examined by Charles’s advisers, **Joan of Arc** entered the campaign as a figure of religious confidence and political urgency. Her arrival with a relief convoy changed morale inside the city, and French forces began attacking the English bastides rather than merely enduring them. The fighting around **Les Tourelles** became especially important: when that position fell, the English siege system around Orléans broke. Joan did not single-handedly command the entire war machine, but her presence helped transform a defensive struggle into an offensive effort to break the siege.
    * **Result**: French forces lifted the siege. The English lost the initiative around the Loire, and the psychological balance of the war shifted. The event did not immediately expel the English from France, but it made Valois recovery plausible.
    * **Significance**: Orléans matters because it joined strategy, morale, legitimacy, and sacred kingship. The war had become not only a contest of armies but a contest over who represented lawful France. Joan’s role gave Charles VII’s cause a new symbolic force at precisely the moment when military survival was at stake.
    * **Common Pitfall**: Joan of Arc should not be treated either as a magical replacement for military causality or as a minor mascot. Her significance lay in the interaction between charisma, religious belief, morale, royal legitimacy, and concrete military action.
* 1429

  * **June 18, Patay reverses the tactical memory of Crécy, Poitiers, and Agincourt**

    * **Background**: After Orléans, French forces moved quickly against English positions in the Loire campaign. The English defensive method depended on preparation, archers, stakes, terrain selection, and disciplined waiting. If caught before proper deployment, the system could fail.
    * **Process**: At **Patay**, French cavalry attacked before the English archers could fully prepare their defensive position. The result was a rapid and damaging defeat for the English. The battle did not mirror Agincourt; it inverted it. The French, acting with speed, denied the English the conditions under which the longbow-centered defensive system worked best.
    * **Result**: English prestige declined sharply in the Loire region. French confidence rose, and Charles VII’s party gained momentum for a politically decisive march toward coronation.
    * **Significance**: Patay shows that tactics are conditional, not absolute. The English battlefield system was powerful when terrain, deployment, and command circumstances favored it; it was vulnerable when surprised or forced into movement before preparation.
  * **July 17, the coronation at Reims restores the sacred legitimacy of Charles VII**

    * **Background**: Medieval French kingship was not only hereditary. It was sacralized through coronation at **Reims**, where French kings were traditionally anointed. For **Charles VII**, reaching Reims after the victories around the Loire was politically essential because the **Treaty of Troyes** had tried to exclude him from the succession.
    * **Process**: Joan urged the campaign toward Reims, and several towns submitted or opened the way. The coronation transformed Charles from a contested Dauphin into an anointed king in the eyes of his supporters. The event did not end the war, but it damaged the symbolic foundation of the Lancastrian claim.
    * **Result**: Charles VII gained a legitimacy that military victories alone could not provide. The English still held Paris and much of northern France, but their claim now faced an anointed Valois king with renewed political authority.
    * **Significance**: The coronation was one of the decisive legitimacy events of the war. It shows why the conflict cannot be understood only through territory. Sacred kingship, ritual, public recognition, and dynastic law shaped the political meaning of military success.

* 1430

  * **May 23, Joan of Arc is captured before Compiègne**

    * **Background**: After the relief of **Orléans** and the coronation of **Charles VII** at **Reims**, the French cause had gained momentum, but the war remained unresolved. **Paris** and much of northern France were still under **English** or **Burgundian** influence. Joan’s authority depended on continued military usefulness, royal support, and the belief that her mission served legitimate kingship.
    * **Process**: During operations near **Compiègne**, Joan was captured by Burgundian forces. She was not immediately taken by the English in battle; she entered Burgundian custody first and was then transferred to the English, who had strong political reasons to destroy her credibility. The English and their French clerical allies understood that Joan was not only a battlefield figure but a symbol of Valois legitimacy.
    * **Result**: Her capture removed the most visible charismatic figure in Charles VII’s recovery. Yet it did not undo the strategic effects of Orléans, Patay, and Reims. The French cause no longer depended entirely on her presence, because the legitimacy she helped revive had already been ritually confirmed through the coronation.
    * **Significance**: Joan’s capture shows the political nature of late-medieval warfare. A prisoner could be valuable not merely for ransom but for propaganda, legitimacy, and judicial destruction. The campaign against Joan after her capture aimed to discredit the sacred and political meaning she had given to Charles VII’s kingship.
* 1431

  * **January–May, the Rouen trial turns Joan of Arc into a judicial weapon against Charles VII**

    * **Background**: Joan was tried at **Rouen**, an English-controlled city, before an ecclesiastical court led by **Pierre Cauchon**, bishop of Beauvais. The trial’s legal form mattered because the English could not simply execute her as an enemy captain without losing the opportunity to attack the legitimacy of her mission. If she could be condemned as heretical or fraudulent, then Charles VII’s coronation would appear contaminated by false sanctity.
    * **Process**: The court questioned Joan on her voices, clothing, obedience to the Church, and claims of divine mission. The procedure used learned theological and legal categories, but the political context was unmistakable. Joan was a prisoner of the English, tried in English-held territory, by judges with strong incentives to serve the Anglo-Burgundian cause. Under pressure, she made an abjuration and was sentenced to imprisonment, but the case was reopened as relapse when she resumed or was found again in male clothing and rejected the abjuration’s meaning.
    * **Result**: Joan was condemned and burned at the stake on **May 30**. Her death removed her physically but did not erase her symbolic force. The trial record preserves unusually extensive interrogation material, but it must be read critically because it was produced under hostile political conditions. Later rehabilitation proceedings under Charles VII would reverse the condemnation and reshape her memory.
    * **Common Pitfall**: Joan should not be treated as a modern nationalist in a simple sense. Her world was structured by sacred kingship, prophecy, obedience, local identity, dynastic legitimacy, and war against occupying enemies. Her importance lies in how these categories converged around the Valois restoration.
  * **December 16, Henry VI is crowned in Paris but cannot replace the legitimacy of Reims**

    * **Background**: After the **Treaty of Troyes**, the Lancastrian regime claimed that **Henry VI** was the rightful king of both England and France. Because he was still a child, his French kingship depended on regents, garrisons, Burgundian cooperation, and ceremonies designed to make the claim visible.
    * **Process**: Henry VI was crowned at **Notre-Dame de Paris**. The ceremony asserted Lancastrian kingship in the French capital, but it lacked the traditional French sacral setting of **Reims**, where Charles VII had already been anointed. Paris was politically important, but Reims carried dynastic and religious weight in French royal tradition.
    * **Result**: The coronation did not reverse the symbolic effect of Charles VII’s anointing. It displayed English power in the north, but it could not create the broad consent needed for a durable dual monarchy.
    * **Significance**: The contrast between **Paris** and **Reims** shows that medieval legitimacy was not interchangeable ceremony. Place, rite, precedent, and recognition mattered. The English could stage a royal coronation, but they could not easily make it mean what Valois coronation at Reims meant.
* 1435

  * **September 21, the Treaty of Arras breaks the Anglo-Burgundian alliance**

    * **Background**: The **Burgundian alliance** had been the central pillar of English success after the murder of **John the Fearless**. **Philip the Good** had cooperated with the English because hostility to the Armagnac-Valois party served Burgundian interests. But by the 1430s, English power looked less decisive, and Burgundy had reasons to seek reconciliation with Charles VII.
    * **Process**: At **Arras**, diplomats tried to end the quarrel that had begun with the murder of **John the Fearless**. The negotiations were not a sentimental reconciliation. **Philip the Good** wanted security, honor, and recognition of Burgundian interests; **Charles VII** needed to detach Burgundy from England even if that required concessions. The French king made concessions, including recognition of Burgundian dignity and practical autonomy in important respects. Burgundy did not become a simple servant of the French crown, but it withdrew from the alliance that had made English occupation politically viable in northern France.
    * **Result**: The treaty isolated the English. Without Burgundian cooperation, English garrisons, administrators, and tax collectors faced a more hostile political landscape. French recovery became not merely a military possibility but a diplomatic reality.
    * **Significance**: Arras was one of the decisive diplomatic turning points of the war. The Lancastrian regime had depended on French civil division; when that division narrowed, English power in France began to lose its structural foundation.
  * **September, the death of Bedford removes the strongest defender of the Lancastrian settlement**

    * **Background**: **John, duke of Bedford**, had been the most capable English regent in France. He preserved the military structure after Henry V’s death, managed the Burgundian alliance, and maintained the Lancastrian claim during Henry VI’s minority.
    * **Process**: Bedford died in the same year as the Burgundian reconciliation with Charles VII. The timing was disastrous for the English. The regime lost both a crucial ally and its most effective political-military manager.
    * **Result**: English government in France became increasingly reactive. Later commanders could still fight effectively, but the strategic coherence of Henry V’s project weakened.
    * **Significance**: Bedford’s death shows the importance of political administration in a war often remembered for battles. The English occupation required negotiation, finance, garrisons, urban cooperation, and alliance management. Without Bedford, that system became harder to sustain.
* 1436

  * **April, Paris returns to Charles VII and the Lancastrian capital strategy collapses**

    * **Background**: **Paris** had been central to the English and Burgundian position. It was not simply a large city; it was an administrative, symbolic, and political center. Yet after Arras, English control over Paris became vulnerable because Burgundian support had weakened or disappeared.
    * **Process**: Forces loyal to Charles VII entered Paris, and the city returned to Valois obedience. The transition did not instantly end the war in northern France, but it deprived the English of a major political anchor.
    * **Result**: Charles VII recovered the historic capital. The English still held **Normandy**, **Calais**, and territories in the southwest, but their claim to govern France from within its central political space was gravely damaged.
    * **Significance**: The recovery of Paris reversed the symbolic geography of the war. After this point, the Lancastrian regime increasingly looked like an occupying power holding fragments rather than a credible alternative monarchy for France.
* 1438–1439

  * **Royal and ecclesiastical reforms strengthen Charles VII’s capacity to govern and fight**

    * **Background**: Military recovery required institutional recovery. France could not expel the English through battlefield enthusiasm alone. The crown needed reliable revenue, disciplined troops, stronger royal justice, and control over ecclesiastical and political resources.
    * **Process**: Charles VII’s government strengthened royal authority through measures associated with taxation, church policy, and military organization. The **Pragmatic Sanction of Bourges** asserted significant royal and Gallican influence over ecclesiastical appointments and church governance in France. Military ordinances began to address the problem of uncontrolled armed bands and the need for more permanent, crown-directed forces.
    * **Result**: The French monarchy became better able to sustain war. Instead of relying only on feudal levies, temporary noble forces, or emergency mobilization, the crown moved toward more regularized military and fiscal structures.
    * **Significance**: These reforms are central to the war’s long-term importance. The conflict accelerated the development of stronger royal administration, permanent taxation, and standing military forces. Victory depended on state capacity as much as courage or battlefield luck.
    * **Common Pitfall**: French recovery should not be explained only by Joan of Arc or English weakness. Joan mattered, and English weakness mattered, but Charles VII’s later success depended heavily on institutional reform, fiscal consolidation, and military reorganization.
* 1440

  * **Spring–Summer, the Praguerie reveals aristocratic resistance to royal centralization**

    * **Background**: Charles VII’s reforms threatened noble autonomy. Efforts to discipline armed companies, regulate military command, and strengthen royal taxation created resistance among princes and great lords who feared losing military and political independence.
    * **Process**: A revolt known as the **Praguerie** broke out, involving nobles dissatisfied with royal reform. The rebellion included figures who opposed the crown’s attempt to control military structures. Charles VII suppressed it, preserving the reform direction of the monarchy.
    * **Result**: The failure of the revolt strengthened the king’s authority. It showed that the crown could resist noble obstruction and continue building the institutions needed for final victory over the English.
    * **Significance**: The Praguerie shows that state formation was not a smooth national awakening. It was a conflict between royal centralization and aristocratic power. The French monarchy’s success in the Hundred Years’ War required defeating or absorbing domestic resistance as well as foreign occupation.
* 1444

  * **May, the Truce of Tours gives Charles VII time to reorganize the French war machine**

    * **Background**: By the 1440s, both sides needed a pause. England was financially strained and politically divided, while France needed time to regularize its military system and reduce the damage caused by unemployed soldiers. The truce was tied to diplomacy, including the marriage arrangement between **Henry VI** and **Margaret of Anjou**.
    * **Process**: The **Truce of Tours** suspended major hostilities. For England, it reflected hope that marriage diplomacy and negotiation might preserve some continental position. For France, it offered space to reform forces, discipline armed men, and prepare for renewed action under better conditions.
    * **Result**: The truce did not settle the war. Instead, it created a breathing period in which the balance of capacity shifted further toward France.
    * **Significance**: Temporary peace often serves the stronger reorganizing state. In this case, the truce gave Charles VII an opportunity to turn recovery into superiority.
* 1445

  * **The compagnies d’ordonnance create a more permanent royal army**

    * **Background**: One of the great problems of late-medieval warfare was the presence of armed companies that lived by plunder when unpaid or unemployed. These forces could defend the kingdom, but they could also devastate it. Charles VII’s government needed to transform military violence from a semi-private aristocratic and mercenary system into a more controlled royal instrument.
    * **Process**: The crown organized **compagnies d’ordonnance**, more permanent cavalry companies under royal authority. These were supported by regular taxation and accompanied by efforts to manage infantry, artillery, and garrison structures. The reform did not create a modern national army, but it moved France toward standing military capacity.
    * **Result**: French military operations became more disciplined and sustainable. The crown could plan campaigns with forces more directly dependent on royal pay and command.
    * **Significance**: This reform is one of the clearest examples of the war’s state-building effect. The French monarchy learned that victory required permanent fiscal-military institutions. The Hundred Years’ War therefore helped push France toward a more centralized royal state.
* 1449

  * **Summer–Autumn, the French conquest of Normandy begins after the breakdown of the truce**

    * **Background**: The truce collapsed amid disputes, provocations, and the weakening of English credibility. Normandy had been the core achievement of Henry V’s conquest. If France could recover it, the Lancastrian project in northern France would be fundamentally broken.
    * **Process**: Charles VII’s forces moved rapidly into Normandy, supported by improved artillery, better-organized troops, and local willingness to abandon English rule. Towns and fortresses fell through a combination of siege, negotiation, and military pressure. The campaign showed how far French operational capacity had improved since the disasters of the fourteenth century.
    * **Result**: English positions in Normandy began collapsing. The war shifted from English occupation to French reconquest.
    * **Significance**: The campaign demonstrates that Charles VII’s reforms had practical military consequences. France now possessed the administrative and military tools to reconquer rather than merely resist.
* 1450

  * **April 15, Formigny destroys English field power in Normandy**

    * **Background**: English forces attempted to relieve pressure in Normandy, but they faced a French army increasingly capable of coordinated operations. French use of artillery and cavalry, combined with improved command, made the old English tactical advantage less reliable.
    * **Process**: At **Formigny**, French forces confronted an English army trying to restore the old pattern of disciplined defense. French artillery disrupted the English position, and the arrival and pressure of mounted forces helped turn disruption into collapse. The battle did not simply repeat Crécy or Agincourt in reverse; it reflected a changed military environment in which the French had learned to avoid the conditions that favored English archery and to combine artillery, cavalry, and coordinated pursuit.
    * **Result**: English military capacity in Normandy was broken. **Caen**, **Cherbourg**, and other remaining positions soon fell or became untenable.
    * **Significance**: Formigny was the decisive battle for Normandy. It marks the point at which Henry V’s conquest ceased to be recoverable in practice.
  * **Summer–August, the fall of the remaining Norman strongholds ends English Normandy**

    * **Background**: Once English field forces were defeated, garrisons could no longer rely on major relief. Normandy’s towns and fortresses faced siege, negotiation, and isolation.
    * **Process**: French forces captured remaining centers, including important strongholds such as **Caen** and **Cherbourg**. Local elites adjusted to the new balance because political survival often required accommodation with the power that could actually protect and govern the region.
    * **Result**: Normandy returned to Valois control. England lost the central territorial achievement of the Lancastrian war.
    * **Significance**: The loss of Normandy reduced English continental power to a much narrower base. It also intensified political pressure inside England, where defeat in France contributed to elite conflict and criticism of royal government.
* 1451

  * **June, Bordeaux submits and English Gascony appears lost**

    * **Background**: **Gascony** was different from Normandy. It had long-standing commercial and political ties to England, especially through the wine trade. Many Gascon elites valued English connection because it supported privileges, trade access, and local autonomy against French centralizing pressure.
    * **Process**: French forces advanced into the southwest and captured **Bordeaux**. The submission of the city seemed to end English rule in Gascony. Yet the region’s economic and political links to England meant that French control was less secure than in some other recovered territories.
    * **Result**: England lost nearly all of its historic continental possessions except **Calais**. But Gascon discontent created an opportunity for one final English intervention.
    * **Significance**: The fall of Bordeaux shows both French strength and the complexity of local allegiance. Regions did not simply “belong” naturally to one crown. Trade, law, privilege, taxation, and local elite interest shaped political loyalty.
* 1452

  * **October, Talbot returns to Bordeaux and briefly revives English hopes**

    * **Background**: Many in **Bordeaux** and parts of Gascony disliked French rule or feared loss of privileges under the Valois monarchy. They appealed for English help, giving England a final chance to restore its position in the southwest.
    * **Process**: **John Talbot**, earl of Shrewsbury, landed and was welcomed by Bordeaux. His return temporarily reversed the French conquest of the previous year and showed that English rule still had local supporters in Gascony. Talbot was an experienced commander whose reputation rested on speed, aggression, and long service in France, but the strategic environment had changed: France now had stronger artillery, better organization, and greater capacity to concentrate force.
    * **Result**: English hopes revived briefly, but they depended on rapid success before the French crown could respond. The intervention created the conditions for the final major battle of the war.
    * **Significance**: Talbot’s return shows that English Gascony was not merely an occupation imposed from abroad. Local support for England could be real, especially where economic interests and privileges were involved. Yet local support could not compensate for England’s declining military and fiscal capacity.
* 1453

  * **July 17, Castillon ends the war militarily and marks the triumph of French artillery-centered warfare**

    * **Background**: The French moved against English-held **Castillon** near Bordeaux. By this phase, French warfare had changed significantly. The crown used more organized troops, stronger siege methods, and effective artillery under commanders such as the **Bureau brothers**. English commanders could no longer assume that aggressive advance against a French position would reproduce earlier victories.
    * **Process**: Talbot moved toward **Castillon** believing he had to act quickly before French pressure crushed Bordeaux’s restored English position. He attacked a fortified French artillery camp, misjudging the strength and readiness of the position. The French had prepared earthworks and guns, turning the camp itself into a weapon. Artillery and defensive fire broke the English assault; Talbot was killed, and the English army was defeated. The battle revealed how much the tactical balance had changed since Crécy, Poitiers, and Agincourt.
    * **Result**: The defeat destroyed the last serious English attempt to recover Gascony. Bordeaux surrendered again later in the year, and English continental rule was reduced essentially to **Calais**.
    * **Significance**: Castillon is often treated as the final battle of the Hundred Years’ War. It also symbolizes a military transition: fortified artillery positions, improved royal armies, and coordinated state resources had overtaken the conditions that once favored English defensive archery tactics.
    * **Common Pitfall**: Castillon was not just “the battle where cannon defeated the longbow.” It was a result of deeper changes: French fiscal reform, professionalized forces, siege expertise, artillery development, and England’s inability to sustain large continental commitments.
  * **October, Bordeaux’s final surrender ends England’s effective territorial empire in France**

    * **Immediate Impact**: The final surrender of **Bordeaux** after the defeat at **Castillon** ended English rule in **Gascony**, the region that had tied the English crown to southwestern France for centuries. This was not merely the loss of one city. It meant the collapse of the commercial, legal, and aristocratic network through which England had maintained a continental political identity.
    * **Impact on England**: The English crown still retained **Calais**, but Calais was a fortified enclave rather than the basis for a French territorial empire. The practical result was that the English monarchy lost the lands that had justified repeated taxation, noble military service, and dynastic claims since the age of **Edward III**.
    * **Impact on France**: For **Charles VII**, the recovery of **Gascony** completed the major territorial reconquest of the kingdom. The French monarchy could now present itself as the effective ruler of almost all the lands claimed by the French crown, except for the English-held enclave of Calais and some still-powerful semi-independent princely territories.
    * **Long-Term Significance**: The end of English Gascony transformed the meaning of the English claim to France. The claim survived as royal ideology, but it no longer rested on broad territorial control. This separation between formal title and actual power became one of the clearest signs that the old Anglo-French feudal world had collapsed.
  * **After the final military defeat, the English monarchy faces a crisis of credibility**

    * **Immediate Impact**: The defeat in France damaged the prestige of **Henry VI’s** government. The Lancastrian monarchy had inherited the memory of **Henry V’s** conquests, but by the early 1450s it had lost Normandy, Gascony, and nearly all practical influence in France. The contrast between inherited glory and present failure made the regime look incompetent.
    * **Impact on Political Elites**: English nobles, soldiers, creditors, and administrators had invested money, reputation, and service in the French war. When the war ended in defeat, blame turned inward. Failed commanders, royal advisers, and court factions became targets of resentment.
    * **Indirect Impact**: The loss of France did not by itself cause the **Wars of the Roses**, but it contributed directly to the political environment that made dynastic conflict possible. Military failure abroad weakened confidence in royal authority at home, especially when combined with Henry VI’s personal incapacity, financial weakness, and factional government.
* 1455–1485

  * **The Wars of the Roses redirect military aristocratic violence inward**

    * **Background**: During the Hundred Years’ War, many English nobles had built careers through service in France. They commanded retinues, negotiated ransoms, held captaincies, and gained military experience. After the collapse of the continental war, that martial aristocratic culture did not disappear.
    * **Immediate Impact**: The end of large-scale campaigning in France removed an external arena for noble competition. English aristocratic rivalry increasingly turned toward domestic politics. Military households, private affinities, and noble retainers became instruments of factional struggle inside England.
    * **Indirect Impact**: The **Wars of the Roses** were caused by multiple factors, including dynastic rivalry, weak kingship, aristocratic competition, and financial disorder. But the failure of the French war intensified all of these. It discredited the Lancastrian regime, deprived nobles of continental rewards, and made domestic power struggle more attractive and more dangerous.
    * **Historical Significance**: The aftermath of the Hundred Years’ War therefore shaped English politics by transforming a monarchy of external conquest into a monarchy trapped in internal conflict. The same elite culture that had fought in France now fought over control of the English crown.
* 1450s–1460s

  * **Charles VII’s monarchy turns wartime recovery into institutional consolidation**

    * **Immediate Impact**: After victory, **Charles VII** did not simply demobilize and return France to an earlier feudal order. The crown preserved many of the fiscal and military tools that had made reconquest possible. Permanent taxation, royal artillery, and more regular military companies became part of the monarchy’s practical power.
    * **Impact on Royal Authority**: The French crown emerged from the war with stronger authority over territory, taxation, and armed force. This did not create absolute monarchy immediately, but it gave the king a stronger administrative and military base than earlier medieval French kings had possessed.
    * **Impact on Local Powers**: Towns, nobles, and regional elites still retained privileges, but they now faced a crown that had proved it could raise revenue, command armies, and recover territory. The balance between local autonomy and royal centralization shifted toward the monarchy.
    * **Long-Term Significance**: The Hundred Years’ War helped transform the French crown into a more durable fiscal-military monarchy. Victory depended on institutions, and those institutions survived victory.
* 1461–1483

  * **Louis XI inherits the strengthened monarchy and applies it against internal rivals**

    * **Background**: **Louis XI**, son of **Charles VII**, inherited a kingdom strengthened by the end of the Hundred Years’ War. He did not inherit a fully centralized state, but he inherited a monarchy with greater fiscal reach, stronger administrative tools, and a clearer territorial program.
    * **Immediate Impact**: The strengthened postwar monarchy allowed Louis XI to act more aggressively against overmighty princes, especially **Burgundy**. The French crown could now focus less on English occupation and more on internal consolidation.
    * **Indirect Impact**: The disappearance of England as a major territorial power in France changed the political balance among French princes. During the Hundred Years’ War, Burgundy could use England as an ally or counterweight. After English collapse, Burgundy faced the French crown more directly.
    * **Historical Significance**: The postwar French monarchy did not become powerful simply because England lost. It became powerful because the war had trained the monarchy to use taxation, diplomacy, military organization, and territorial policy together.
* 1460s–1470s

  * **Burgundy loses its role as kingmaker between England and France**

    * **Background**: During the war, **Burgundy** had been decisive. Its alliance with England helped make **Henry V’s** conquest politically viable, while its reconciliation with **Charles VII** through the **Treaty of Arras** helped isolate the English.
    * **Immediate Impact**: After 1453, Burgundy remained wealthy and powerful, but its diplomatic position changed. It could no longer balance effectively between a strong English occupation in northern France and a weakened Valois monarchy. England had largely disappeared from the French territorial struggle.
    * **Impact on French Politics**: This left Burgundy more exposed to direct confrontation with the French crown. The political question shifted from “Will Burgundy support England or France?” to “Can Burgundy survive as a quasi-independent princely power beside a recovering French monarchy?”
    * **Long-Term Significance**: The later conflict between **Louis XI** and **Charles the Bold** grew out of this postwar balance. The Hundred Years’ War had allowed Burgundy to rise; the end of the war made Burgundy’s autonomy harder to preserve.
* 1475

  * **The Treaty of Picquigny confirms that England cannot realistically restart the conquest of France**

    * **Background**: **Edward IV** of England invaded France with the hope of reviving English claims and extracting advantage from French politics. But the conditions that had enabled earlier English success no longer existed. France was more consolidated, and England was weakened by decades of internal conflict.
    * **Immediate Impact**: The **Treaty of Picquigny** ended Edward IV’s expedition through negotiation rather than conquest. **Louis XI** paid Edward to withdraw, turning a potential military campaign into a diplomatic settlement.
    * **Impact on England**: The treaty showed that England could still pressure France, but not restore the territorial empire of Edward III or Henry V. English claims to France increasingly became bargaining tools rather than realistic programs of conquest.
    * **Impact on France**: For Louis XI, the treaty was a practical success. By using money and diplomacy, he avoided a renewed English war and kept attention focused on internal French consolidation and the Burgundian problem.
    * **Historical Significance**: Picquigny marks the postwar transformation of Anglo-French rivalry. England had not forgotten France, but it no longer possessed the conditions necessary for sustained continental reconquest.
* 1477

  * **January, the death of Charles the Bold allows the French crown to absorb parts of the Burgundian inheritance**

    * **Background**: **Charles the Bold**, duke of **Burgundy**, attempted to build a powerful territorial state between France and the Empire. His ambitions represented one of the last great princely challenges to Valois consolidation after the Hundred Years’ War.
    * **Immediate Impact**: Charles’s death at **Nancy** created a succession crisis. **Louis XI** moved quickly to seize or pressure Burgundian territories, especially those that the French crown could claim as reverting fiefs.
    * **Impact on France**: The French crown gained from Burgundy’s weakening, though not all Burgundian lands were absorbed. The Burgundian inheritance also passed partly through **Mary of Burgundy** into the Habsburg orbit, creating a new long-term geopolitical problem.
    * **Indirect Impact**: The collapse of independent Burgundian power confirmed one of the central outcomes of the post-Hundred Years’ War period: the French monarchy was increasingly able to defeat or absorb great internal rivals.
    * **Long-Term Significance**: The end of Burgundian autonomy did not simply close the Hundred Years’ War. It redirected European politics. The French crown no longer faced England as the main continental threat; instead, rivalry with the **Habsburgs** would become increasingly important.
* Late 15th century

  * **The French fiscal-military monarchy becomes a durable political structure**

    * **Background**: The Hundred Years’ War forced the French crown to develop more reliable taxation, artillery, paid soldiers, and administrative coordination. These structures were not temporary wartime improvisations only.
    * **Immediate Impact**: After the war, the French crown retained stronger control over military resources. The **compagnies d’ordonnance**, artillery administration, and permanent fiscal mechanisms gave the monarchy a practical ability to project force inside and outside the kingdom.
    * **Impact on Warfare**: The late medieval battlefield increasingly favored rulers who could organize artillery, sieges, engineers, supply, and paid forces. The aristocratic cavalry charge did not disappear, but it no longer defined military success by itself.
    * **Indirect Impact**: These developments helped prepare France for the **Italian Wars** at the end of the fifteenth century. The same kind of fiscal-military capacity that helped expel the English also allowed French kings to intervene beyond France.
    * **Historical Significance**: The war’s military legacy was not simply that the longbow declined or cannon rose. The deeper transformation was institutional: war increasingly required organized state finance, permanent forces, technical expertise, and centralized command.
  * **Political identity becomes more territorial, but not yet modern nationalism**

    * **Immediate Impact**: The war sharpened distinctions between English and French political communities. Royal propaganda, taxation, military service, occupation, resistance, and dynastic claims all encouraged people to think more clearly about allegiance.
    * **Impact on France**: The French monarchy increasingly presented itself as the defender and restorer of the kingdom’s territorial integrity. The recovery of Normandy, Paris, and Gascony gave this claim practical force.
    * **Impact on England**: English identity was also affected. The loss of France pushed England toward a more insular political orientation, even though English kings continued to preserve claims and memories of continental rule.
    * **Common Pitfall**: This should not be mistaken for modern nationalism. Many people still acted according to local privilege, lordship, trade, dynasty, religion, and survival. The war strengthened territorial monarchy, but it did not create modern nation-states in a fully developed sense.
* Late 15th–early 16th century

  * **The English claim to France survives after the loss of real territorial power**

    * **Background**: After **1453**, the English crown no longer possessed the material base needed to reconquer France, but English monarchs did not immediately abandon the style and ideology of claiming the French crown. Medieval and early modern dynastic titles often outlived actual control. A claim could remain politically useful even when it had become militarily unrealistic.
    * **Immediate Impact**: The claim to France allowed English kings to preserve continuity with **Edward III** and **Henry V**. It functioned as a language of royal dignity, diplomatic pressure, and historical memory rather than as an immediately executable military plan.
    * **Indirect Impact**: The persistence of the claim kept Anglo-French rivalry ideologically alive. Even when England could no longer occupy Normandy or Gascony, the monarchy could still invoke the French title to assert rank, grievance, or bargaining position.
    * **Historical Significance**: This shows the difference between **formal dynastic right** and **effective territorial sovereignty**. The Hundred Years’ War ended in military reality before it ended in royal titulature.
  * **Tudor foreign policy treats France less as recoverable inheritance and more as a strategic rival**

    * **Background**: Under the **Tudors**, especially **Henry VII** and **Henry VIII**, England still remembered the continental ambitions of earlier kings. But the political environment had changed. France was more consolidated, England had emerged from civil war, and the costs of continental conquest were far clearer.
    * **Immediate Impact**: English campaigns in France during the Tudor period were more limited than the projects of **Edward III** or **Henry V**. They aimed at pressure, prestige, alliance leverage, border gains, or diplomatic advantage rather than a realistic conquest of the French kingdom.
    * **Indirect Impact**: France remained England’s major rival, but the rivalry increasingly operated through diplomacy, dynastic marriage, naval policy, alliance systems, and later religious conflict. The older feudal problem of English lordship inside France had largely disappeared.
    * **Historical Significance**: The post-Hundred Years’ War relationship between England and France became less feudal and more geopolitical. The rivalry survived, but its structure changed.
* 1513

  * **September, Flodden shows that Anglo-French conflict still affects the British Isles**

    * **Background**: Anglo-French rivalry continued to intersect with the **Auld Alliance** between **France** and **Scotland**. Even after the main territorial war in France had ended, French diplomacy could still pressure England by encouraging Scottish action.
    * **Immediate Impact**: During **Henry VIII’s** campaign against France, **James IV of Scotland** invaded England in support of France. The English defeated the Scots at **Flodden**, where James IV was killed. The battle showed that conflict with France could still trigger war on England’s northern frontier.
    * **Indirect Impact**: The event reinforced the strategic problem that had existed throughout the Hundred Years’ War: England could not treat France as only a Channel or continental issue. French alliances could make the war a British Isles problem.
    * **Historical Significance**: Flodden belongs to the postwar world, not the Hundred Years’ War itself, but it shows the survival of the same diplomatic geography. France, Scotland, and England remained connected in a triangular strategic system.
* 1520s–1550s

  * **Gunpowder warfare makes the older Hundred Years’ War battlefield pattern obsolete**

    * **Background**: The later phase of the Hundred Years’ War had already shown the growing importance of **artillery**, especially in sieges and fortified positions such as **Castillon**. In the sixteenth century, gunpowder warfare became even more central to European military organization.
    * **Immediate Impact**: Fortifications, artillery trains, professional infantry, engineers, and state finance became increasingly important. The old image of the war as a contest between mounted knights and English longbowmen became less relevant to actual military practice.
    * **Indirect Impact**: Rulers needed larger revenues and more sophisticated administration to wage war effectively. The fiscal-military tendencies visible in late fifteenth-century France expanded across Europe.
    * **Historical Significance**: The Hundred Years’ War did not single-handedly create gunpowder warfare, but its final phase clearly belonged to a transition in which technical arms, siege logistics, and state organization became increasingly decisive.
* 1558

  * **January, the fall of Calais completes the territorial aftermath of the Hundred Years’ War**

    * **Background**: **Calais** had survived as the last major English possession in France after **1453**. Its continued English control preserved a visible remnant of the medieval Anglo-French territorial struggle.
    * **Immediate Impact**: The French capture of Calais removed England’s final mainland foothold. The loss was militarily important, but also emotionally and symbolically severe because it ended the last physical trace of the English continental position created during the medieval wars.
    * **Indirect Impact**: After Calais, English policy could no longer pretend that it still possessed a serious territorial base in France. Future English power would increasingly rely on maritime strategy, overseas expansion, dynastic diplomacy, and naval conflict rather than direct territorial lordship in France.
    * **Historical Significance**: Although the conventional Hundred Years’ War had ended in **1453**, the fall of Calais in **1558** closed the territorial epilogue. The English king was no longer a practical French landholder in any major sense.
* 16th century

  * **France turns from recovery to expansion and European rivalry**

    * **Background**: The victory in the Hundred Years’ War gave the French monarchy a more coherent territorial base. Once the English threat had largely disappeared from French soil, French kings could redirect ambition outward.
    * **Immediate Impact**: The **Italian Wars** showed that France had become capable of projecting force beyond its own borders. The same fiscal and military capacities strengthened during and after the Hundred Years’ War helped make these campaigns possible.
    * **Indirect Impact**: France’s main strategic rival gradually shifted from England to the **Habsburgs**, especially because the Burgundian inheritance helped connect Habsburg power to territories surrounding France. This changed the main axis of European politics.
    * **Historical Significance**: The postwar French monarchy was no longer simply trying to survive English invasion. It was becoming one of the central powers in early modern European politics.
  * **England’s strategic orientation moves from continental lordship toward maritime power**

    * **Background**: The loss of Normandy, Gascony, and Calais made large-scale territorial monarchy in France unrealistic. England remained involved in European diplomacy, but its long-term strategic options changed.
    * **Immediate Impact**: England increasingly relied on naval strength, trade, diplomacy, and later overseas expansion. This was not an instant transformation, but the end of French landholding removed one of the older foundations of English policy.
    * **Indirect Impact**: The English monarchy’s center of gravity shifted more clearly toward the British Isles and maritime routes. The later rise of English and British sea power cannot be reduced to the Hundred Years’ War, but the loss of continental lordship was one of the conditions that made maritime orientation more important.
    * **Historical Significance**: The Hundred Years’ War’s failure helped define what England would *not* become: it would not become a durable territorial monarchy inside France. Its later imperial development followed a different path.
* 1603

  * **The Union of the Crowns changes England’s strategic position after centuries of Anglo-Scottish-French linkage**

    * **Background**: During the Hundred Years’ War and after it, the **Auld Alliance** between France and Scotland had repeatedly threatened England with a two-front strategic problem. French pressure in the south could combine with Scottish invasion in the north.
    * **Immediate Impact**: When **James VI of Scotland** became **James I of England**, the English and Scottish crowns were united under one monarch. This did not immediately create a unified British state, but it transformed the old strategic geometry.
    * **Indirect Impact**: France could no longer use Scotland against England in the same straightforward way as before. The diplomatic pattern that had shaped much medieval Anglo-French warfare weakened.
    * **Historical Significance**: This was a distant consequence rather than a direct result of the Hundred Years’ War. But it closed one of the strategic conditions that had made Anglo-French conflict so dangerous for England during the medieval period.
* 17th century

  * **The memory of the Hundred Years’ War survives in royal ideology and anti-French politics**

    * **Background**: Long after the war ended, English political culture remembered victories such as **Crécy**, **Poitiers**, and **Agincourt**. These memories could be used to celebrate martial kingship, criticize weakness, or frame France as a traditional rival.
    * **Immediate Impact**: The war’s memory gave English rulers and writers a symbolic archive of heroic victory. **Henry V** became especially important as an image of successful kingship and national martial glory.
    * **Indirect Impact**: Such memory could distort historical understanding. It encouraged a selective focus on English victories and heroic battles while obscuring the eventual English defeat, the suffering of civilians, the importance of French state-building, and the complexity of Burgundian and Gascon politics.
    * **Historical Significance**: The war’s cultural afterlife mattered because memory shaped later political imagination. The Hundred Years’ War became not only an event in medieval history but a resource for later English identity-making.
* 18th century

  * **Anglo-French rivalry becomes imperial and maritime rather than feudal and dynastic**

    * **Background**: By the eighteenth century, the old medieval issues of homage, Aquitaine, and succession had lost practical relevance. England, then Britain, and France increasingly competed through colonies, trade, naval warfare, and European alliance systems.
    * **Immediate Impact**: Conflicts such as the **War of the Spanish Succession**, the **Seven Years’ War**, and later imperial wars show a very different structure of rivalry. The battleground shifted from feudal territories in France to global trade routes, colonies, and naval dominance.
    * **Indirect Impact**: The long memory of Anglo-French hostility remained useful, but the material stakes had changed. The rivalry was no longer about whether an English king could be a French duke or king; it was about global power, commerce, and empire.
    * **Historical Significance**: This long transformation shows the historical distance between the Hundred Years’ War and later Anglo-French conflict. Continuity existed at the level of rivalry and memory, but the institutional and geopolitical structure was fundamentally different.
* 1801

  * **The British monarchy finally drops the formal royal claim to France**

    * **Background**: English and later British monarchs had retained the formal style of claiming France long after they lost effective control over French territory. The claim had become ceremonial, not realistic.
    * **Immediate Impact**: With the creation of the **United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland**, the royal style was changed, and the formal claim to the French crown was abandoned. This removed a dynastic title whose practical basis had disappeared centuries earlier.
    * **Indirect Impact**: The abandonment of the title confirmed the final symbolic end of a claim that had begun in the political world of Edward III. By this point, Britain and France were modern rival states, not medieval dynastic competitors over feudal lordship.
    * **Historical Significance**: The claim’s survival until **1801** reveals how long medieval political language could persist after medieval political realities had vanished. The Hundred Years’ War ended militarily in **1453**, territorially with the loss of **Calais** in **1558**, and symbolically only when the royal claim was formally dropped.

* 19th century

  * **Romantic nationalism turns the Hundred Years’ War into a story of national awakening**

    * **Background**: In the nineteenth century, European historians, writers, and political cultures often interpreted medieval history through the lens of **nation formation**. The Hundred Years’ War became attractive because it seemed to offer a dramatic story of England and France discovering themselves through conflict.
    * **Immediate Impact**: French historical writing increasingly treated **Joan of Arc** as a figure of national resistance, while English cultural memory continued to celebrate **Agincourt** and **Henry V** as symbols of martial courage. The war was no longer only a medieval dynastic conflict; it became a storehouse of national symbols.
    * **Indirect Impact**: This nineteenth-century framing encouraged simplified readings. France could be imagined as awakening through Joan, while England could be imagined as heroic through its battlefield victories. Such interpretations often reduced the roles of **Gascony**, **Burgundy**, feudal law, dynastic claims, and local political interests.
    * **Historical Significance**: The nineteenth-century memory of the war shaped how later students and readers first encountered it. Many popular versions still inherit this framework, even though modern scholarship treats the war as a complex interaction of monarchy, lordship, fiscal institutions, diplomacy, military systems, and political legitimacy.
  * **Joan of Arc becomes a contested national, religious, and political symbol**

    * **Background**: **Joan of Arc** had already been rehabilitated in the fifteenth century, but her symbolic importance expanded dramatically in later French culture. She could be interpreted as a saintly figure, a patriotic heroine, a royalist symbol, a republican symbol, or a figure of popular resistance.
    * **Immediate Impact**: Her memory became politically flexible. Different groups could claim her because her historical role touched several powerful themes at once: religion, monarchy, foreign occupation, youth, prophecy, military courage, and French territorial recovery.
    * **Indirect Impact**: This symbolic flexibility sometimes made Joan appear detached from her fifteenth-century context. Later interpretations could turn her into a modern nationalist before modern nationalism existed, or into a purely religious figure without enough attention to dynastic politics and royal legitimacy.
    * **Historical Significance**: Joan’s afterlife shows how historical actors can become symbols far beyond the conditions in which they lived. Her medieval role was tied to **Charles VII**, **Reims**, and the crisis of Valois legitimacy; her later memory became a broader debate over France itself.
* Late 19th–early 20th century

  * **Military history turns Crécy, Poitiers, Agincourt, Formigny, and Castillon into case studies of tactical change**

    * **Background**: As professional military history developed, the Hundred Years’ War became a useful field for studying the relationship between weapons, tactics, discipline, command, terrain, and social organization. The famous contrast between the **longbow** and mounted chivalry attracted special attention.
    * **Immediate Impact**: Battles such as **Crécy**, **Poitiers**, and **Agincourt** were often used to illustrate the decline of aristocratic cavalry and the rise of infantry missile power. Later battles such as **Formigny** and **Castillon** were used to show the increasing importance of artillery and organized royal armies.
    * **Indirect Impact**: This created another simplification. The war could be reduced to a technological story: “longbow defeats knight,” then “cannon defeats longbow.” That pattern is memorable, but incomplete. Tactical success depended on deployment, terrain, morale, command, logistics, weather, social discipline, and institutional capacity.
    * **Historical Significance**: The military legacy of the war is best understood not as a single weapons revolution, but as a series of tactical and institutional adaptations. The English early-war system worked under specific conditions; the French late-war system succeeded because it combined artillery, taxation, permanent companies, siegecraft, and political recovery.
  * **Medieval constitutional history uses the war to explain taxation and representative institutions**

    * **Background**: The war forced both England and France to raise money repeatedly. This made it important for historians of political institutions, because taxation connected warfare to parliaments, estates, royal councils, credit, and public consent.
    * **Immediate Impact**: In **England**, repeated war taxation strengthened the importance of **Parliament** as a body through which the crown sought consent for extraordinary revenue. In **France**, the pressure of war helped normalize more permanent royal taxation, especially as the monarchy rebuilt its military system under **Charles VII**.
    * **Indirect Impact**: The two kingdoms therefore developed different fiscal-political patterns. England preserved a stronger link between extraordinary taxation and parliamentary bargaining, while France moved more clearly toward permanent royal fiscal capacity. This contrast became important for later historians comparing English constitutional development with French monarchy.
    * **Historical Significance**: The Hundred Years’ War’s institutional legacy lies partly in this fiscal contrast. War did not mechanically create Parliament or French monarchy, but it intensified the need for governments to justify, collect, and regularize revenue.
* Early–mid 20th century

  * **World-war-era memory makes the medieval Anglo-French conflict newly sensitive**

    * **Background**: During the twentieth century, especially around the two world wars, England and France were often allies rather than enemies. This changed the emotional use of the Hundred Years’ War. The old story of permanent Anglo-French hostility became less politically useful in official culture.
    * **Immediate Impact**: The war remained important as medieval history, but its nationalist edge softened in some contexts. The memory of **Agincourt** or **Joan of Arc** could still be invoked, yet modern Anglo-French cooperation made crude anti-French or anti-English readings less acceptable.
    * **Indirect Impact**: Historians became more interested in structural explanation: administration, finance, social impact, military organization, and documentary evidence. The war was increasingly studied as a complex medieval system rather than merely as a heroic national drama.
    * **Historical Significance**: This shift helped move interpretation away from patriotic myth toward institutional and social history. It did not erase national memory, but it complicated it.
  * **Social history redirects attention from kings and battles to civilians, taxation, and devastation**

    * **Background**: Earlier accounts often centered on kings, princes, battles, and treaties. Twentieth-century social and economic history asked different questions: What happened to peasants, towns, merchants, laborers, soldiers, taxpayers, and refugees?
    * **Immediate Impact**: The **chevauchée** became more than a cavalry operation in military narrative. It became evidence of systematic violence against rural society. Historians paid greater attention to burned villages, disrupted agriculture, ransom burdens, garrison violence, famine, and local survival strategies.
    * **Indirect Impact**: This changed the moral and analytical picture of the war. The Hundred Years’ War could no longer be understood mainly as a sequence of famous battles. Its ordinary victims became central to explaining how war affected society and state power.
    * **Historical Significance**: Social history restored the material cost of the conflict. The war’s significance lies not only in **Crécy**, **Poitiers**, **Agincourt**, or **Castillon**, but also in the pressure placed on communities that had to feed armies, pay taxes, endure raids, and negotiate survival.
* Late 20th century

  * **Revisionist scholarship weakens the idea of a simple English-versus-French national war**

    * **Background**: Later scholarship became more cautious about projecting modern nation-states backward into the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries. Medieval allegiance was often local, dynastic, feudal, commercial, and factional rather than simply national.
    * **Immediate Impact**: Historians placed more weight on **Gascony**, **Burgundy**, **Flanders**, **Scotland**, **Castile**, and internal French civil war. This widened the interpretation beyond the two crowns alone.
    * **Indirect Impact**: The war began to appear less like a straightforward national duel and more like a layered political crisis across western Europe. The English crown, French crown, Burgundian dukes, Gascon elites, Flemish towns, Scottish allies, and Castilian fleets all became part of the explanation.
    * **Historical Significance**: This interpretive shift makes the war more historically accurate but less simple. It shows that the conflict helped produce stronger national monarchies, but it did not begin from fully modern national identities.
  * **State-formation studies reinterpret the war as a fiscal-military crisis**

    * **Background**: The war placed extreme pressure on medieval governments. Armies, fleets, garrisons, artillery, diplomacy, ransoms, and fortifications all required money. This made the conflict central to the history of **state formation**.
    * **Immediate Impact**: Scholars increasingly emphasized taxation, credit, administrative records, military contracts, and institutional continuity. The question became not only “Who won?” but “Which political system could sustain war over time?”
    * **Indirect Impact**: This approach helps explain the final French victory. France did not win simply because Joan appeared, or because England made mistakes. France won because the monarchy gradually developed stronger fiscal and military instruments, especially under **Charles VII**.
    * **Historical Significance**: The Hundred Years’ War becomes a major example of how prolonged conflict can transform political institutions. War damaged societies, but it also forced governments to become more organized, more extractive, and more territorially focused.
* 21st century

  * **Modern teaching increasingly presents the war as a connected system rather than a list of battles**

    * **Background**: Current historical teaching often tries to move beyond a battlefield sequence. The war is now commonly explained through causes, institutions, military change, diplomacy, civil war, economic pressure, social suffering, and memory.
    * **Immediate Impact**: A strong account of the war now needs to include **Gascony**, the **Valois succession**, **Crécy**, **Poitiers**, the **Treaty of Brétigny**, **Charles V’s recovery**, the **Armagnac–Burgundian civil war**, **Henry V**, **Joan of Arc**, **Arras**, **Formigny**, **Castillon**, and the postwar consequences.
    * **Indirect Impact**: This makes the war harder to summarize but easier to understand accurately. Instead of one cause and one turning point, the war appears as a sequence of connected crises: feudal, dynastic, military, fiscal, diplomatic, social, and symbolic.
    * **Historical Significance**: The best modern interpretation treats the Hundred Years’ War as a long transformation in western European monarchy. It began with medieval lordship and inheritance disputes; it ended by revealing the importance of taxation, administration, artillery, standing forces, and territorial sovereignty.
  * **The main interpretive warning is to separate direct consequences from later memory**

    * **Background**: Not every later Anglo-French rivalry, English naval development, French centralization, or national myth can be directly attributed to the Hundred Years’ War. Some are direct consequences; others are indirect continuities; still others are later reinterpretations.
    * **Direct Consequences**: The direct consequences include the loss of English Normandy and Gascony, the strengthening of the French crown, the weakening of Lancastrian prestige, the survival of Calais, and the decline of English territorial lordship in France.
    * **Indirect Consequences**: The indirect consequences include England’s later insular and maritime orientation, the intensification of domestic aristocratic conflict, France’s stronger monarchy under later kings, and the changing balance between France and Burgundy.
    * **Memory Effects**: The memory effects include the heroic image of **Henry V**, the national and religious symbolism of **Joan of Arc**, the mythic status of **Agincourt**, and the later use of the war as a story of English courage or French awakening.
    * **Historical Significance**: This distinction is essential. The Hundred Years’ War must be studied both as a medieval conflict and as a later memory tradition, but those two layers should not be confused.

### 1 — What Was the Hundred Years’ War?

**Q: Was the Hundred Years’ War really one continuous war?**

No. The **Hundred Years’ War** was not a single uninterrupted war lasting exactly one hundred years. It is the conventional name for a long series of conflicts between the kings of **England** and **France**, usually dated from **1337 to 1453**. Within that period there were major campaigns, truces, diplomatic negotiations, civil wars, dynastic crises, and long pauses in large-scale fighting.

The name is useful because it captures a long historical struggle, but it can also mislead. The conflict was not one simple war between two modern nation-states. It began in a medieval world of **feudal lordship**, **dynastic inheritance**, **homage**, **territorial rights**, and **aristocratic politics**. By the end, however, it had helped push both England and France toward more centralized forms of monarchy.

**Q: What was the war mainly about?**

At its core, the war was about who had the right to rule parts of France, and eventually who had the right to rule France itself. The English king held lands in southwestern France, especially **Gascony** or **Aquitaine**, but he held them in a legally awkward way. As king of England, he was a sovereign ruler. But as duke of Aquitaine, he was technically a vassal of the French king.

This created a structural contradiction. The French king could claim the right to judge, discipline, or even confiscate the English king’s French lands. The English king could see that as an intolerable attack on his royal dignity and territorial power. The war grew out of that contradiction.

**Q: Why did the English king claim the French crown?**

The dynastic claim came from **Edward III** of England. His mother, **Isabella of France**, was the daughter of **Philip IV of France**. When the direct male Capetian line failed, Edward argued that he had a legitimate claim to the French throne through his mother.

The French political elite rejected that claim and supported **Philip VI of Valois**, a male-line relative of the Capetian dynasty. Later legal language associated this rejection with the idea that the French crown could not pass through a woman. But the issue was not only technical inheritance law. French nobles did not want the French kingdom to fall under a ruler whose main political base was England.

**Q: Was it a national war between England and France?**

Only partly, and not in the modern sense. English and French identities did become sharper during the war, but medieval political loyalty was layered. People acted according to dynasty, lordship, trade interest, local privilege, religion, aristocratic faction, and practical survival.

A Gascon merchant might prefer English rule because it protected wine trade with England. A Burgundian noble might ally with England because he hated the Armagnac faction more than he hated the English. A Parisian citizen might support whichever side seemed able to restore order. The war helped create stronger English and French political identities, but it did not begin as a modern nationalist war.

### 2 — Why Did the War Begin?

**Q: What made England and France enemies before 1337?**

The deeper background goes back to the **Angevin Empire** of the twelfth century. English kings once held enormous territories in France, especially under **Henry II** and his descendants. These lands included **Normandy**, **Anjou**, **Maine**, **Touraine**, **Poitou**, and **Aquitaine**.

French kings gradually recovered much of this territory, especially after **King John of England** lost Normandy and other lands in the early thirteenth century. But England retained Gascony. This meant that a large piece of southwestern France remained tied to the English crown. For the French monarchy, this was a long-term obstacle to territorial consolidation. For the English monarchy, Gascony was economically and politically valuable.

**Q: Why was Gascony so important?**

Gascony mattered because it was rich, strategically located, and commercially connected to England. Its wine trade with England was especially important. Bordeaux wine became a major export, and Gascon elites often benefited from their relationship with the English crown.

This meant Gascony was not merely an occupied foreign possession. Some local interests genuinely preferred English rule, especially when English kings protected privileges and trade. French royal authority, by contrast, could look more intrusive because it implied legal appeals, taxation, and stronger central control.

**Q: What immediate event started the war?**

The conventional starting point is **1337**, when **Philip VI of France** confiscated **Guyenne**, the English-held duchy in southwestern France. In feudal terms, confiscation meant that the French king claimed Edward III had failed as a vassal. In political terms, it was a direct attack on English continental power.

Edward III responded by escalating the dispute. Instead of accepting the French king’s superior jurisdiction, he revived and asserted his own claim to the French crown. This turned a dispute over land and homage into a dynastic war over kingship.

**Q: Did both sides expect the conflict to last so long?**

No. Medieval rulers often began wars expecting limited gains, negotiated settlements, or temporary advantage. The Hundred Years’ War lasted so long because its causes were layered. A treaty could settle one issue but leave others unresolved. A battlefield victory could create leverage but not durable control. A dynastic claim could be suspended but not forgotten.

The war kept returning because the underlying problems remained: English landholding in France, French royal centralization, competing legal claims, aristocratic politics, and the immense prestige of kingship.

### 3 — Why Did England Win So Many Early Victories?

**Q: Why was England able to defeat larger French armies?**

England’s early battlefield success came from a combination of tactical discipline, defensive positioning, and effective use of archers. Battles such as **Crécy** in 1346 and **Poitiers** in 1356 showed that English armies could defeat larger French forces when the French attacked prepared positions.

The famous **longbow** mattered, but it should not be treated as a magic weapon. English success depended on the whole tactical system: dismounted men-at-arms, archers placed on the flanks, defensive ground, stakes or natural protection, and disciplined command. French cavalry and noble forces often attacked in disorder, reducing their own numerical advantage.

**Q: What happened at Crécy?**

At **Crécy**, Edward III’s army took a strong defensive position. The French attacked in waves, but their forces were poorly coordinated. Genoese crossbowmen were ineffective under difficult conditions, and French mounted nobles pushed forward into a killing zone shaped by English archery and terrain.

The battle was devastating for the French aristocracy. It showed that noble cavalry charges could fail badly against disciplined defensive formations. It also gave Edward III enormous prestige and opened the way for the English siege and capture of **Calais**.

**Q: Why was Calais important?**

The capture of **Calais** in 1347 gave England a fortified port on the French coast. This was more valuable than a single battlefield victory. Calais became a bridgehead, a military base, a commercial center, and a symbol of English power on the continent.

For later English campaigns, Calais was crucial. It allowed English armies to enter northern France more easily and gave the crown a durable strategic foothold. England would hold Calais long after losing most other French possessions.

**Q: What happened at Poitiers?**

At **Poitiers** in 1356, the **Black Prince**, Edward III’s son, defeated the army of **John II of France**. More importantly, the French king himself was captured. This was a catastrophic political event.

In medieval warfare, noble prisoners were valuable because they could be ransomed. But when the prisoner was a king, the consequences were enormous. France now had to raise money for ransom, manage government without the king, and survive a legitimacy crisis. Poitiers was therefore not just a military defeat. It was a constitutional and fiscal crisis for the French monarchy.

**Q: What was the Treaty of Brétigny?**

The **Treaty of Brétigny** in 1360 ended the first major phase of the war. It gave England enlarged control over territories in southwestern France and imposed heavy ransom obligations for John II. In return, Edward III was supposed to give up his claim to the French crown.

But the settlement was unstable. It did not fully resolve the question of sovereignty. Was Aquitaine truly independent under the English king, or still connected to French royal jurisdiction? That ambiguity later reopened the conflict.

### 4 — How Did France Recover?

**Q: Why did France not collapse after Crécy and Poitiers?**

France survived because military defeat did not destroy the French monarchy’s long-term resources. France had a larger population, deeper territorial reserves, and a stronger base for eventual recovery. More importantly, under **Charles V**, the French crown changed strategy.

Instead of seeking immediate revenge through large set-piece battles, Charles V used a more cautious method. He avoided fighting the English under conditions that favored English tactics. He relied on commanders such as **Bertrand du Guesclin**, local reconquest, diplomacy, attrition, and pressure on isolated English positions.

**Q: What was different about Charles V’s strategy?**

Charles V understood that England’s greatest victories came when French armies attacked prepared English defensive positions. So he avoided repeating that mistake. French forces harassed, isolated, negotiated, and gradually recovered territory.

This strategy was less dramatic than Crécy or Poitiers, but it worked. It treated war as a problem of administration and endurance, not only battlefield honor. The French crown slowly reversed many English gains from the Treaty of Brétigny.

**Q: Why was the Battle of La Rochelle important?**

The naval defeat of the English off **La Rochelle** in 1372 was crucial because Aquitaine depended on maritime connection with England. If England could not reliably supply and reinforce its southwestern territories, its position weakened.

The French alliance with **Castile** helped shift the naval balance. This shows that the war was not only Anglo-French. Scotland, Castile, Burgundy, Flanders, and other powers all affected the outcome.

**Q: Did France fully recover in the late fourteenth century?**

Not completely. Charles V’s recovery was impressive, but it did not end the war. After his death in 1380, France entered a period of instability. The new king, **Charles VI**, eventually suffered severe mental illness, and royal authority weakened.

This opened the way for factional conflict between the **Burgundians** and **Armagnacs**. That French civil war would later become one of the main reasons England was able to return so powerfully under **Henry V**.

### 5 — How Did Civil War in France Change Everything?

**Q: Why did France become internally divided?**

The crisis came from the weakness of royal authority under **Charles VI**. Because the king was often unable to rule effectively, powerful princes competed to control the government, the royal treasury, Paris, and the direction of war policy.

The two major factions were the **Burgundians**, led by the dukes of Burgundy, and the **Armagnacs**, associated with the supporters of the duke of Orléans and later Bernard of Armagnac. Their conflict became violent and destabilizing.

**Q: Why was the murder of Louis of Orléans so important?**

In 1407, **Louis of Orléans** was assassinated on the orders of **John the Fearless**, duke of Burgundy. This turned elite rivalry into open civil conflict. The murder destroyed trust between factions and made reconciliation difficult.

France’s internal division gave England an opportunity. A united France had already shown under Charles V that it could resist and reverse English gains. A divided France could be exploited.

**Q: Why did Burgundy matter so much?**

**Burgundy** was not just a noble faction. It controlled major territories and had deep connections to **Flanders**, one of Europe’s richest commercial regions. Burgundian support could make English power in northern France politically viable.

When Burgundy cooperated with England, the English could present themselves not merely as invaders but as allies in a French civil conflict. When Burgundy later reconciled with Charles VII, English occupation became much harder to sustain.

**Q: How did Henry V use French division?**

**Henry V** understood that France was vulnerable. He revived the English claim to the French crown and launched a major invasion. His war was not only about recovering old lands. It was about forcing a dynastic settlement that would make the king of England heir to France.

French civil war made this possible. Without Burgundian hostility toward the Armagnac-Valois party, Henry’s project would have been far more difficult.

### 6 — What Did Henry V Achieve?

**Q: Why did Henry V invade France?**

Henry V invaded France in 1415 for several reasons. He wanted to assert the English claim to the French crown, recover prestige, strengthen the Lancastrian dynasty, and redirect English aristocratic energy outward. His father, Henry IV, had taken the English throne by deposition, so the Lancastrian dynasty needed legitimacy.

A successful French war could connect Henry V to the heroic memory of Edward III. It could also make him appear as a lawful warrior-king rather than merely the son of a usurper.

**Q: What happened at Harfleur?**

Henry began by besieging **Harfleur**, a port in Normandy. The siege succeeded, but it was costly. Disease weakened the English army, and the campaign lost time. Henry then marched toward Calais with a reduced and exhausted force.

This march was dangerous. The French gathered to intercept him, and the result was the Battle of **Agincourt**.

**Q: Why was Agincourt so famous?**

At **Agincourt** in 1415, Henry V’s smaller English army defeated a much larger French force. The English used defensive positioning, archers, dismounted men-at-arms, and muddy terrain to neutralize French numerical superiority.

The battle became one of England’s most famous victories. But like Crécy, it should not be explained only by the longbow. French overcrowding, mud, poor coordination, narrow terrain, and English discipline all mattered.

**Q: Did Agincourt immediately give Henry control of France?**

No. Agincourt was a spectacular victory, but battles alone do not govern kingdoms. Henry had to return and conquer territory systematically. His later campaigns in **Normandy** were more important for actual control.

The capture of **Rouen** in 1419 was especially significant. Rouen was the chief city of Normandy. Once Henry controlled it, he could claim to be building a real territorial regime, not merely raiding.

**Q: What was the Treaty of Troyes?**

The **Treaty of Troyes** in 1420 was the high point of Henry V’s success. It recognized Henry as heir to **Charles VI of France**, arranged his marriage to **Catherine of Valois**, and excluded the Dauphin, the future **Charles VII**, from the succession.

In theory, the treaty created a future dual monarchy: one ruler would govern both England and France. In practice, it was fragile. It depended on English military power, Burgundian cooperation, and the willingness of French communities to accept the disinheritance of the Dauphin.

**Q: Why did Henry V’s project fail after his death?**

Henry V died in 1422, before Charles VI. When Charles VI also died soon afterward, Henry’s infant son, **Henry VI**, was proclaimed king of both England and France by the English and their allies.

But an infant could not personally command armies, negotiate alliances, or embody conquest the way Henry V had. The English regime depended on regents, especially the capable **duke of Bedford**, but the structure was fragile. It required constant military success and Burgundian support.

### 7 — Why Was Joan of Arc So Important?

**Q: What was the situation before Joan appeared?**

By the late 1420s, the position of **Charles VII** was dangerous. The English and Burgundians controlled much of northern France. Paris was not in his hands. His legitimacy was damaged by the Treaty of Troyes, which had disinherited him.

The siege of **Orléans** became critical. If Orléans fell, the English could threaten the Loire region and push deeper into the territory still loyal to Charles. The city became both a military and symbolic crisis.

**Q: What did Joan of Arc actually do?**

**Joan of Arc** helped transform the morale and political meaning of the French cause. She arrived claiming a divine mission to support Charles and drive out the English. Her presence helped inspire the defenders of Orléans and encouraged offensive action.

She did not replace generals, logistics, or military planning. Her importance lay in the interaction between charisma, religious belief, political legitimacy, and military momentum. She made Charles’s cause feel providential at a moment when it was in danger.

**Q: Why was the relief of Orléans a turning point?**

The lifting of the siege in 1429 changed the psychological balance of the war. The English were no longer invincible. Charles VII’s cause was no longer merely surviving; it was advancing.

The French victory at **Patay** soon afterward further damaged English prestige. Unlike Agincourt, where the English had time to prepare a strong defensive position, Patay caught English forces before they could properly deploy. It showed that the famous English tactical system could fail under different conditions.

**Q: Why did the coronation at Reims matter?**

Joan pushed Charles to go to **Reims**, the traditional coronation city of French kings. This mattered enormously. The Treaty of Troyes had tried to deny Charles’s legitimacy. Coronation at Reims publicly restored it.

After Charles VII was anointed, he was no longer merely a disputed claimant. He was a consecrated king in the traditional French royal sense. That did not end the war, but it changed the legitimacy structure of the conflict.

**Q: Why did the English put Joan on trial?**

After Joan was captured in 1430, she was eventually handed over to the English and tried at **Rouen**. The trial was not only about religion. It was political.

If Joan could be condemned as heretical or fraudulent, then the English could attack the legitimacy of Charles VII’s coronation. Her trial was therefore a judicial weapon against Valois kingship. She was executed in 1431, but her death did not reverse the recovery she had helped set in motion.

### 8 — Why Did England Lose?

**Q: What broke the English position in France?**

The English position depended on several fragile conditions: military success, Burgundian alliance, money, garrisons, administrative control, and local cooperation. Over time, these conditions weakened.

The decisive diplomatic shift came with the **Treaty of Arras** in 1435, when **Burgundy reconciled with Charles VII**. This broke the Anglo-Burgundian alliance. Without Burgundy, the English were increasingly isolated in France.

**Q: Why was the loss of Paris important?**

Paris returned to Charles VII in 1436. This was symbolically and politically devastating for the English. Henry VI had been crowned in Paris, but Charles VII had been crowned at Reims. Once Paris returned to Valois control, the English claim to govern France looked far less credible.

The English still held Normandy, Gascony, and Calais, but their dream of ruling France through a dual monarchy was collapsing.

**Q: How did Charles VII strengthen France?**

Charles VII’s later success depended on reform. The French crown developed more permanent military forces, especially the **compagnies d’ordonnance**. It improved artillery, strengthened taxation, and brought armed companies more directly under royal control.

This was crucial. France did not win merely because of Joan of Arc or because England became weak. France won because the monarchy became more capable of sustained war.

**Q: Why did Normandy fall?**

In 1449–1450, Charles VII launched a campaign to recover **Normandy**. French forces used improved organization, artillery, and coordinated operations. The English defeat at **Formigny** in 1450 broke their field power in Normandy.

Once English armies could no longer relieve garrisons, towns and fortresses fell. Normandy, the great achievement of Henry V’s conquest, returned to French control.

**Q: Why was Castillon the end?**

The final major battle was **Castillon** in 1453. **John Talbot**, earl of Shrewsbury, attempted to recover English power in Gascony, but he attacked a fortified French artillery position. The English were defeated, and Talbot was killed.

Castillon symbolized the end of the old English advantage. Earlier English victories had depended on prepared defensive archery tactics. Now the French had artillery, better organization, and stronger state support. After Bordeaux surrendered again, English continental power was effectively reduced to **Calais**.

### 9 — What Did the War Change?

**Q: Did the Hundred Years’ War create the modern state?**

Not by itself. But it strongly accelerated state formation, especially in France. The war forced rulers to raise taxes, organize armies, manage garrisons, negotiate with elites, and control military violence.

By the end of the war, the French monarchy had become stronger than it had been at the beginning. Permanent taxation and more regular military organization gave the crown greater power over the kingdom.

**Q: What changed in warfare?**

The war showed that aristocratic cavalry alone could not dominate battlefields. English longbow tactics were devastating under the right conditions, but they were not unbeatable. French commanders eventually learned to avoid those conditions.

By the final phase, artillery and siege warfare became increasingly important. Castles and towns still mattered, but artillery made it easier for a well-funded crown to reduce fortifications. War became more dependent on state resources, not only noble valor.

**Q: What happened to England after defeat?**

England’s defeat damaged the prestige of the **Lancastrian monarchy**. The loss of France did not alone cause the **Wars of the Roses**, but it contributed to political instability. English nobles blamed weak royal government, financial mismanagement, and failed policy.

Henry VI was a weak king, and the collapse of English power in France intensified domestic crisis. The war that Henry V had used to strengthen Lancastrian legitimacy eventually helped undermine it.

**Q: What happened to France after victory?**

France emerged stronger, but not peacefully unified. The monarchy gained power, yet regional privileges, noble resistance, and Burgundian strength remained important. Victory over England was also a victory for royal centralization.

The war helped France move toward a more territorially coherent monarchy. But that process involved coercion, taxation, negotiation, and internal conflict. It was not simply a romantic story of national liberation.

**Q: What is the central historical lesson of the Hundred Years’ War?**

The Hundred Years’ War began as a medieval dispute over feudal rights, dynastic succession, and territorial lordship. It ended in a world where taxation, artillery, permanent armies, royal administration, and territorial sovereignty mattered far more.

Its deepest significance is this transition. The war began in the language of **homage** and **inheritance**. It ended in the language of **state capacity**, **military organization**, and **centralized monarchy**. That is why it is one of the major turning points between the medieval and early modern political worlds.
