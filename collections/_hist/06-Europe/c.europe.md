## Prehistory and Early Civilizations (c. 45,000 BCE–800 BCE)

* c. 45,000–30,000 BCE — Upper Paleolithic cultures spread across Europe (art, tools, long-distance exchange).
* c. 7000–4000 BCE — Neolithic farming spreads from Anatolia/Balkans across Europe; permanent settlements expand.
* c. 3300 BCE — Earliest writing in the Mediterranean world (not Europe proper, but foundational to later European literate civilizations).
* c. 3000–1200 BCE — Bronze Age societies across Europe; long-range trade networks (tin, copper, amber).
* c. 1600–1100 BCE — Mycenaean Greece (palatial centers; early Greek).
* c. 1200–1150 BCE — Late Bronze Age collapse in the Eastern Mediterranean; reconfigures Aegean and Near Eastern powers.
* c. 800 BCE — Greek polis (city-state) formation accelerates; Mediterranean colonization begins.

## Classical Greece and the Hellenistic World (c. 800–146 BCE)

* 776 BCE — Traditional date of the first Olympic Games.
* c. 750–550 BCE — Greek colonization around the Mediterranean and Black Sea; spreads language, institutions, trade.
* 508/507 BCE — Athenian democratic reforms (Cleisthenes).
* 490–479 BCE — Greco-Persian Wars; major Greek victories (Marathon, Salamis, Plataea).
* 431–404 BCE — Peloponnesian War; Athens defeated; long-term destabilization.
* 399 BCE — Execution of Socrates; emblematic for classical philosophy and politics.
* 336–323 BCE — Alexander the Great; Greek culture spreads across a vast empire.
* 323–146 BCE — Hellenistic kingdoms; new political forms, science, and urban cultures.
* 146 BCE — Rome defeats Corinth; Greece absorbed into Roman sphere.

## Roman Republic and Roman Empire (c. 509 BCE–476 CE in the West)

* 509 BCE — Traditional founding of the Roman Republic.
* 264–146 BCE — Punic Wars; Rome becomes dominant Mediterranean power.
* 133–31 BCE — Crisis of the Republic (civil wars; social and political upheaval).
* 44 BCE — Assassination of Julius Caesar.
* 27 BCE — Augustus establishes the Principate; start of Roman Empire (conventionally).
* 1st–2nd c. CE — “Pax Romana”; imperial integration, infrastructure, urban growth.
* 64 CE — Great Fire of Rome; early persecutions in imperial narratives.
* 212 CE — Caracalla grants Roman citizenship broadly (Constitutio Antoniniana).
* 3rd c. CE — “Crisis of the Third Century”; fragmentation and instability.
* 313 CE — Edict of Milan: toleration of Christianity (Constantine era).
* 330 CE — Constantinople founded as imperial capital.
* 380 CE — Christianity becomes the official religion of the Roman Empire (Theodosius I).
* 395 CE — Administrative division between Western and Eastern empires becomes durable.
* 410 CE — Sack of Rome by Visigoths (symbolic shock).
* 476 CE — Deposition of Romulus Augustulus; conventional end of Western Roman Empire.

## Late Antiquity, Byzantium, and the Early Middle Ages (c. 476–1000)

* 5th–7th c. — Germanic successor kingdoms in the West (Franks, Visigoths, Ostrogoths, etc.).
* 527–565 — Justinian’s reign (Byzantine reconquests; codification of Roman law).
* 6th c. — Justinianic Plague contributes to demographic and economic stress.
* 622–750 — Islamic expansion reshapes Mediterranean political and economic geography.
* 711 — Umayyad conquest of much of Iberia.
* 732 — Battle of Tours/Poitiers (later mythologized; complex historical significance).
* 800 — Charlemagne crowned Emperor; Carolingian “renovation” of imperial idea.
* 843 — Treaty of Verdun divides Carolingian empire; seeds later France/Germany trajectories.
* 9th–10th c. — Viking expansion and settlement (British Isles, Normandy, Rus’ routes).
* 955 — Otto I defeats Magyars at Lechfeld; consolidation of East Frankish/German power.
* 962 — “Holy Roman Empire” (Ottonian imperial coronation tradition).

## High Middle Ages (c. 1000–1300)

* 1054 — Great Schism (conventional date for East–West ecclesiastical rupture).
* 1066 — Norman Conquest of England.
* 1095–1291 — Crusades; Latin kingdoms in the Levant; long-term interreligious and political consequences.
* 12th c. — Growth of universities (Bologna, Paris, Oxford traditions); scholasticism.
* 1215 — Magna Carta; evolving constraints on royal authority in England.
* 1240s — Mongol invasions reach Eastern/Central Europe; political shock and military adaptation.
* 1273–1300 — Consolidation of major monarchies and late medieval state-building begins to accelerate.

## Late Middle Ages and Renaissance Foundations (c. 1300–1500)

* 1309–1377 — Avignon Papacy; later Great Western Schism (1378–1417).
* 1337–1453 — Hundred Years’ War; military, fiscal, and national-political transformations.
* 1347–1351 — Black Death; massive demographic shock; labor relations and social order altered.
* c. 1450 — Gutenberg printing revolution; accelerates diffusion of texts and controversy.
* 1453 — Fall of Constantinople to the Ottomans; major geopolitical shift.
* 1492 — Completion of Reconquista (Granada) and Columbus’s voyage; Europe’s global expansion era intensifies.
* Late 14th–15th c. — Renaissance humanism (Italy → broader Europe); new philology, art, patronage systems.

## Reformation, Confessional Europe, and State Formation (c. 1500–1648)

* 1517 — Martin Luther’s challenge; Protestant Reformation begins to institutionalize.
* 1520s–1560s — Spread of Lutheran and Reformed traditions; Catholic Counter-Reformation.
* 1543 — Copernican publication; emblematic of the Scientific Revolution’s early phase.
* 1555 — Peace of Augsburg (“cuius regio, eius religio”) formalizes confessional politics.
* 1588 — Defeat of the Spanish Armada; shifting naval power perceptions.
* 1618–1648 — Thirty Years’ War; demographic and political devastation in parts of Central Europe.
* 1648 — Peace of Westphalia; modern diplomatic order and state sovereignty narratives consolidate.

## Absolutism, Enlightenment, and Global Competition (c. 1648–1789)

* 1683 — Ottoman defeat at Vienna; Ottoman–Habsburg balance changes.
* 1688–1689 — Glorious Revolution in England; parliamentary sovereignty tradition strengthens.
* 1701–1714 — War of the Spanish Succession; European balance-of-power diplomacy.
* 1713 — Treaty of Utrecht; major settlement shaping 18th-century geopolitics.
* 1756–1763 — Seven Years’ War; a truly global war with decisive European implications.
* 18th c. — Enlightenment; new political economy, public sphere, critique of absolutism.
* 1776–1783 — American Revolution; strong ideological reverberations in Europe.

## Revolutionary and Napoleonic Europe (1789–1815)

* 1789 — French Revolution begins; sovereignty redefined, mass politics escalates.
* 1792–1802 — Revolutionary wars; Europe-wide conflict.
* 1799 — Napoleon’s rise (coup); authoritarian consolidation after revolutionary turbulence.
* 1804 — Napoleon crowned Emperor; export of legal/administrative reforms and warfare.
* 1812 — Napoleon’s invasion of Russia; turning point.
* 1814–1815 — Congress of Vienna; European order and conservative restoration.
* 1815 — Waterloo; end of Napoleonic Wars.

## Industrialization, Nationalism, and Mass Politics (1815–1914)

* 1820s–1840s — Industrial Revolution accelerates (Britain → Western/Central Europe); urbanization and class politics.
* 1830 — July Revolution (France); waves of liberal-national movements.
* 1848 — Revolutions across Europe; liberalism, nationalism, labor politics; most suppressed but transformative.
* 1853–1856 — Crimean War; reveals military/administrative weaknesses; press and public opinion grow.
* 1861 — Kingdom of Italy proclaimed (unification process).
* 1867 — Austro-Hungarian Compromise; Dual Monarchy.
* 1870–1871 — Franco-Prussian War; German unification (1871); shift in continental power.
* 1880s–1914 — “New imperialism”; European empires expand; rivalries sharpen.
* 1905 — Russian Revolution of 1905; constitutional experiments and repression.
* 1912–1913 — Balkan Wars; destabilize Southeast Europe.

## World Wars and Europe’s Reordering (1914–1945)

* 1914–1918 — World War I; collapse of empires (German, Austro-Hungarian, Ottoman, Russian).
* 1917 — Russian Revolutions; Bolshevik takeover; civil war and the USSR’s emergence.
* 1919 — Treaty of Versailles and associated settlements; disputed legitimacy and revisionism.
* 1929 — Great Depression; political radicalization and state intervention intensify.
* 1933 — Nazi seizure of power in Germany.
* 1936–1939 — Spanish Civil War; ideological proxy conflict.
* 1939–1945 — World War II; Holocaust; total war and postwar division.
* 1945 — Europe devastated; US–Soviet bipolar order crystallizes.

## Cold War Europe and Integration (1945–1991)

* 1947–1948 — Marshall Plan; Western reconstruction and Cold War alignment.
* 1949 — NATO founded; collective defense framework.
* 1951 — European Coal and Steel Community (integration begins).
* 1957 — Treaties of Rome create the EEC; deeper economic integration.
* 1961 — Berlin Wall built; symbol of division.
* 1968 — Prague Spring and its suppression; limits of reform in Soviet bloc.
* 1973 — Oil crisis; stagflation; economic restructuring.
* 1975–1979 — Democratic transitions in Southern Europe (Portugal, Spain, Greece).
* 1989 — Fall of communist regimes in Eastern Europe; Berlin Wall falls.
* 1991 — Soviet Union dissolves; Cold War ends.

## Post–Cold War Europe (1991–2019)

* 1991–2001 — Yugoslav wars; ethnic cleansing; NATO interventions; new Balkan states.
* 1992–1993 — Maastricht Treaty establishes the European Union (EU).
* 1999/2002 — Euro introduced (accounting then cash); monetary union deepens.
* 2004 — Major EU enlargement (Central/Eastern Europe); continental reconfiguration.
* 2008 — Global financial crisis; severe European banking and sovereign-debt fallout.
* 2010–2012 — Eurozone sovereign debt crisis; austerity politics and institutional reforms.
* 2014 — Russia annexes Crimea; war in Donbas begins; European security order destabilized.
* 2015 — Refugee and migration crisis peaks; EU internal tensions rise.

## 2020s (2020–present)

* 2020 — The UK leaves the EU (Brexit takes effect). ([European Commission][1])
* 2020–2022 — COVID-19 reshapes economies, public health policy, and state capacity debates.
* 24 Feb 2022 — Russia launches full-scale invasion of Ukraine. ([Al Jazeera][2])
* 4 Apr 2023 — Finland becomes a full NATO member. ([北大西洋条約機構][3])
* 7 Mar 2024 — Sweden becomes a full NATO member. ([北大西洋条約機構][4])
* 2022–present — War in Ukraine continues; recurrent major strikes and continued European security/economic effects. ([Reuters][5])
