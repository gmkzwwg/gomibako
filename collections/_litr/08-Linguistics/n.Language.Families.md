## Executive summary

The world’s languages are best compared along a small set of typological dimensions that interact with genealogy, contact, and cultural–interactional norms. Genealogical classification (e.g., “Indo-European”) is a historical claim about descent; typology (e.g., “agglutinative morphology”) is a structural profile; and areal patterns (e.g., Mainland Southeast Asia’s tone–classifier–serial-verb complex) often cut across genealogical boundaries. Any family-level “characteristic feature” is therefore a statistical tendency with substantial internal diversity, not a deterministic property. 1

A productive comparative frame is to track how languages distribute grammatical work across (i) phonology (tone, phonation/register, consonant/vowel inventories), (ii) morphology (fusion and exponence; synthesis vs. analyticity), (iii) syntax (basic word order, alignment, head/dependent marking), and (iv) discourse/pragmatics (information-structure marking, evidentiality, honorifics, reference tracking). These dimensions have clear consequences for learnability: the “bottleneck” differs sharply between, for example, tonal–analytic systems (lexical tone + particle grammar), polysynthetic systems (morphological parsing + templatic constraints), and noun-class systems (agreement patterns + derivational morphology). 2

The notes below profile the families requested (plus several widely recognized additional families/areas), with dense family-by-family sketches and pedagogical implications. Interlinear glosses follow the Leipzig Glossing Rules; examples are illustrative and are explicitly not intended as representing every branch within a family. 3

## Sources and analytic framework

These notes rely on three complementary “reference layers”:

1.  Genealogy and macro-distribution: Glottolog for conservative family/dialect classification and bibliographic anchoring (current release indicated on its landing page and Zenodo metadata). 4
    
2.  Typological features: The World Atlas of Language Structures Online for cross-linguistic feature definitions and global distributions (e.g., tone; S/O/V order; fusion; exponence). 5
    
3.  Phonological inventories and comparability: PHOIBLE (release metadata and summary statistics) for inventories; and CLTS for cross-system transcription linking. 6
    

For language counts and “largest families,” Ethnologue provides an alternative counting methodology and current estimates of living languages and large families (noting that counting depends on language/dialect decisions and is “in flux”). 7

Analytic primitives used (high-level reminders, not exhaustive):

*   Fusion and exponence (morphology): isolating vs concatenative vs non-linear; monoexponential vs cumulative markers. 2
*   Tone: lexical vs grammatical tone; simple vs complex tone. 8
*   Clause word order: dominant SOV/SVO/VSO etc, with the well-known caveat that “dominant order” abstracts away from pragmatically conditioned alternations and from pronominal clitic placement. 9
*   Alignment and marking: nominative–accusative vs ergative–absolutive vs splits; head-marking vs dependent-marking; special systems such as direct–inverse and syntactic ergativity. 10
*   Evidentiality: grammaticalized information source, distinct from (though often interacting with) epistemic modality. 11
*   Interlinear glossing conventions: Leipzig glossing principles (abbreviation discipline, segmentation conventions). 3

## Africa and West Eurasia family profiles

### Indo-European

Genealogical scope and distribution: very large family with worldwide spread via historical expansions; Glottolog notes “more than 500” languages (and provides fine-grained subgrouping). 12

Representative languages (high-frequency/representative for learning): English, Spanish, French, Russian, Hindi/Urdu, Bengali, Persian, German, Greek.

Phonological profile: typically stress/pitch-accent systems rather than lexical tone (tone exists in some branches via contact or internal development but is not dominant); consonant/vowel inventories vary widely; many languages show strong morphophonological alternations tied to inflection (ablaut, consonant alternations). 8

Morphological typology: historically rich inflection with fusional/cumulative markers is reconstructible for Proto-Indo-European; many modern branches show partial analytic drift (loss of case, periphrastic tense/aspect, reliance on prepositions). The “case+number cumulative endings” archetype is explicitly noted as a familiar instance of polyexponence in typological summaries. 13

Core syntax: historically SOV is widely argued for older stages in several branches; modern Indo-European exhibits both SOV (e.g., much of Indo-Iranian) and SVO (much of Romance/Germanic) with robust drift and cycling across time. 9

Key grammatical categories: tense–aspect systems vary; agreement (person/number; sometimes gender) is common; case marking ranges from extensive (Slavic) to minimal (English), shifting the functional burden onto word order and prepositions. 13

Discourse/pragmatics: information structure is often managed through prosody, clefting, and word-order alternations; many languages have politeness systems but typically not the grammatically regimented multi-level addressee systems of Koreanic/Japonic. (This is a comparative tendency, not a universal.) 9

Learning implications:  
A common bottleneck is paradigmatic compression: many categories can be fused into single affixes; learners benefit from “paradigm-first” routing (declensions/conjugations) plus exposure-based automation to reduce retrieval cost. WALS’s exponence/fusion distinctions supply a useful metalinguistic lens for designing drills: isolate the categories that are cumulatively realized in one formative. 2

### Afro-Asiatic

Genealogical scope and distribution: macro-family across North Africa, the Horn of Africa, and Southwest Asia; Glottolog treats membership questions conservatively (e.g., explicit notes on Omotic membership debates). 14

Representative languages: Arabic, Hebrew, Amharic, Hausa, Somali, Berber varieties, Oromo.

Phonological profile: often characterized by emphatic/pharyngeal consonants in Semitic and several branches; consonant-heavy roots in Semitic interact with templatic vocalism. 15

Morphological typology: Semitic is the classic case of non-concatenative “root-and-pattern” morphology; this is well treated in theoretical accounts of nonconcatenative morphology and in Afroasiatic typological overviews. Other branches show more concatenative morphology; the family is typologically diverse. 15

Core syntax: word order varies substantially (VSO, SVO, SOV), and historical stages show shifts; the family cannot be summarized by a single “basic order.” 15

Key grammatical categories: grammatical gender is widespread; rich verbal morphology (person/number/gender; aspect) is common in Semitic; Chadic and Cushitic display diverse TAM and derivational patterns. 15

Discourse/pragmatics: definiteness, agreement, and clause linkage strategies vary by branch; the typologically salient contribution—relative to many other families—is the strong morphological packaging (including nonlinearity) for lexical and inflectional formation in Semitic. 16

Learning implications:  
The common learner bottleneck in Semitic-type systems is mapping between consonantal roots, patterns, and surface vocalism (lexeme families), plus orthography–phonology interfaces (e.g., abjad writing). Pedagogically, “root families” + pattern practice yields higher leverage than isolated word memorization, because the morphology is productivity-rich and information-dense. 16

### Niger-Congo (macro-phylum; internal scope contested) and the Atlantic-Congo core

Genealogical scope and distribution: Niger-Congo is commonly proposed as the major sub-Saharan phylum, but internal subgrouping and even membership of some branches remain disputed in specialist literature. Current conservative practice in Glottolog emphasizes demonstrated families such as Atlantic-Congo and explicitly flags groups not yet shown to share key diagnostics (e.g., notes on Ijoid not yet shown to have systematic correspondences with “Niger-Congo in some form”). 17

Representative languages (high speaker relevance): Swahili (Bantu), Yoruba, Igbo, Zulu, Shona, Wolof, Fula, Akan.

Phonological profile: tone is widespread and can be lexically and grammatically functional; tonal morphology is documented in several subgroups (including Mande, often treated separately or as an early-branching group in Niger-Congo discussions). 8

Morphological typology: strong presence of noun-class / gender-like agreement systems in much of Atlantic-Congo (especially Bantu), including complex interactions between agreement classes (“gender”) and nominal form classes (“deriflection”). Contemporary analyses stress that “noun class” is not a monolithic construct and propose more articulated models for what is being tracked. 17

Core syntax: SVO is common across many well-described branches; head-marking vs dependent-marking varies, but agreement systems can be extensive, distributing information across the clause. 17

Key grammatical categories: noun-class agreement; derivational verb extensions in many branches; TAM systems vary; pronominal systems often interact with discourse tracking (topic continuity) through agreement and cliticization. 17

Discourse/pragmatics: reference tracking can be mediated by agreement and nominal class markers; tonal phrasing can carry pragmatic contrasts. 18

Learning implications:  
For Atlantic-Congo languages with noun classes, the central pedagogical move is to learn agreement as a system (a small number of recurrent concord patterns across determiners/adjectives/verbs), rather than as isolated affixes per lexical item. For tonal systems, a parallel “tone-first” discipline is necessary: tone should be memorized as part of the lexical entry and practiced in sentence prosody early. 17

### Uralic

Genealogical scope and distribution: medium-size family across Northern Eurasia; Glottolog lists Uralic with 49 languages and detailed internal subgrouping. 19

Representative languages: Finnish, Hungarian, Estonian, Sámi varieties.

Phonological profile: vowel harmony is prominent in several branches (not uniform across the family); consonant gradation and rich morphophonology in Finnic are typologically salient. 19

Morphological typology: strongly agglutinative case-rich systems are common, with long suffix chains; negative auxiliaries appear in Finnic, an important “functional unusualness” for learners from Indo-European backgrounds. 19

Core syntax: historically and typologically, Uralic includes both SOV and SVO languages with flexible order supported by case marking; head-directionality tends to be postpositional (again with exceptions and change). 19

Key grammatical categories: numerous local cases (especially Finnic), possessive suffixes in many languages, derivational morphology, and information-structural alternations supported by word order. 19

Discourse/pragmatics: topic/focus is often mediated by word order and particles; the “grammar of information” is frequently realized through case and constituent ordering rather than obligatory agreement paradigms. 9

Learning implications:  
The bottleneck is morphological parsing and suffix stacking. High-return pedagogy: train “morpheme boundary perception” early (segmentation drills) and treat harmony as a predictive constraint that reduces memorization, not as an irregularity. 2

## South and Mainland Southeast Asia profiles

### Dravidian

Genealogical scope and distribution: South Asian family; Glottolog lists 82 languages and internal subgrouping. 20

Representative languages: Tamil, Telugu, Kannada, Malayalam.

Phonological profile: retroflex consonants are typologically salient; relatively rich consonant inventories are common; prosody is typically stress-based rather than lexical tone. 21

Morphological typology: strong agglutination, extensive suffixal morphology, productive case marking, and robust verb inflectional morphology. 21

Core syntax: strong SOV tendency with postpositions; complex predicate formation via auxiliaries and participles is common. 21

Key grammatical categories: rich case; TAM systems often with participial constructions; clusivity in some languages; extensive derivation. 21

Discourse/pragmatics: discourse particles and word-order choices support topic/focus; honorifics exist but are generally less morphologically stratified than in Koreanic/Japonic. 22

Learning implications:  
Learners benefit from an “SOV + suffix stack” pipeline: master postpositions/case early, then move to TAM-as-participle logic. Because morphology is relatively concatenative, morpheme-by-morpheme decoding is tractable once a finite set of suffixes is internalized. 2

### Austroasiatic

Genealogical scope and distribution: large family across Mainland Southeast Asia and adjacent regions; Glottolog treats Austroasiatic as a top-level family with extensive internal branching (often summarized as 100+ languages; Glottolog-linked resources show 158 in some enumerations). 23

Representative languages: Vietnamese (Vietic), Khmer (Khmeric), Mon, Santali (Munda).

Phonological profile: strong diversity; Vietic shows tone and complex syllable structure in many varieties, while Khmer is classically non-tonal but exhibits register/phonation contrasts and complex consonant clusters; thus “tone” is not a family-wide invariant. 24

Morphological typology: ranges from analytic isolating profiles (Vietnamese) to more affixal/derivational morphology in other branches; typological overview work emphasizes diversity rather than a single template. 24

Core syntax: SVO common in many Mainland Southeast Asian Austroasiatic languages; clause chaining and serial-verb-like constructions occur areally across families. 9

Key grammatical categories: classifiers frequently occur in the analytic branches; TAM often realized via particles/serial constructions; rich systems of voice/valency alternations exist in some branches. 24

Discourse/pragmatics: strong areal convergence with Tai-Kadai and Sinitic in Mainland Southeast Asia (topic prominence, sentence-final particles, pragmatically conditioned alternations). 25

Learning implications:  
Two distinct pedagogical regimes are needed depending on the target language: (i) Vietnamese-like: tone + syllable lexicon + particle grammar; (ii) Khmer-like: complex consonant clusters and register contrasts plus a different functional distribution of morphology. Family labels do not predict the primary difficulty; areal phonological profile often does. 8

### Tai-Kadai (Kra–Dai)

Genealogical scope and distribution: Southeast Asia and southern China; Glottolog provides detailed membership notes (including explicit discussion of problematic cases such as Jiamao) and mapping. 26

Representative languages: Thai, Lao, Shan, Zhuang.

Phonological profile: tone is nearly universal across the family; syllable structure tends toward monosyllabic roots with compound/serial constructions; phonological alternations interact with tone splits historically. 8

Morphological typology: predominantly analytic/isolating; grammar is realized by particles, word order, and serial verbs rather than inflectional morphology. 2

Core syntax: SVO dominant; serial verb constructions and clause chaining are common in the Mainland Southeast Asia area regardless of family. 9

Key grammatical categories: classifiers are central; tense is often weakly grammaticalized relative to aspect/modality particles; evidentiality is typically lexical/particle-based rather than heavy inflectional paradigms. 25

Discourse/pragmatics: sentence-final particles and pragmatic markers are crucial; politeness is realized through particles and lexical choices rather than systematic honorific agreement. 25

Learning implications:  
For many learners, the bottleneck is tonal lexicalization plus pragmatic particle mastery. Accuracy improves when tones are trained as part of minimal-pair lexical categories and practiced in connected speech early (tone sandhi, phrasing). 8

### Hmong-Mien

Genealogical scope and distribution: southern China and Southeast Asia; Glottolog provides family anchoring and points to core classificatory references (e.g., Ratliff). 27

Representative languages: Hmong Daw (White Hmong), Hmong Njua (Green Hmong), Iu Mien.

Phonological profile: tone systems are typically complex; syllable structure and historical tone splits are central to analysis, and reconstruction work emphasizes rich onset inventories and tone-bearing units. 28

Morphological typology: often described as largely analytic/isolating, with grammatical relations encoded via particles, word order, and serial constructions; morphology is not necessarily “simple” because tone and morphophonology can carry grammatical work. 28

Core syntax: SVO common; areal convergence with Tai-Kadai and Sinitic in information-structural strategies and classifier use. 25

Key grammatical categories: classifier systems (in many varieties), aspectual particles, and rich pronominal/serial-verb strategies; extensive grammatical tone phenomena are attested in the broader area (and in some Hmong-Mien descriptions). 28

Discourse/pragmatics: pragmatic particles and topic management are central; discourse coherence can be carried by particle choice and ellipsis patterns. 25

Learning implications:  
Tone acquisition is non-negotiable; an effective curriculum treats tone as segmental (phonemic) and trains perception/production with controlled minimal sets before moving to free conversation. The second bottleneck is pragmatic particles (scope and discourse function), which demand corpus-based exposure and interactional feedback. 8

## East and North Eurasia profiles, including the Transeurasian controversy

### Sino-Tibetan (Trans-Himalayan)

Genealogical scope and distribution: very large Eurasian family; Glottolog notes “more than 500” languages. Modern phylogenetic work and handbook overviews emphasize large internal diversity and continuing debates about subgrouping. 29

Representative languages: Mandarin (Sinitic), Cantonese (Sinitic), Burmese (Lolo-Burmese), Tibetan varieties (Tibetic), Karenic languages.

Phonological profile: tone is widespread (especially in Sinitic and many Southeast Asian branches), but not uniform; syllable structure ranges from strongly monosyllabic to more complex; phonation/register contrasts occur in some languages. 8

Morphological typology: Sinitic is often analytic with derivational morphology and compounding; many non-Sinitic branches show richer agglutinative morphology and case/postpositions (SOV profiles). This family is therefore a canonical example of large typological variance within one genealogy. 30

Core syntax: SVO dominates in Sinitic; SOV is common elsewhere; topic-comment structures are prominent in Sinitic and converge areally in East/Southeast Asia. 9

Key grammatical categories: classifiers in Sinitic; aspectual particles; evidentiality and egophoricity are prominent in some Tibetic descriptions (not a family-wide invariant). 11

Discourse/pragmatics: sentence-final particles, topic chains, and pragmatic ellipsis can be central in Sinitic; stance marking in Tibetic-type systems can grammaticize information source and speaker perspective. 25

Learning implications:  
For Sinitic: tonal lexicon + aspect particles + classifier system are the high-yield targets; for SOV Tibeto-Burman branches: postpositional syntax and verbal morphology dominate. A family-aware learner should not transfer “Chinese expectations” to the whole family. 30

### Japonic

Genealogical scope and distribution: Japanese plus Ryukyuan languages (and Hachijō in many classifications); an authoritative introductory chapter provides a modern overview of subgrouping and the Japanese–Ryukyuan split. 31

Representative languages: Japanese; Okinawan (Ryukyuan); Miyako (Ryukyuan).

Phonological profile: pitch accent systems are common (especially in Japanese); syllable structure is moderate; phonotactics differ substantially across dialects and Ryukyuan languages. 31

Morphological typology: agglutinative suffixing with extensive verbal inflection and derivation; particles mark case-like roles and information-structure functions. 31

Core syntax: SOV with postpositions/particles; topic marker is structurally central to discourse grammar. 9

Key grammatical categories: rich TAM and modality; productive nominalization; politeness and honorific morphology is a major grammatical subsystem in Japanese, analyzed in both morphosyntactic and pragmatic terms. 32

Discourse/pragmatics: high grammaticalization of addressee and referent honorification (“keigo” system), which shapes not only word choice but morphosyntax; extensive particle system for stance and turn management. 32

Learning implications:  
The limiting factor is usually not basic SOV order but (i) particle semantics (especially topic vs subject vs object marking), and (ii) honorific/politeness as interactional grammar. Pedagogically, honorifics should be treated as a coherent system tied to social indexing, not as “extra vocabulary.” 33

### Koreanic

Genealogical scope and distribution: small family; Glottolog treats Koreanic as a family-level unit and provides classification. 34

Representative languages: Standard Korean; Jeju variety (often highlighted for divergence).

Phonological profile: complex laryngeal contrasts in stops; prosodic patterns differ by dialect; historical tone is documented for Middle Korean. 35

Morphological typology: agglutinative with rich verbal suffix sequencing; extensive sentence-ending morphology encodes speech level and clause type. 36

Core syntax: SOV; case particles; extensive topic/comment packaging; argument omission is frequent and pragmatically licensed. 9

Key grammatical categories: honorific agreement and speech-level marking are structurally central; pedagogical literature emphasizes the multiplicity of speech levels and interaction-driven alternation. 37

Discourse/pragmatics: honorification and politeness are not merely optional; they are grammatically encoded and used to manage footing and social stance. 37

Learning implications:  
Core difficulty: mapping social context to obligatory morphosyntax (speech levels, honorific markers). A practical strategy is to learn a minimal stable pair first (e.g., polite vs deferential styles as commonly taught in applied work), then extend. 38

### Turkic

Genealogical scope and distribution: Glottolog lists Turkic with 43 languages and a Bolgar vs Common Turkic split. 39

Representative languages: Turkish, Kazakh, Uzbek, Uyghur, Tatar.

Phonological profile: vowel harmony is a canonical family tendency (with known erosion/loss in some contact settings); stress/pitch accent interacts with suffixes. 40

Morphological typology: strongly agglutinative, suffixing, often with long but transparent morpheme chains; comparatively stable morphosyntactic core structures are emphasized in typological discussions. 40

Core syntax: strong SOV tendency with postpositions; rich non-finite clause chaining; relatively free constituent ordering under information structure. 40

Key grammatical categories: evidentiality-like categories in some languages (e.g., Turkish -miş type), extensive derivation, and participial systems. 40

Discourse/pragmatics: pragmatic word-order alternations; topic/focus often realized via scrambling and clausal particles rather than agreement. 41

Learning implications:  
A “suffix-chain parsing” approach with harmony as constraint yields fast returns: once common suffix templates are internalized, new forms become predictable. 2

### Mongolic

Genealogical scope and distribution: a well-defined family with wide Eurasian spread; English reference treatments emphasize typological regularities and areal contact. 42

Representative languages: Khalkha Mongolian, Buryat, Kalmyk, Dagur.

Phonological profile: vowel harmony and rich vowel systems in many languages; consonant inventories vary; stress and prosody are language-specific. 42

Morphological typology: agglutinative with rich case and derivational morphology; participial and converb systems support clause chaining. 42

Core syntax: strongly SOV with postpositions; head-final tendencies are common. 9

Key grammatical categories: case systems, evidential/epistemic particles, non-finite subordination. 42

Discourse/pragmatics: information structure often realized by word order and particles; contact with Turkic and Tungusic is historically important for areal typology. 42

Learning implications:  
Learners benefit from “case-map” pedagogy (spatial/relational cases) and from treating non-finite verb morphology as the core subordination mechanism. 42

### Tungusic

Genealogical scope and distribution: small family across Siberia and northern China; recent open-access survey work emphasizes endangerment and historical contact patterns. 43

Representative languages: Evenki, Nanai, Manchu (heritage/literary).

Phonological profile: family-internal variation; areal contact effects are prominent; inventories differ widely across languages. 44

Morphological typology: agglutinative, with rich derivation, case, and non-finite morphology typical of northern Eurasian head-final profiles. 43

Core syntax: strong head-final tendencies; postpositions; clause chaining. 43

Key grammatical categories: extensive non-finite verb forms; person marking varies; contact-induced change is a recurring theme. 44

Discourse/pragmatics: discourse cohesion often realized through converbs and clause-linking; small speech communities and intense contact produce rapid microvariation. 44

Learning implications:  
Primary burden is morphological (case + non-finite verb systems). High leverage: learn a small set of case functions and converb constructions that dominate narratives. 43

### Altaic / Transeurasian (controversial macrofamily)

Status: “Altaic” (classically Turkic–Mongolic–Tungusic, sometimes extended to Koreanic/Japonic) is widely treated as unproven; recent “Transeurasian” proposals revive a broader grouping using both lexical and phylogenetic methods, but substantial criticism remains and many specialists interpret shared traits as areal diffusion plus typological convergence rather than common descent. 45

What is typologically stable across the putative set (regardless of genealogy): a recognizable northern Eurasian profile—SOV, agglutinative suffix chains, rich non-finite morphology, postpositions, and often vowel harmony—can emerge through both inheritance and contact. Therefore, family-level pedagogy should treat “Transeurasian” as an areal/typological learning bundle rather than a settled phylogenetic tree. 41

## Oceania and Australia profiles

### Austronesian

Genealogical scope and distribution: one of the world’s largest families; Glottolog lists 1276 languages and broad dispersal across Island Southeast Asia, the Pacific, and Madagascar. 46

Representative languages: Indonesian/Malay, Javanese, Tagalog/Filipino, Malagasy, Fijian.

Phonological profile: highly diverse; both stress-based and tone/pitch systems occur; prosodic surveys emphasize the breadth of Austronesian prosodic patterns rather than a single template. 47

Morphological typology: ranges from relatively analytic (Indonesian) to morphologically rich systems with complex voice/alignment phenomena in “Philippine-type” languages; typological work highlights voice systems where verbal morphology selects the privileged argument (often discussed as “Philippine-type alignment,” itself theoretically debated). 48

Core syntax: widespread verb-initial orders in many Oceanic and Philippine-type languages; SVO also common; Austronesian provides many canonical cases for studying word order–information structure interactions. 9

Key grammatical categories: rich voice systems in some branches; inclusive/exclusive distinctions frequent; reduplication productive; numeral classifiers in some languages. 48

Discourse/pragmatics: discourse particles and clause chaining vary; in many languages, voice alternations are used for discourse prominence and information structure. 48

Learning implications:  
For analytic Indonesian-type targets: focus on derivation, reduplication, and pragmatic particles; for Philippine-type targets: treat voice morphology as core syntax, not as optional alternation, and learn constructions via argument-structure templates. 48

### Trans-New Guinea (TNG) and wider “Papuan” non-Austronesian groupings

Genealogical scope and controversy: “Trans-New Guinea” is a major proposed macro-family in New Guinea; current conservative practice distinguishes a “Nuclear Trans New Guinea” subset and stresses that more conservative classifications recognize a larger number of separate demonstrated families. 49

Papuan ≠ a single family: “Papuan languages” is explicitly a negative label for non-Austronesian languages of New Guinea and surroundings; they belong to many distinct families and isolates (Foley’s classic formulation emphasizes at least dozens of families and several isolates, recommending conservative classification). 50

Representative languages (illustrative only, given diversity): Enga (TNG), Dani varieties (TNG), Yimas (Lower Sepik), Kuot (isolate; not covered in detail here).

Phonological profile: extremely diverse; New Guinea is a global hotspot of genetic and typological diversity, so family-level generalizations are weak. 25

Morphological and syntactic tendencies (areal, not universal): Papuan languages often show head-final profiles, postpositions, and rich verbal morphology; however, verb-medial patterns and prepositions occur, and contact with Austronesian shapes outcomes. Foley’s survey details that “Papuan” is not a typological monolith. 50

Key grammatical categories: switch-reference and complex clause chaining appear in some areas; numeral systems and body-part tally systems are also relevant in parts of the region, but these are not reconstructable across all proposed TNG branches (recent areal scholarship stresses limited reconstruction). 51

Learning implications:  
For fieldwork and learning, the key is to avoid “Papuan stereotypes.” Each target language requires grammar-first acquisition. Where verb morphology is rich, early mastery of clause-chaining and argument indexing yields the highest payoff for comprehension. 50

### Australian families (Pama-Nyungan and non-Pama-Nyungan)

Genealogical scope and controversy: Australia contains one very large family (Pama-Nyungan) plus multiple non-Pama-Nyungan families; phylogenetic and areal histories are actively researched, including models of Pama-Nyungan expansion and debates about subgrouping and contact. 52

Representative languages (illustrative): Warlpiri (Pama-Nyungan), Dyirbal (Pama-Nyungan), Warlmanpa (Pama-Nyungan).

Phonological profile: many Australian languages have rich consonant inventories with multiple coronal places; phonological typology is regionally patterned. 53

Morphological typology: case marking is often central; morphology is typically concatenative; ergative patterns (often split by tense/aspect or person) are frequent, enabling relatively free word order. 54

Core syntax: word order can be flexible because case carries grammatical relations; areal features such as switch-reference occur in certain contiguous areas and are explicitly treated as areal (not purely genealogical). 55

Key grammatical categories: rich sets of case suffixes and clitics; complex auxiliary systems in some languages; clause linking strategies. 56

Discourse/pragmatics: discourse-driven word order and reference tracking are crucial; case-marking interacts with topicality and focus. 55

Learning implications:  
The bottleneck is learning to read case morphology and clitic systems as the primary carriers of argument structure, rather than relying on “English-like” word order. For comprehension, case-first parsing is essential. 56

## Americas and major isolates

### Eskimo-Aleut

Genealogical scope and distribution: Arctic/subarctic family; Glottolog provides membership references and notes. 57

Representative languages: Greenlandic (Kalaallisut), Inuktitut varieties, Central Alaskan Yupik, Aleut (Unangam Tunuu).

Phonological profile: morphophonology and phonotactics are tightly coupled to morphology; long-word formation interacts with phonological processes. 58

Morphological typology: exceptionally polysynthetic; Fortescue characterizes the family as among the most polysynthetic, with very large inventories of bound suffixes and “internal syntax” within words. 59

Core syntax: clause structure varies across branches; the key property is that many clausal relations are realized within morphological word forms. 59

Key grammatical categories: extensive derivational affixes, mood and clause-type marking, and complex agreement/indexing; word-level composition can encode what are full sentences in more analytic languages. 59

Discourse/pragmatics: the density of morphological encoding supports compact discourse; derivational choices provide fine-grained event structure. 58

Learning implications:  
The learner task is morphological, not syntactic in the narrow sense: mastery requires learning productive affix inventories and their combinatorics. Pedagogically, begin from high-frequency “word-templates” (common postbases) and expand via controlled composition exercises. 58

### Na-Dene (Athabaskan–Eyak–Tlingit; and Dene–Yeniseian debate)

Genealogical scope: Na-Dene in the narrow sense typically includes Athabaskan (Dene), Eyak (extinct), and Tlingit; Glottolog provides detailed membership notes and explicitly treats some proposed larger relations (e.g., Dene–Yeniseian) as not yet mature enough to conclude. 60

Representative languages: Navajo (Athabaskan), Apache languages, Tlingit.

Phonological profile: complex consonantism is common; Athabaskan languages often have tone; segment inventories can be large and include ejectives/aspiration contrasts. 61

Morphological typology: polysynthetic, with strong prefixing and templatic verb morphology (especially Athabaskan), making verb-word parsing central. 61

Core syntax: SOV tendencies; argument structure is heavily encoded in the verb; nouns can be optional because pronominal indexing is rich. 61

Key grammatical categories: complex aspectual systems; classificatory verb stems in some languages; elaborate derivational prefix slots. 61

Discourse/pragmatics: reference tracking relies on verbal indexing; clause chaining and subordination are often realized via verbal morphology. 61

Learning implications:  
The central strategy is to treat the verb as the clause: learn prefix-slot templates and high-frequency aspectual paradigms before trying to “translate word-by-word.” 61

### Algic (focusing on Algonquian)

Genealogical scope: Algic includes Algonquian plus the California languages Yurok and Wiyot; Glottolog lists 47 languages for the family and provides internal classification. 62

Representative languages: Plains Cree, Ojibwe, Mi’kmaq, Blackfoot.

Phonological profile: varies; morphophonology is tightly integrated with complex verbal morphology. 63

Morphological typology: strongly polysynthetic with rich verbal inflection, including person hierarchies and direct–inverse marking; obviation provides a reference-tracking system. 10

Key grammatical categories: proximate/obviative distinctions; direct–inverse morphology; animacy hierarchies. 10

Discourse/pragmatics: reference tracking is central; systems are explicitly analyzed as integrating semantic role prominence and pragmatic topicality. 63

Learning implications:  
Learners should treat Algonquian verbal morphology as a discourse engine: practice narrative discourse early, because obviation and inverse are not adequately learned from isolated sentences. 63

### Mayan

Genealogical scope and distribution: Mesoamerican family; Glottolog provides classification and references; modern surveys describe the family as typically head-marking and often verb-initial with ergative morphology (with important cross-language variation). 65

Representative languages: Yucatec Maya, K’iche’, Kaqchikel, Tzotzil.

Phonological profile: diverse; some branches have tone; glottalization contrasts are frequent. 65

Morphological typology: agglutinative to moderately synthetic with strong head-marking; ergative–absolutive person marking on the verb is a classic typological theme; voice systems interact with ergativity. 66

Core syntax: verb-initial orders are common, though word order can vary by language and construction; VOS is widely discussed for certain Mayan languages. 67

Key grammatical categories: aspect is often more central than tense; status markers and clause-type morphology vary; ergativity interacts with extraction in some languages (syntactic ergativity debates). 68

Discourse/pragmatics: discourse is shaped by voice choices and extraction constraints; information structure conditions order and voice. 66

Learning implications:  
Learners benefit from an aspect-first approach and from learning person-marking paradigms as a single integrated system (set A/B markers). Practice should focus on clause types and extraction contexts, because they drive alternations. 66

### Oto-Manguean

Genealogical scope and distribution: major Mesoamerican family; Glottolog provides classification; typological literature emphasizes that Oto-Manguean is uniformly tonal and that tone can function in inflection, producing complex inflectional class systems. 69

Representative languages: Zapotec varieties, Mixtec varieties, Otomi varieties, Triqui varieties.

Phonological profile: tone is central and often complex; some languages have large tone inventories; tone interacts with morphology and clitic systems. 70

Morphological typology: mixed; some Oto-Manguean languages are relatively isolating, but inflection can be complex via tonal and morphophonological alternations; research on grammatical tone in Triqui and related languages exemplifies this. 71

Core syntax: varies by branch and language; both VSO and SVO and other orders are attested, frequently conditioned by information structure. 9

Key grammatical categories: tonal inflection for person/aspect in some languages; inflectional classes with layered exponence (suffixes + tone + stem alternations). 72

Discourse/pragmatics: tone and clitics can encode focus and clause type; narrative styles rely on these systems for cohesion. 70

Learning implications:  
The main pedagogical principle: treat tone as morphology as well as phonology. Learners must practice paradigms with tone alternations, not only “word tones.” 70

### Major isolates and additional widely recognized families

Isolates are languages with no demonstrated close relatives; this is a classificatory statement contingent on current evidence, not a claim about absolute uniqueness. 73

Basque: widely treated as a language isolate in Europe; typological overviews emphasize its ergative morphology and rich agreement system. 74

Ainu (Ainuic): widely described as an isolate (or small Ainuic family of closely related varieties); scholarship highlights its SOV order and polysynthetic tendencies, with long-standing attention to incorporation and applicatives. 75

Caucasian families: three indigenous families are commonly recognized—Kartvelian (South Caucasian), Northwest Caucasian, and Nakh-Daghestanian (Northeast Caucasian)—with abundant cross-linguistically unusual phonologies (ejectives, rich consonant inventories) and ergativity patterns, and no consensus on deeper genetic relations among the three. 76

Other widely recognized families/foci (briefly, because full profiles would require a separate volume):

*   Mande (often discussed in Niger-Congo debates; tone and grammatical tone are important in some languages). 77
*   Nilo-Saharan (a proposed phylum with continuing debate about unity; large areal spread). 78
*   “Khoisan” is not accepted as a single genetic family by specialists; current practice recognizes families such as Khoe–Kwadi, Kx’a, and Tuu (plus East African Hadza/Sandawe not demonstrably related to southern groups). 79
*   Major South American families with strong typological signatures: Arawakan (notable in evidentiality literature), Tupian, Cariban, Quechuan, etc. 80
