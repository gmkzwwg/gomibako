## Continental Philosophy: Language, Style, and Core Concepts

### Core Concepts and Vocabulary

- **Dasein (German, “being-there”)** – Heidegger’s term for human existence. Literally _Da_ “there” + _Sein_ “being”, Dasein names the mode of being unique to humans (Da-sein).
> **Dasein（德语，“此在”）** – 海德格尔（Heidegger）用于描述人类生存的术语。字面直译为 *Da*（此/那里）+ *Sein*（在/存在）。“此在”指涉了人类特有的存在方式（Da-sein/在之中的那个人）。
- **Intentionality** – (from Husserl) consciousness is always _about_ something: thoughts are directed toward objects. The _intentional content_ (noema) is how an object appears to consciousness.
> **Intentionality（指向性/意向性）** – （源自胡塞尔）意识总是关于某物的（*about* something）：思想总是指向对象。**意向内容（noema/意向客体）**指对象在意识中呈现出的方式。
- **Epoché (Greek)** – Husserl’s “suspension” or “bracketing” of the natural attitude to examine pure experience. It “suspends” (neutralizes) our dogmatic beliefs about reality to analyze consciousness itself.
> **Epoché（希腊语，“悬置/中止判断”）** – 胡塞尔对“自然态度”的“悬置”或“括号化”，旨在考察纯粹经验。它“悬置”（中立化）了我们关于现实的教条式信念，以便分析意识本身。
- **Lebenswelt (Ger., “lifeworld”)** – pre-reflective everyday world of lived experience (Husserl’s term for the background of meaning).
> **Lebenswelt（德语，“生活世界”）** – 前反思的、被体验到的日常世界（胡塞尔术语，指涉意义生成的背景）。
- **Transcendental** – (Kant, then Husserl) denotes the _conditions of possibility_ of experience. Husserl’s _transcendental reduction_ returns attention from the natural world to those constitutive conditions.
> **Transcendental（先验的）** – （康德，后为胡塞尔）指经验的**可能性条件（conditions of possibility）**。胡塞尔的**先验还原（transcendental reduction）**将注意力从自然世界回归到这些构成性条件上。
- **Sein vs. Seiendes (Being vs beings)** – Heidegger’s **ontological difference** between _Sein_ (Being as such) and _das Seiende_ (entities). This distinction underlies much of Heidegger’s thought about what it means “to be.”
> **Sein vs. Seiendes（存在与存在者）** – 海德格尔的**存在论差异（ontological difference）**：区分 *Sein*（存在本身）与 *das Seiende*（实存的实体/具体存在者）。这一区分是海德格尔探讨“‘存在’意味着什么”的基石。
- **Existenz precedes Essenz (Eng., “existence precedes essence”)** – Sartre’s famous phrase (Fr. _l’existence précède l’essence_) that in human life the fact of existing comes before any fixed nature. It reverses the traditional view that essence (nature) is prior to existence.
> **Existenz precedes Essenz（存在先于本质）** – 萨特（Sartre）的名言（法文 *l’existence précède l’essence*），主张在人类生活中，存在的客观事实先于任何既定的本质。它颠覆了本质（本性）优于存在的传统观点。
- **Mauvaise foi (Fr., “bad faith”)** – Sartrean concept for self-deception about one’s freedom.
> **Mauvaise foi（法语，“自欺/自欺丧志”）** – 萨特关于个人在自身自由问题上自我欺骗的概念。
- **Affirmation/Aufhebung** – Hegelian terms: _Aufheben_ (Ger.) means literally “to lift up” but carries the contradictory senses of “to cancel,” “to preserve,” and “to sublate.” Hegel’s dialectical _Aufhebung_ preserves and changes the original in one movement.
> **Affirmation/Aufhebung（肯定/扬弃）** – 黑格尔（Hegelian）术语：*Aufheben*（德语）字面意思是“举起”，但包含着“废除”、“保存”和“提升”的三重矛盾含义。黑格尔辩证法中的**扬弃**在同一运动中既保留又改变了原初物。
- **Phänomenologie (Phenomenology)** – philosophical method of describing structures of experience (Husserl, Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty). Key notions: _Noesis_ (act of consciousness) vs _noema_ (content of consciousness).
> **Phänomenologie（现象学）** – 描述经验结构的哲学方法（胡塞尔、海德格尔、梅洛-庞蒂）。核心范畴：**Noesis（意向行为/意识活动）**与 **Noema（意向客体/意识内容）**。
- **Dialektik (Dialectic)** – argument or method of thesis–antithesis–synthesis (Hegelian dialectic, also used broadly for back-and-forth critique). In Hegel, contradiction and sublation drive progress.
> **Dialektik（辩证法）** – 正-反-合（thesis–antithesis–synthesis）的论证或方法（黑格尔辩证法，亦广义地指代往复式的批判）。在黑格尔看来，矛盾与扬弃驱动着进步。
- **Genealogie (Genealogy)** – (Nietzsche, Foucault) a historical technique tracing the evolution of ideas, questioning their “natural” emergence. It interrogates the _conditions of possibility_ of beliefs and exposes the contingent power-relations shaping them.
> **Genealogie（谱系学）** – （尼采、福柯）一种追踪思想演变的史学技术，质疑思想的“自然”涌现。它审问信念的**可能性条件**，并揭示塑造它们的偶然权力关系。
- **Aporie (Greek, “impasse”)** – a conceptual deadlock or paradox. Continental writers often deliberately reach an **aporia** to show that a question cannot be resolved within the existing conceptual scheme.
> **Aporie（希腊语，“困境/无路可走”）** – 概念上的死胡同或悖论。欧陆作家常刻意达到一种**离解（aporia）**状态，以展示某个问题在现有的概念框架内是无法解决的。
- **Hermeneutik (Hermeneutics)** – theory of interpretation (e.g. Gadamer). Involves the **hermeneutic circle** (interpreting parts in terms of whole and vice versa) and concepts like _Horizontverschmelzung_ (fusion of horizons).
> **Hermeneutik（解释学/阐释学）** – 关于理解与解释的理论（如伽达默尔）。涉及**解释学循环（hermeneutic circle）**（根据整体理解部分，反之亦然）以及**地平线融合（Horizontverschmelzung/视域融合）**等概念。
- **Différance (Fr.)** – Derrida’s coined term (pronounced like “différence”) combining _différer_ (“to defer”) and _différer_ (“to differ”). It signals that meaning is generated by differences between signs and is constantly deferred.
> **Différance（法语，“延异”）** – 德里达（Derrida）创造的术语（发音同“différence”），结合了 *différer*（推迟）与 *différer*（差异）。它标志着意义是由符号之间的差异生成的，并且不断被推迟。
- **Trace** – (Derrida) the mark of absence in presence; the way a sign carries an ungraspable remainder.
> **Trace（痕迹）** – （德里达）现场（在场）中留下的缺席标记；指符号携带了无法把握的剩余物。
- **Supplement (supplément)** – (Derrida) an “extra” that paradoxically completes and displaces the original, undermining any supposed fullness.
> **Supplement（supplément/补遗/补充）** – （德里达）一种“额外之物”，矛盾地完整了原初物又取代了原初物，从而削弱了任何所谓的“充盈性”。
- **Archéologie / Archival discourse** – (Foucault) analyzing the underlying rules of discourse in a given epoch. Foucault’s _episteme_ denotes the unconscious framework of knowledge at a historical moment.
> **Archéologie / Archival discourse（考古学/档案话语）** – （福柯）分析特定时代话语背后的潜在规则。福柯的**认识论（episteme）**指涉了历史时刻中潜意识下的知识框架。
- **Power/Knowledge** – (Foucault) the insight that power relations and knowledge production are inseparable; what counts as “truth” is shaped by power structures.
> **Power/Knowledge（权力/知识）** – （福柯）这一见解认为权力关系与知识生产是不可分割的；何为“真理”是由权力结构塑造的。

### Key Distinctions

- **Being vs Being (Sein vs. seienden)** – Heidegger stresses that _Being_ (Sein) is not just another being; it is the **ground** for beings. This _ontological difference_ (between beings and the Being of beings) is Heidegger’s central concern.
> * **Being vs. Seiendes (Sein vs. Seiendes / 存在与存在者)** —— 海德格尔强调，**存在**（*Sein*）并非仅仅是另一种存在者；它是存在者的**根据**（ground）。这种“存在论差异”（即存在者与存在者的存在之间的区别）是海德格尔关注的核心问题。
- **Essence vs Existence** – The distinction reversed by existentialists: traditionally an entity’s essence (nature) was thought prior to its existence; existentialists insist existence comes first.
> * **Essence vs. Existence (本质与存在)** —— 这一被存在主义者颠倒的区别：传统上，一个实体的本质（本性）被认为先于其存在；而存在主义者坚持“存在先于本质”。
- **Structure vs Event** – In post-structural debates (e.g. Badiou vs structuralists), a clear distinction is drawn between enduring structures of meaning and singular disruptive events. Continental writing often plays these off each other without fully collapsing them.
> * **Structure vs. Event (结构与事件)** —— 在后结构主义的辩论中（如巴丢对阵结构主义者），意义的持久**结构**与单一的、破坏性的**事件**之间被划定了清晰的区别。欧陆写作经常在两者之间游走，而不使其中一方完全消解于另一方。
- **Phenomenon vs Noumenon** – (Kant) phenomena are appearances to us; noumena (“things-in-themselves”) are beyond appearance. Husserl sidesteps noumena by treating experience itself as phenomenon.
> * **Phenomenon vs. Noumenon (现象与物自身/本体)** —— （康德术语）**现象**是对我们显现的表象；**物自身**（本体）则是表象之外的事物。胡塞尔通过将经验本身视为现象，规避了对物自身的探讨。
- **Subject vs Object** – Continental texts often blur the subject/object split: e.g. Heidegger’s Dasein is “being-in-the-world” (no detached subject), Lacan’s “subject of the unconscious,” etc.
> * **Subject vs. Object (主体与客体)** —— 欧陆文本经常模糊主客体之分：例如，海德格尔的“此在”是“在世界之中存在”（不存在孤立脱离的主体），拉康的“无意识主体”等。
- **Immanence vs Transcendence** – Many continental thinkers emphasize immanence (e.g. Hegelian Spirit immanent in history, Spinoza), but they also speak of a transcendent _ring_ (Derrida’s “messianicity without messianism,” Heidegger’s Wesen).
> * **Immanence vs. Transcendence (内在性与超越性)** —— 许多欧陆思想家强调内在性（如黑格尔的精神内在干历史之中，斯宾诺莎的实体等），但他们也谈论某种超越性的“回响”（如德里达的“没有救世主主义的救世主性”，海德格尔的“本质/本现” *Wesen*）。

### Rhetorical and Argumentative Techniques

- **Dialectical Method** – Continual interplay of opposites (thesis/antithesis), with each negating yet preserving the other (Aufhebung). Questions are probed by pushing them into contradiction and then seeing what emerges.
> **Dialectical Method (辩证法)** —— 对立面（正/反）持续的相互作用，每一方都在否定另一方的同时也保留了对方（扬弃 *Aufhebung*）。通过将问题推向矛盾点并观察随后产生的结果来深入探究。
- **Genealogical Critique** – Tracing a concept’s history to uncover its contingent origins. Rather than seeking an origin, genealogy shows how “truths” change over time and serve power, revealing multiple, conflicting origins.
> **Genealogical Critique (谱系学批判)** —— 追踪一个概念的历史以揭示其偶然的起源。谱系学并非寻找某种单一的初源，而是展示“真理”如何随时间演变并服务于权力，从而揭示出多元且冲突的起源。
- **Aporia and Embedding** – Instead of winning an argument by force, many continental texts reach a puzzle or contradiction (aporia) to highlight hidden assumptions. Critique often “embeds” the opponent’s valid point into a broader problem: what looks like a victory is actually evidence of a deeper issue.
> **Aporia and Embedding (难题与嵌入)** —— 许多欧陆文本并不试图通过强力赢得争论，而是触及某种困境或矛盾（难题/离解 *aporia*），以凸显隐藏的假设。批判经常将对手的有效观点“嵌入”到一个更广泛的问题中：看起来的胜利实际上是更深层问题的证据。
- **Hermeneutic Circle** – In interpretation, one moves back and forth between parts and whole (text and context) to deepen understanding. Gadamer emphasizes how prejudices and historical context shape all interpretation.
> **Hermeneutic Circle (解释学循环)** —— 在理解过程中，人们在部分与整体（文本与语境）之间反复移动以深化理解。伽达默尔强调前见（prejudices）和历史语境如何塑造了所有的解释活动。
- **Topos and Trope** – Continued use of key images or metaphors (e.g. “clearing” (Lichtung), “horizon,” “clearing,” “destruction” (Destruktion) vs “construction”). Continental styles favor such evocative tropes rather than enumerating examples.
> **Topos and Trope (处所与转义/隐喻)** —— 持续使用关键意象或隐喻（例如“林中路/林中空地” *Lichtung*、“视域/地平线”、“破坏/解构” *Destruktion* 与“建构”）。欧陆文风更偏好此类具有启发性的隐喻，而非列举具体实例。
- **Absence of strict formal proof** – Appeals often take the form of thought-experiments, phenomenological descriptions, or literary examples rather than formal logic. Writing aims to provoke insight rather than theorem.
> **Absence of strict formal proof (缺乏严格的形式证明)** —— 诉诸形式通常采取思想实验、现象学描述或文学典故，而非形式逻辑。其写作旨在激发洞见（insight）而非推导定理。

### Stylistic and Structural Features

- **Abstract, Dense Syntax** – Sentences are often long and abstract, with layers of subordination, clauses, and nominalizations. Authors may write in the passive or deploy indefinite “one finds that…”, or use constructions like “Dasein is…” or “it is said that…” to generalize.
> **抽象且稠密的句法 (Abstract, Dense Syntax)** —— 句子通常冗长且抽象，带有层层递进的从句、插入语和名词化表达。作者可能使用被动语态，或部署不定代词如“人们发现……”（one finds that…），或使用类似“此在是……”或“据说……”（it is said that…）的结构来进行概括。
- **Paradox and Negative Definitions** – Continental authors define by what something is _not_ or by paradox. For example, Hegel defines new concepts as both preserving and negating old ones. Derrida often inverts claims (“there is no X except by way of Y”).
> **悖论与否定性定义 (Paradox and Negative Definitions)** —— 欧陆作家常通过“非某物”或通过悖论来定义。例如，黑格尔将新概念定义为对旧概念既保留又否定的产物。德里达经常倒置论断（如“除了通过 Y，不存在 X”）。
- **Qualification and Hyperbole** – Terms like “primarily,” “essentially,” “in the last resort,” “overdetermined,” are frequent. Authors insist a phenomenon “ultimately,” “fundamentally,” or “as such” means something.
> **限定与夸张 (Qualification and Hyperbole)** —— 频繁使用如“首要地”、“本质上”、“作为最后的手段”、“超前决定”（overdetermined）等术语。作者往往坚持某个现象“最终地”、“根本上”或“就其本身而言”意味着什么。
- **Temporal Emphasis** – Phrases about time are common: “always already” (German _immer schon_, Fr. _toujours déjà_) signals something pre-existing. More examples: “prior to any…,” “for the first time,” “in its essence,” etc.
> **对时间性的强调 (Temporal Emphasis)** —— 关于时间的短语非常普遍：“总是已经”（德语 *immer schon*，法语 *toujours déjà*）标志着某种预先存在的东西。更多例子包括：“在任何……之前”、“第一次”、“在其本质中”等。
- **Technicalizing the Ordinary** – Ordinary words are given special meanings (e.g. Heidegger’s “world” (_Welt_), “truth” (_Wahrheit_ as _aletheia_), “to be” (Sein) itself treated as a verb-noun).
> **平庸词汇的技术化 (Technicalizing the Ordinary)** —— 普通词汇被赋予特殊含义。例如海德格尔对“世界”（*Welt*）的重构，将“真理”（*Wahrheit*）视为“去蔽”（*aletheia*），以及将“存在”（*Sein*）本身作为动词性名词处理。
- **Citation of Canonical Texts** – Frequent allusions to authors (Hegel, Nietzsche, Husserl, Heidegger, etc.) often without explanation. Phrases may appear in quotations or as riffs on classical lines (e.g. rephrasing Aristotle, Plato, or biblical phrases).
> **引用经典文本 (Citation of Canonical Texts)** —— 频繁影射经典作者（黑格尔、尼采、胡塞尔、海德格尔等），且往往不作解释。短语可能以引文形式出现，或作为对经典名句的变奏（例如转述亚里士多德、柏拉图或圣经短语）。

### Figurative Language and Neologisms

- **Heidegger’s Wordplay** – Heidegger loves German etymology: he hyphenates compounds (e.g. _Da-sein_), coins forms (e.g. _Da-sein_ vs _Da-seyn_), and revives old spellings (“Seyn” or “Beyng” for _Sein_). He marks out roots: e.g. _Ge-stell_ (enframing) is _Gestell_ split to suggest _Gestell – stellen_. Translators note his use of hyphens and archaic forms (e.g. _seyn_, _istet_, _Ereignis_ spelled with capital).
> **海德格尔式的文字游戏 (Heidegger’s Wordplay)** —— 海德格尔钟情于德语词源学：他使用连字符连接复合词（如 *Da-sein*），创造新形式（如 *Da-sein* 对比 *Da-seyn*），并复活古拼法（用 *Seyn* 或 *Beyng* 代替 *Sein*）。他标出词根：例如将“集置”（*Ge-stell*）拆分，以暗示 *Gestell* 与 *stellen*（放置）的关系。译者通常会注明他对连字符和古体形式（如 *seyn*、*istet*、大写的 *Ereignis*）的使用。
- **Derrida’s Neologisms** – Derrida deliberately alters spellings to signal meaning-shifts. _Différance_ (with an ‘a’) conflates différer “to differ” and différer “to defer”. _Pharmakon_ (Greek) plays on “cure” vs “poison.” He coins terms like “archi-writing” (_archi-écriture_) to challenge _arche_ (origin).
> **德里达的新词 (Derrida’s Neologisms)** —— 德里达刻意改变拼写以信号化意义的转换。“延异”（*Différance*，带有字母 'a'）合并了 *différer*（差异）与 *différer*（推迟）。“药”（*Pharmakon*，希腊语）利用了其“解药”与“毒药”的双重性。他创造了诸如“原文字”（*archi-écriture*）之类的术语来挑战“原初/始基”（*arche*）。
- **Metaphors** – Concepts are often metaphorically described: e.g. Husserl’s “stream of consciousness,” Heidegger’s “clearing” (_Lichtung_) where beings appear, Foucault’s “archaeology” or “genealogy,” Badiou’s “event,” Derrida’s “trace” and “supplement.” Understanding often relies on visual or spatial metaphors (horizons, depths, chasms).
> **隐喻 (Metaphors)** —— 概念经常通过隐喻来描述：例如胡塞尔的“意识流”，海德格尔描述存在者显现的“林中空地”（*Lichtung*），福柯的“考古学”或“谱系学”，巴丢的“事件”，以及德里达的“痕迹”与“补遗”。理解往往依赖于视觉或空间隐喻（视域、深度、鸿沟）。
- **Latin and Greek Terms** – Many Greek or Latin loanwords appear un-translated (e.g. _aletheia_, _Gestell_, _Dasein_, _intentio_, _ontologia_). Writers often slip between languages when a word carries a unique resonance.
> **拉丁语与希腊语术语 (Latin and Greek Terms)** —— 许多希腊语或拉丁语借词未经翻译便直接出现（如 *aletheia*、*Gestell*、*Dasein*、*intentio*、*ontologia*）。当某个词带有独特的共鸣时，作者常在不同语言间自由切换。

### Typical Phrases and Constructions

- **“Immer schon” / “always already”** – A leitmotif: something is _“always already”_ the case, implying it precedes any determination. (E.g. “Being is _immer schon_ there before Dasein grasps it.”)
> **“Immer schon” / “Always already” （总是已经）** – 一个主导动机：某事“总是已经”如此，暗示它先于任何规定性。（例如：“在‘此在’把握‘存在’之前，存在‘总是已经’在那里了。”）
- **“What is at stake…”** – Philosophers frame importance by “what is at stake is…” or “in the last analysis, X boils down to…”
> **“What is at stake…” （问题的关键在于/利害攸关的是……）** – 哲学家通过“问题的关键在于……”或“归根结底，X 可以归结为……”来界定重要性。 
- **“It is no longer a matter of X but of Y”** – A reversal construction: e.g. “It is no longer a question of essence versus existence, but of possibility and freedom.”
> **“It is no longer a matter of X but of Y” （这不再是 X 的问题，而是 Y 的问题）** – 一种反转句式：例如，“这不再是关于本质还是存在的问题，而是关于可能性与自由的问题。”
- **Passive and Cleft Constructions** – “It is said that…”, “What counts is…”, “The decision was made…”, “The task is to think…” are common.
> **被动语态与分裂句 (Passive and Cleft Constructions)** – 常见表达如“据说……”、“重要的是……”、“做出了决定……”、“任务是去思考……”。
- **Emphasis by Negation** – “Not merely X, but X-which-is-Y,” e.g. “Not just presence, but presence-as-temporality” or “not X as substance, but X as process.”
> **通过否定进行强调 (Emphasis by Negation)** – “不仅仅是 X，而是身为 Y 的 X”，例如，“不只是在场，而是作为时间性的在场”或“不将 X 视为实体，而将 X 视为过程”。
- **Rhetorical Questions** – Often posed to shake the reader: “Who speaks of Being today?” or “Can ontology be done as before?”
> **修辞性提问 (Rhetorical Questions)** – 通常用于震慑读者：“今天谁还在谈论存在？”或“存在论还能像以前那样进行吗？”
- **“Question concerning…”** – Heidegger’s phrasing: “the question of Being” (_Seinsfrage_), “the question concerning technology.”
> **“Question concerning…” （关于……的问题）** – 海德格尔式的表述：“存在问题”（*Seinsfrage*），“关于技术的问题”。
- **“Thinking X differently”** – Writers often say “we must think X anew” or “this means thinking X from within Y.”
> **“Thinking X differently” （以不同的方式思考 X）** – 作者常说“我们必须重新思考 X”或“这意味着从 Y 内部去思考 X”。
- **References to Authorities** – “Aristotle calls…”, “as Husserl notes, …”, often without translation (e.g. quoting Latin or Ancient Greek).
> **诉诸权威 (References to Authorities)** – “亚里士多德称……”、“正如胡塞尔所注意到的……”，通常不加翻译（如直接引用拉丁语或古希腊语）。

### Reading and Writing Practice

- **Engage the Original Terms**: Whenever encountering a German, French, or Latin term (e.g. _Dasein_, _différance_, _transcendental_, _Ereignis_), note its meaning and resist the urge to replace it with a simple English gloss. Continental authors often leave such terms untranslated for effect.
> **切近原词 (Engage the Original Terms)**：每当遇到德语、法语或拉丁语术语（如 *Dasein*、*différance*、*transcendental*、*Ereignis*）时，记录其含义，并抵制将其简单替换为英语释义的冲动。欧陆作者常为了效果而保留这些词不翻译。
- **Break Long Sentences into Clauses**: Continental sentences often pack many ideas. Identify the main clause and then add subordinate clauses. Pay attention to negatives or qualifiers (“not only…but also,” “even if,” etc.).
> **将长句拆解为从句**：欧陆式句子往往承载大量思想。先识别主句，再添加从句。注意否定词或限定词（“不仅……而且”、“即使”等）。
- **Watch for Oppositions and Resolutions**: Note dialectical turns: when authors introduce a “but on the other hand,” “sublates,” or “yet it remains…” they are articulating a tension. Trace how these oppositions resolve (or intentionally don’t resolve) in the text.
> **观察对立与和解 (Watch for Oppositions and Resolutions)**：注意辩证法的转向：当作者引入“但在另一方面”、“扬弃”或“然而它依然是……”时，他们正在阐述一种张力。追踪这些对立如何在文本中得到解决（或刻意不予解决）。
- **Mark Key Phrases and Performative Verbs**: Phrases like _“man kann sagen”_ (one might say), _“es geht um”_ (it’s a question of), or verbs like _“enthüllen”_ (to unconceal), _“abheben”_, _“aufheben”_ signal important moves. Conjunctive connectors (jedoch, allerdings, somit, folglich) often indicate the logic flow.
> **标记关键短语和施事动词**：短语如 *“man kann sagen”*（可以说）、*“es geht um”*（问题的核心是），或动词如 *“enthüllen”*（去蔽/揭示）、*“abheben”*（区分/突出）、*“aufheben”*（扬弃）标志着重要的理论推进。连接词（jedoch, allerdings, somit, folglich）通常指示逻辑流向。
- **Translate the Essence**: When writing, try echoing the style: coin a German/French term and explain it (“Gelassenheit (releasement) means…”), use phrases like “one must think of X as Y,” and practice defining a term by paradox (“X is nothing other than Y-which-it-is-not”).
> **翻译其神韵 (Translate the Essence)**：写作时，尝试回响这种风格：创造一个德语/法语术语并解释它（“*Gelassenheit*（泰然任之）意味着……”），使用“必须将 X 视为 Y”等短语，并练习通过悖论来定义术语（“X 不外乎是其所不是的 Y”）。
- **Use Key Transitions**: Incorporate “in fact,” “indeed,” “thus,” “therefore,” “hence,” and double-negatives for emphasis (“not… without…”). For example, _“It is not merely… but rather…”_ or _“What counts is…”_.
> **使用关键过渡词**：结合使用“事实上”、“确实”、“因此”、“所以”、“由此”，并使用双重否定来强调（“并非……而不……”）。例如，“这不仅仅是……而是……”或“重要的是……”。
- **Practice with Examples**: Take a simple philosophical claim and rephrase it in continental style. E.g., transform “Free will exists” into _“Free will is never simply given as a fact: it must first be constituted by the choices (Entscheidungen) that Dasein confronts; in this sense existence itself must be understood as a ‘project’ (Projet), not as an essenced substance.”_
> **通过实例练习**：选取一个简单的哲学主张，用欧陆风格重述。例如，将“自由意志存在”转化为：*“自由意志绝非简单地作为一个事实给定的：它必须首先由‘此在’所面对的各种选择（Entscheidungen）所构成；在这个意义上，生存本身必须被理解为一个‘筹划’（Projet），而非一个本质化的实体。”*

By focusing on these vocabulary items, distinctions, and stylistic patterns – and seeing them in context – a student can begin to read and write continental philosophy. Embrace the original terminology alongside its translation, and practice layering definitions, contradictions, and rich metaphors. Over time, the characteristic 80/20 vocabulary and turn-of-phrase of continental thought will become familiar and usable.

## Analytic Philosophy Language

### Introduction

Analytic philosophy is known for its precision and clarity. Its language favors **clarity, economy, and simplicity**, virtues “borrowed from the realm of mathematics and logic”. The goal is to express complex ideas in straightforward terms, avoiding rhetorical excess. This tutorial covers the _20% of linguistic tools_ – vocabulary, structures, distinctions, and moves – that account for _80%_ of analytic philosophical writing. By mastering these essentials, you can both read philosophical texts more fluently and **write with the formal clarity** expected in the field.

> 分析哲学以其精确与清晰著称。其语言偏好**清晰、经济与简洁**，这些美德“借用自数学与逻辑领域”。其目标是用直截了当的术语表达复杂的思想，避免修辞上的冗余。本教程涵盖了分析哲学写作中“以 20% 的语言工具（词汇、结构、区分与论证手法）实现 80% 表达效果”的核心内容。掌握这些要点，你既能更流畅地阅读哲学文献，也能写出该领域所要求的**形式清晰度**。

We present these notes in a high-density, logically ordered format. Each section provides definitions, key sentence patterns, and examples (drawn from or inspired by real philosophical texts) to illustrate usage.

> 我们以高密度、逻辑有序的格式呈现这些笔记。每个部分都提供了定义、关键句式以及（源自或受启发于真实哲学文本的）示例，以说明其用法。

### 1\. Formal Clarity and Syntactic Minimalism

Analytic writing strives for a **formally clear, minimalist style**. Arguments are often broken into explicit premises and conclusions, and sentences are crafted to avoid ambiguity. Some guidelines:

>分析性写作追求一种**形式清晰、极简的风格**。论证通常被拆解为明确的前提与结论，句子的构建旨在消除歧义。一些准则如下：

- **Keep sentences concise and unambiguous:** Prefer short, declarative sentences or clearly structured complex sentences. Avoid unnecessary qualifiers or digressions that obscure the main point. _Example:_ Instead of “The nature of freedom, which is a concept subject to various interpretations, is arguably a matter of some debate,” write “Freedom is a contested concept.”
> **保持句子简洁且无歧义**：优选简短的陈述句或结构清晰的复合句。避免使用不必要的限定词或会遮蔽主旨的离题话。*示例*：不要写“自由的本质作为一个受多种解释影响的概念，可以说是存在一些争议的问题”，而应写成“**自由是一个具有争议的概念。**”

- **One idea at a time:** Each sentence (or tight group of sentences) should convey a single step of an argument or exposition. This syntactic simplicity makes the logical structure transparent.
> **一次只表达一个观点**：每个句子（或紧密结合的句组）应传达论证或阐述中的单一步骤。这种句法上的简洁性能使逻辑结构变得透明。
- **Explicit structure and signposting:** Use straightforward phrases to **announce your game plan**. For instance: “First, I will define X. Second, I examine Y. Finally, I argue that Z.” This helps the reader follow the argument’s progression. In academic philosophy, authors often literally enumerate points (“First…”, “Second…”, “Finally…”) to ensure clarity.
> **显式结构与预示（Signposting）**：使用直白短语来**宣布你的论证计划**。*例如*：“首先（First），我将定义 X。其次（Second），我考察 Y。最后（Finally），我论证 Z。”这有助于读者跟随论证的进展。在学术哲学中，作者经常逐一列举要点（“第一……”、“第二……”、“最后……”）以确保清晰。
- **Mathematical-style brevity:** Many analytic philosophers write with a brevity that mirrors mathematical proofs. They “seek to avoid the perceived philosophical and rhetorical excesses” of more literary styles. They might use symbolic logic or abbreviations (like _iff_, ∴, ∵) for precision. Even without symbols, the prose maintains a “just the facts” tone.
> **数学式的简练**：许多分析哲学家写作时的简练程度堪比数学证明。他们“力求避免被认为属于文学风格的哲学与修辞冗余”。为了精确，他们可能会使用符号逻辑或缩写（如 *iff* [当且仅当]、∴ [所以]、∵ [因为]）。即使不使用符号，散文也保持着一种“只陈述事实”的语调。
- **Example (clarity in action):**  
  _Imprecise:_ “Marx’s notion of value, in a sense, kind of tries to get at what might be considered the _essence_ of commodities, if you will.”  
  _Rewritten:_ “Marx’s notion of value aims to capture the essential nature of commodities.”  
  The rewritten version is shorter, clearer, and free of colloquial filler.
> **示例（清晰性的实际运用）**：*不精确*：“马恩关于价值的概念，在某种意义上，有点试图触及那些如果可以的话可能被认为是商品‘本质’的东西。”*重写后*：“**马克思的价值概念旨在捕捉商品的本质特征。**”重写后的版本更短、更清晰，且没有口语化的赘词。

**Tip:** If a sentence becomes too complex, consider breaking it into two. It’s better to be clear than to pack too much into one sentence. As one guide puts it: _cut out nonsense, avoid metaphor and analogy, don’t repeat yourself; be clear_.

> **建议：** 如果一个句子变得过于复杂，考虑将其拆分为两个。清晰比在一个句子中塞入过多信息更重要。正如一份指南所言：**删掉废话，避免隐喻和类比，不要重复自己；保持清晰。**

### 2\. Technical Vocabulary: Key Terms (with Definitions)

Analytic philosophy has a core technical vocabulary. Here are some essential terms you’ll encounter frequently, along with concise definitions:

> 分析哲学拥有一套核心技术词汇。以下是你经常会遇到的基本术语及其简洁定义：

- **Argument:** A set of statements with premises offered in support of a conclusion. Philosophical writing is built from arguments. _Example:_ “Descartes argues that **\[Premise\]** the mind can exist without the body; therefore, **\[Conclusion\]** the mind is distinct from the body.” An **argument** in philosophy isn’t a quarrel, but a logical structure consisting of claims and support.
> **论证 (Argument)**：一套命题，其中前提被用来支持一个结论。哲学写作由论证构建而成。*示例*：笛卡尔论证道：**[前提]** 心灵可以不依赖身体而存在；因此，**[结论]** 心灵与身体是截然不同的。在哲学中，“论证”不是争吵，而是一个由主张和支持理由构成的逻辑结构。
- **Premise & Conclusion:** A **premise** is a statement that provides reason or evidence, and a **conclusion** is the statement that the premises are meant to support. In a well-crafted argument, the connection is often signaled by phrases like “since…” (before a premise) or “therefore…” (before the conclusion). _Example:_ “**Premise:** All humans are mortal. **Premise:** Socrates is human. **Conclusion:** Therefore, Socrates is mortal.”
> **前提与结论 (Premise & Conclusion)**：**前提**是提供理由或证据的陈述；**结论**是前提旨在支持的陈述。在一个精心构思的论证中，这种连接通常由“既然……”（位于前提前）或“因此……”（位于结论前）等短语标示。*示例*：**[前提]** 所有人都会死。**[前提]** 苏格拉底是人。**[结论]** 因此，苏格拉底会死。
- **Valid argument:** An argument whose conclusion **necessarily follows** from its premises. In a valid deductive argument, _if_ the premises are true, the conclusion _must_ be true. Validity is about form, not the actual truth of the premises. _Example:_ “If it’s raining, the streets are wet. It’s raining. Thus, the streets are wet.” (This argument is valid; if the premises hold, the conclusion cannot be false.)
> **有效论证 (Valid argument)**：结论从其前提中**必然推导出来**的论证。在一个有效的演绎论证中，*如果*前提为真，结论*必须*为真。有效性关乎的是形式，而非前提实际的真伪。*示例*：如果下雨，街道就是湿的。正在下雨。因此，街道是湿的。（此论证有效；若前提成立，结论绝不可能为假。）
- **Sound argument:** A sound argument is a **valid** argument with **true** premises. Soundness guarantees the truth of the conclusion. _Example:_ The above argument about rain is sound _if_ it is actually raining and indeed rain always makes streets wet.
> **可靠论证 (Sound argument)**：一个**有效**且前提**为真**的论证。可靠性保证了结论的真实性。*示例*：如果确实在下雨且下雨总是会让街道变湿，上述关于下雨的论证就是可靠的。
- **Necessary condition:** A condition that _must_ hold for something to be true or occur. Formally, “Q is true only if P is true” means P is a necessary condition for Q. If Q is the case, P _has_ to be the case. _Example:_ Being a mammal is a necessary condition for being a cat (a cat must be a mammal). If an author says “X is necessary for Y,” they mean without X, you won’t have Y.
> **必要条件 (Necessary condition)**：某事为真或发生所*必须*具备的条件。形式上，“只有当 P 为真，Q 才为真”意味着 P 是 Q 的必要条件。如果 Q 成立，P *必须* 成立。*示例*：身为哺乳动物是身为猫的必要条件（猫必须是哺乳动物）。当作者说“X 对 Y 是必要的”，他们意指如果没有 X，你就不会有 Y。
- **Sufficient condition:** A condition that, if satisfied, _guarantees_ something else. “P is sufficient for Q” means _if_ P is true, then Q is true. _Example:_ Being a cat is sufficient for being a mammal (if something is a cat, it _must_ be a mammal). In logic: _P is sufficient for Q_ and _Q is necessary for P_ are equivalent claims.  
> **充分条件 (Sufficient condition)**：一旦满足就*保证*另一事物成立的条件。“P 是 Q 的充分条件”意味着 *如果* P 为真，那么 Q 为真。*示例*：身为猫是身为哺乳动物的充分条件（如果某物是猫，它*必须*是哺乳动物）。逻辑学中：*P 是 Q 的充分条件* 与 *Q 是 P 的必要条件* 是等价的命题。
  – **Necessary _and_ Sufficient:** When a condition is both necessary and sufficient, we often use the phrase **“if and only if”** (abbreviated **iff**). For example: “A figure is a triangle _iff_ it is a three-sided polygon” means (1) if it’s a triangle, then it’s a three-sided polygon (triangle → three-sided polygon, necessity), and (2) if it’s a three-sided polygon, then it’s a triangle (three-sided polygon → triangle, sufficiency). The two conditions exactly coincide.
  > **充分必要条件 (Necessary and Sufficient)**：当一个条件既是必要的又是充分的时，我们常使用短语**“当且仅当” (if and only if，缩写为 iff)**。例如：“一个图形是三角形，*当且仅当*它是一个三边多边形”意味着：(1) 如果它是三角形，那么它就是三边多边形（三角形 → 三边多边形，必要性）；(2) 如果它是三边多边形，那么它就是三角形（三边多边形 → 三角形，充分性）。这两个条件完全吻合。
- **Counterexample:** A specific case that **contradicts a general claim**. Providing a counterexample is a common way to refute a theory or definition. If someone claims “all swans are white,” a single black swan is a counterexample that disproves this universal claim. Philosophers often say “Here’s a counterexample to that argument/theory…” and then describe a scenario that conflicts with it. Using counterexamples rigorously is a hallmark of analytic method (e.g., Gettier’s famous counterexamples to the definition of knowledge).
> **反例 (Counterexample)**：一个**违背普遍主张**的具体案例。提供反例是反驳某种理论或定义的常用方法。如果有人主张“天鹅全是白的”，那么一只黑天鹅就是证明这一普遍主张为假的反例。哲学家常说“这里有一个针对该论证/理论的反例……”，然后描述一个与其相悖的情境。严谨地使用反例是分析方法的标志（例如葛梯尔针对知识定义的著名反例）。
- **Supervenience:** A technical term describing a dependency relation between two sets of properties. **A-properties supervene on B-properties** if no two things can differ in their A-properties without also differing in their B-properties. In slogan form, “there cannot be an A-difference without a B-difference.” For example, some hold that mental properties supervene on physical properties: if two situations are identical in all physical details (B-properties), they cannot differ in their mental properties (A-properties). Supervenience says **if B-properties are the same, A-properties are guaranteed to be the same**. This concept often appears in discussions of mind, morality, and other areas where higher-level properties depend on lower-level bases.
> **随附性 (Supervenience)**：描述两组属性之间依赖关系的技术术语。如果两事物在 B 属性上没有差异，那么它们在 A 属性上也不可能有差异，我们就说 **A 属性随附于 B 属性**。口号式的表达是：“没有 B 属性的差异，就没有 A 属性的差异”。例如，有人认为心理属性随附于物理属性：如果两个情境在所有物理细节（B 属性）上完全相同，那么它们在心理属性（A 属性）上也不可能有差异。随附性意指：**如果 B 属性相同，A 属性就保证相同**。这一概念经常出现在关于心灵、道德以及其他高层属性依赖于底层基础的讨论中。
- **Entailment:** A relation between statements where one statement (the premise or set of premises) **entails** another if it is impossible for the first to be true while the second is false. Entailment is like a strict logical implication. _Example:_ “X is a square” entails “X has four sides” (you can’t have a true “X is a square” and a false “X has four sides”). Philosophers use “entails” to indicate a _necessary logical connection_ between propositions.
> **蕴含 (Entailment)**：陈述之间的一种关系。如果一个陈述（前提或前提集）为真时，另一个陈述绝不可能为假，则前者**蕴含**后者。蕴含类似于严格的逻辑推论。*示例*：“X 是正方形”蕴含“X 有四条边”（你不可能在“X 是正方形”为真的同时，“X 有四条边”为假）。哲学家使用“蕴含”来表示命题之间*必然的逻辑联系*。
- **Inference:** The reasoning process from premises to conclusion. “To infer” is to draw a conclusion from evidence or premises. In writing, you may see phrases like “from this, **it follows that**…” or “one can **infer that**…”, signaling an inference.
> **推论 (Inference)**：从前提得出结论的推理过程。“推论”就是从证据或前提中提取结论。在写作中，你可能会看到诸如“由此，**可以得出结论……**”或“人们可以**推断出……**”之类的短语，它们标示着一个推论过程。
- **Theory, Hypothesis, Thesis:** In philosophy, **thesis** often means the main claim being advanced (e.g., “I will defend the thesis that free will is compatible with determinism”). **Hypothesis** might be used similarly to a supposition or proposal to test, and **theory** usually refers to a more developed system of claims or principles. Understanding these terms in context is important: e.g., “Hume’s theory of causation” or “the hypothesis of an evil demon” as a thought experiment.
> **理论 (Theory)、假设 (Hypothesis)、论题 (Thesis)**：在哲学中，**论题**通常指正在提出的主要观点（例如：“我将捍卫‘自由意志与决定论相兼容’这一论题”）。**假设**的用法可能类似于设想或待测试的提议，而**理论**通常指更成熟的主张或原则体系。在语境中理解这些词很重要：例如，“休谟的因果理论”或作为思想实验的“邪恶恶魔假设”。

_(These terms represent the core vocabulary you'll repeatedly encounter. By learning these definitions, you’ll understand statements like “Smith’s argument isn’t sound because, although valid, one premise is false (as a counterexample shows)” or “Mental properties supervene on physical properties, which means any change in mental facts requires some change in physical facts.”)_

> *(这些术语代表了你将反复遇到的核心词汇。通过学习这些定义，你将能够理解如下表述：“史密斯的论证并不可靠，因为尽管有效，但其中一个前提为假（如反例所示）”或“心理属性随附于物理属性，这意味着心理事实的任何改变都需要物理事实发生某些改变。”)*

### 3\. Key Argument Forms and Moves

Analytic philosophers frequently employ a handful of classic argument forms and reasoning strategies. Understanding these will help you both follow and formulate philosophical arguments:

> 分析哲学家经常采用几种经典的论证形式和推理策略。理解这些内容将有助于你追踪并构建哲学论证：

- **Modus Ponens (Affirming the Antecedent):** If P then Q; P is true; therefore Q is true. This form is ubiquitous. _Example:_ “**If** a being is omniscient, then it knows all truths. **God is omniscient. Therefore, God knows all truths.**” The inference is formally valid (modus ponens).
> **肯定前件 (Modus Ponens):** 如果 P 则 Q；P 为真；因此 Q 为真。这种形式无处不在。*示例*：“**如果**一个存在者是全知的，那么它知道所有真理。**上帝是全知的。因此，上帝知道所有真理。**”该推论在形式上是有效的。
- **Modus Tollens (Denying the Consequent):** If P then Q; Q is false; therefore P is false. _Example:_ “**If** the mind were purely physical, it couldn’t have non-physical properties. **But** the mind _does_ have non-physical properties (suppose we argue qualia are non-physical). **Therefore,** the mind is not purely physical.” This is a common move: assume an implication, then show the consequent fails, so the antecedent must fail.
> **否定后件 (Modus Tollens):** 如果 P 则 Q；Q 为假；因此 P 为假。*示例*：“**如果**心灵纯粹是物理的，它就不可能拥有非物理属性。**但**心灵*确实*拥有非物理属性（假设我们论证‘感质’是非物理的）。**因此**，心灵不纯粹是物理的。”这是一个常用手法：先假设一个蕴含关系，然后展示其后果失效，从而得出前件必定失效的结论。

- **Reductio ad Absurdum (Reduction to Absurdity):** A powerful argumentative move where you **assume the negation** of what you want to prove, then show that this assumption leads to a **contradiction or absurd result**. Concluding that the assumption must be false, you indirectly establish your original claim. In essence, _if denying X leads to nonsense, X must be true_.
> **归谬法 (Reductio ad Absurdum):** 一种强有力的论证手法。你先**假设**你想要证明命题的**否定面**为真，然后展示这一假设会导致**矛盾或荒谬的结果**。由于假设导致了谬误，你便间接地确立了原始主张。本质上，*如果否定 X 会导致胡言乱语，那么 X 必定为真*。
  - _Example:_ To prove that there are no round squares, one might say: **Assume (for reductio)** that a round square exists. **Then** it has a shape that is both wholly round and wholly square at the same time – a logical impossibility. **Thus,** the assumption is untenable, so **no round square can exist**.
  > *示例*：为了证明不存在“圆的正方形”，可以这样说：**（为了归谬）假设**存在一个圆的正方形。**那么**它具有一个同时既完全圆又完全方的形状——这在逻辑上是不可能的。**因此**，该假设是站不住脚的，所以**不可能存在圆的正方形**。
  - _Philosophical example:_ In ethics, one might argue **by reductio**: “Suppose there were no objective moral truths (assume moral relativism is true). Then saying _‘Nazi genocide was morally wrong’_ wouldn’t be objectively true – an absurd consequence. Therefore, the supposition is false; some moral truths must be objective.”  
    Reductio arguments often employ phrases like “Suppose, for the sake of argument, that _not-X_; then …; but that’s a contradiction/absurd; hence X.” It “seeks to establish a contention by deriving an absurdity from its denial”.
  > *哲学示例*：在伦理学中，可以**通过归谬法论证**：“假设不存在客观的道德真理（假设道德相对主义为真）。那么说‘纳粹大屠杀在道德上是错误的’就不会是客观真实的——这是一个荒谬的后果。因此，该设想是错误的；某些道德真理必须是客观的。”  
  > 归谬论证通常使用诸如“为了论证起见，假设‘非 X’；那么……；但这产生了矛盾/荒谬；因此 X 成立”等短语。它“旨在通过从否定项中推导出荒谬性来确立一个论点”。

- **Dilemma:** An argument that forces a choice between **two (or a few) options**, each of which has some consequence. A dilemma often has the form: “Either A or B. If A, then X. If B, then X. Therefore, X is inevitable.” In a **philosophical dilemma**, neither option may be appealing (hence the phrase “horns of a dilemma”).
> **两难困境 (Dilemma):** 强制在**两个（或少数几个）选项**中做出选择的论证，每个选项都有某种后果。形式通常为：“要么 A 要么 B。如果 A，则 X。如果 B，则 X。因此，X 是不可避免的。”在**哲学两难**中，两个选项往往都不讨喜（故有短语“陷入两难困境的两个犄角”）。
  - _Example (moral dilemma):_ “Either lie to save your friend (Option A), or tell the truth and betray your friend (Option B). Lying violates your moral rule against falsehood (Consequence X), while betraying your friend violates loyalty (Consequence X). **Either way,** you violate a moral principle.” Here, the person is caught on the horns of a dilemma: both choices lead to a problematic outcome.
  > *示例（道德两难）*：“要么为了救朋友而撒谎（选项 A），要么说实话而背叛朋友（选项 B）。撒谎违反了反对虚假的道德准则（后果 X），而背叛朋友则违反了忠诚准则（后果 X）。**无论哪种方式**，你都违反了一项道德原则。”
  - Philosophers often try to “escape the horns” of a dilemma by challenging the either/or choice (showing a third alternative) or denying that the consequences are truly unacceptable. Recognizing when an author is setting up a dilemma (“We have two possibilities, and neither is satisfactory…”) will help you follow their strategy.
  > 哲学家经常尝试通过挑战“非此即彼”的选择（展示第三种替代方案）或否认后果确实不可接受来“逃离两难的犄角”。识别作者何时在设置两难境地（“我们有两种可能性，但两者都不令人满意……”）将有助于你理解其策略。

- **Thought Experiment:** A **hypothetical scenario** devised to test intuitions or illustrate a concept. Thought experiments are often prefaced by “Imagine that…” or “Suppose…”. They allow philosophers to **explore consequences in imagination** when real experiments are impossible, unethical, or unfeasible.
> **思想实验 (Thought Experiment):** 为测试直觉或阐明概念而设计的**假设性情景**。思想实验通常以“想象一下……”或“假设……”开头。当现实实验不可能、不道德或不可行时，它们允许哲学家**在想象中探索后果**。
  - _Example:_ Descartes’ **Evil Demon** thought experiment: _Imagine an all-powerful demon bent on deceiving you about everything. Could you know anything for certain?_ This scenario tests the limits of skepticism.
  > *示例*：笛卡尔的**邪恶恶魔**思想实验：*想象一个全能的恶魔一心要在所有事情上欺骗你。你还能确定地知道任何事情吗？* 这个情景测试了怀疑论的极限。
  - Another famous example: **Schrödinger’s Cat** (from physics but often referenced philosophically) asks us to imagine a cat in a box that is simultaneously alive and dead until observed, illustrating paradoxes of quantum theory and the concept of superposition.
  > 伦理学中，朱迪丝·汤姆森的**小提琴家**思想实验让你想象醒来时发现自己被接在一位成名的昏迷小提琴家身上，以阐明关于身体自主权的观点。
  - In ethics, Judith Thomson’s **Violinist** thought experiment asks you to imagine waking up plugged into a famous unconscious violinist to illustrate a point about bodily autonomy (an analogy for abortion ethics). Thought experiments like these help clarify our intuitions and the implications of theories. When reading, if a paragraph starts with “Suppose…” or “Imagine…”, a thought experiment is underway. Pay attention to what principle the author is testing or demonstrating with it.
  > 思想实验有助于澄清我们的直觉和理论的影响。阅读时，如果一段话以“假设……”或“想象……”开头，说明思想实验正在进行。请关注作者正通过它测试或演示什么原则。

- **Analogy:** While analytic philosophers generally avoid flowery metaphor, they _do_ use analogies for argument. **Arguments by analogy** claim that because two situations are similar in relevant ways, what holds for one should hold for the other.  
  _Example:_ Paley’s **watchmaker analogy** compares the complexity of living organisms to a watch; since a watch’s complexity implies a designer, by analogy, so do organisms. In evaluating analogies, philosophers ask: are the two cases truly parallel in the relevant features?
> **类比 (Analogy):** 虽然分析哲学家通常避免华丽的隐喻，但他们*确实*使用类比进行论证。**类比论证**主张：因为两个情况在相关方面相似，所以适用于其中一个的情况也应适用于另一个。*示例*：帕利的**钟表匠类比**将生物体的复杂性与钟表进行对比；既然钟表的复杂性暗示了一位设计者，那么通过类比，生物体也是如此。在评估类比时，哲学家会问：这两个案例在相关特征上真的平行吗？
- **Distinguishing cases:** Philosophers often split an issue into cases to cover all possibilities. E.g., “Either determinism is true or false. If true…; if false…; in both scenarios, I will show free will is coherent.” This is related to dilemma and exhaustive reasoning. It ensures no scenario is left unaddressed.
> **区分情形 (Distinguishing cases):** 哲学家经常将问题拆分为多种情形，以涵盖所有可能性。例如：“要么决定论是真的，要么是假的。如果是真的……；如果是假的……；在这两种情况下，我将证明自由意志都是连贯的。”这确保了没有任何情景被遗漏。
- **Principle-based argument:** Sometimes arguments invoke a general principle (“Principle of Sufficient Reason,” “Kant’s Categorical Imperative,” etc.) and show how a particular case follows from it. For instance: “By the Principle of Sufficient Reason (that everything must have an explanation), there must be an explanation for the universe’s existence; thus we infer a necessary being as its explanation.” Recognizing the use of a named principle or law is key to following the logic.
> **基于原则的论证 (Principle-based argument):** 有时论证会诉诸一个普遍原则（如“充足理由律”、“康德的定言令令”等），并展示具体案例如何从中推导出来。识别对命名原则或法则的使用是理解其逻辑的关键。
- **Inference to the Best Explanation (Abduction):** Not all arguments are deductive; philosophers also use abductive reasoning: among competing hypotheses, choose the one that best explains the data. You’ll see phrases like “the best explanation for these phenomena is X, so we have reason to believe X.” This is common in philosophy of science or metaphysics.
> **溯因推理/最佳解释推理 (Inference to the Best Explanation / Abduction):** 并非所有论证都是演绎的。在竞争性的假设中，选择最能解释数据的那个。你会看到诸如“对这些现象的最佳解释是 X，因此我们有理由相信 X”之类的短语。这在科学哲学或形而上学中很常见。

Understanding these forms helps in two ways: when **reading**, you can identify “Ah, they’re doing a reductio” or “This is a dilemma setup” and anticipate the structure. When **writing**, you can employ these moves to construct strong arguments (e.g., use a thought experiment as a counterexample, or do a reductio on an opponent’s assumption).

> 理解这些形式有两个好处：在**阅读**时，你可以识别出“啊，他们在做归谬”或“这是一个两难困境的设置”并预判结构。在**写作**时，你可以运用这些手法构建强有力的论证（例如，使用思想实验作为反例，或对对手的假设进行归谬）。

### 4\. Semantic Distinctions and Logical Clarity

Analytic philosophers are careful with meanings and reference. There are classic **distinctions** you should know, as they occur often to avoid confusion:

> 分析哲学家对词义与指称极其谨慎。为了避免混淆，有几种经典的**区分（distinctions）**是你必须掌握的，因为它们在文本中频繁出现：

- **Use vs. Mention:** The _use–mention distinction_ is fundamental. **Using** a word is employing it to refer to the thing in the world; **mentioning** a word means referring to the word _itself_ (typically by putting it in quotes or italics).
  - _Example:_
    1.  **Use:** _Cheese is derived from milk._ – Here we use “cheese” to talk about the dairy product (cheese itself).
    2.  **Mention:** _“Cheese” is derived from the Old English word ċēse._ – Here we mention “cheese” as a word, not as food.  
        Philosophers often emphasize this distinction to avoid confusion. For instance, the sentence **Cats** have four letters is false (cats the animals don’t have letters), whereas **“Cats”** has four letters is true (here we’re mentioning the word “cats”). In texts, you’ll see quotes or italics to indicate mention. Failing to respect this distinction can lead to nonsense or _category mistakes_. Always ask: am I talking about the thing or the word/concept of the thing?
> **使用与提及 (Use vs. Mention)**：使用–提及区分是基础性的。**使用**一个词是用它来指代现实世界中的事物；**提及**一个词则是指代这个词**本身**（通常通过将其放入引号或使用斜体来标示）。  
> *示例：*  
> 1. **使用**：*奶酪（Cheese）源自牛奶。* ——此处我们使用“奶酪”来谈论乳制品（奶酪本身）。  
> 2. **提及**：*“Cheese”一词源自古英语词汇 ċēse。* ——此处我们提及“Cheese”这个单词，而非食物。  
> 哲学家经常强调这一区分以避免混乱。例如，“**猫**有四个字母”是错的（猫这种动物没有字母），而“**‘猫’**有四个字母”是对的（此处我们在提及“猫”这个词）。在文本中，你会看到引号或斜体来标示“提及”。不尊重这一区分会导致谬论或**范畴错误（category mistakes）**。务必自问：我是在谈论事物，还是在谈论关于该事物的单词/概念？

- **De re vs. De dicto** (Latin: “of the thing” vs “of the saying”): This distinction comes up in contexts of belief, desire, and modality, to differentiate **what** a statement is about.
  - A **de dicto** statement is about a _description or proposition_ (“what is said”), whereas **de re** is about a _particular thing_ (“the thing itself”).
  - _Example (belief context):_ Lois Lane believes that “Superman can fly.” This can be read de dicto or de re:
    - **De dicto:** Lois believes the _proposition_ “Superman can fly” – i.e., _whoever is Superman, can fly_. On this reading, she might not realize Clark Kent is Superman, so she wouldn’t assent to “Clark Kent can fly.” Her belief is about the _statement_ “Superman is flying” being true.
    - **De re:** There is some actual person (Clark Kent) such that Lois believes _of that person_ that he can fly (even if she calls him “Superman”). Here the belief is about the _thing_ (the man) rather than the proposition under a certain description.
  - _Example (desire context):_ “Jana wants to marry the tallest man in the county.”
    - **De dicto:** Jana wants it to be true that “she marries the tallest man in the county, whoever that may be.” If someone taller moves in, she’d want to marry that new tallest man.
    - **De re:** There is a particular man (say, Bob, who happens to be the tallest now) that Jana wants to marry; it just so happens he is the tallest man in the county. If a taller man moved in, Jana would still want Bob, not the new tallest.  
      Philosophers use this distinction when analyzing statements under belief, desire, or modal operators (like “necessarily”). It helps resolve seeming paradoxes. For instance, “Necessarily, the number of planets is even” can be de re (about the actual number 8, which is necessarily even) vs de dicto (about the proposition “the number of planets is even,” which was contingent because the number of planets could have been different).
> **从物与从言 (De re vs. De dicto)**：这一源自拉丁语的区分（意为“关于物”与“关于言”）出现在信念、欲望和模态语境中，用以区分陈述究竟是**关于什么**的。  
> **从言 (De dicto)** 陈述是关于一个**描述或命题**（“所言之事”）的，而 **从物 (De re)** 则是关于一个**特定事物**（“物本身”）的。  
> *示例（信念语境）：* 露易丝·莱恩相信“超人会飞”。这可以有“从言”或“从物”两种读法：  
> **从言 (De dicto)**：露易丝相信“超人会飞”这个**命题**——即：*无论谁是超人，他都能飞*。在这种读法下，她可能没有意识到克拉克·肯特就是超人，因此她不会赞同“克拉克·肯特会飞”。她的信念是关于“超人在飞行”这个*陈述*为真。  
> **从物 (De re)**：存在某个实际的人（克拉克·肯特），以至于露易丝**针对那个人**相信他会飞（即便她称他为“超人”）。此时信念是关于那个**物/人**本身，而非某种描述下的命题。  
> *示例（欲望语境）：* “雅娜想嫁给本县最高的男人。”  
> **从言 (De dicto)**：雅娜希望“她嫁给本县最高的男人（无论那是谁）”这一命题为真。如果有个更高的人搬进来，她就会想嫁给那个新的最高者。  
> **从物 (De re)**：存在一个特定的男人（比如鲍勃，他恰好是现在的最高者），雅娜想嫁给他；他恰好是本县最高的男人。如果有更高的人搬进来，雅娜依然想嫁给鲍勃，而非新来的最高者。  
> 哲学家在分析信念、欲望或模态算子（如“必然地”）下的陈述时使用此区分。它有助于解决看似矛盾的悖论。例如，“必然地，行星的数量是偶数”既可以是“从物”的（关于实际的数字 8，它必然是偶数），也可以是“从言”的（关于命题“行星的数量是偶数”，这原本是偶然的，因为行星数量本可以不同）。
- **Necessary vs. Contingent (Truths or Beings):** This is a modal distinction about whether something _must_ be the case or could have been otherwise.
  - A **necessary truth** is true in all possible worlds – its falsehood is impossible because it would entail a contradiction. _Example:_ “2+2=4” is usually considered a necessary truth; imagining it false is incoherent (negating it leads to contradiction). In philosophy, definitions or logical truths (“All bachelors are unmarried”) are necessary truths (given the meanings of the words, they cannot be false).
  - A **contingent truth** is true in the actual world but _could_ have been false under different circumstances. Its negation is conceivable without contradiction. _Example:_ “It is raining in Sydney today” is a contingent truth (true in our world if indeed raining, but clearly it might not have rained – no contradiction there). Most empirical facts (and even some philosophical claims about the world) are considered contingent – things could have been otherwise.
  - _Usage:_ Philosophers often ask “Is X necessary or contingent?” For instance, “Is it necessary that laws of nature are as they are, or are they contingent?” Or “Is moral truth necessary (true in all possible worlds) or contingent (could have been different)?” In modal logic notation: necessary truths are often prefaced with a modal operator □ (necessarily), contingent truths are those that are true but ♦ (possibly) could have been false.
  - **Necessary being vs Contingent being:** In metaphysics or philosophy of religion, a necessary being is one that _must_ exist (cannot _not_ exist, e.g., some argue God is necessary), whereas contingent beings exist but could have failed to exist. These concepts tie into arguments like the cosmological argument (which claims the cosmos requires a necessary being as its ultimate cause).  
    When reading, note words like “necessarily”, “must”, “cannot be otherwise” vs. “contingent”, “could have been”, “happens to be”. These signal the modality of the claim.
> **必然与偶然（真理或存在） (Necessary vs. Contingent)**：这是一个模态区分，关乎某事是**必须如此**还是**本可以不同**。  
> **必然真理 (Necessary truth)** 在所有可能世界中都为真——它的否定是不可能的，因为那将导致矛盾。*示例：* “2+2=4”通常被视为必然真理；想象它为假是逻辑不通的（否定它会导致矛盾）。在哲学中，定义或逻辑真理（如“所有单身汉都是未婚的”）是必然真理（给定词义，它们不可能为假）。  
> **偶然真理 (Contingent truth)** 在现实世界中为真，但在不同情况下**本可以**为假。它的否定在构思上不会产生矛盾。*示例：* “今天悉尼在下雨”是一个偶然真理（如果在下雨，在我们的世界确实为真，但显然它本可以不下雨——这并不矛盾）。大多数经验事实（甚至一些关于世界的哲学主张）被视为偶然的——事物本可以有另一种状态。  
> *用法：* 哲学家常问“X 是必然的还是偶然的？”例如，“自然规律按现状存在是必然的还是偶然的？”或“道德真理是必然的（在所有可能世界都为真）还是偶然的（本可以不同）？”在模态逻辑符号中：必然真理常以前缀模态算子 （必然地）表示，偶然真理则是那些虽然为真、但 （可能地）本可以为假的命题。  
> **必然存在者 vs 偶然存在者**：在形而上学或宗教哲学中，**必然存在者**是那种**必须**存在（不能*不*存在，例如有人主张上帝是必然的）的存在者，而**偶然存在者**虽然存在，但本可以不存在。这些概念与宇宙论证明（Cosmological argument）等论证紧密相关。  
> 阅读时，注意“必然地”、“必须”、“不能是其他样子的”等词汇，对比“偶然”、“本可以”、“恰好是”。这些词标志着主张的**模态（modality）**。
- **Analytic vs. Synthetic (truths or statements):** An **analytic** statement is true by virtue of meanings of words alone, whereas a **synthetic** statement’s truth depends on how the world is. _Example:_ “All bachelors are unmarried” is analytic (just unpacking the definition of _bachelor_), while “All bachelors are unhappy” is synthetic (it might be true or false depending on actual bachelors’ lives). This distinction (originating from Kant) is often discussed, though modern philosophers have debated it (Quine famously challenged it). It’s useful to know because the very term _analytic philosophy_ partly echoes a focus on conceptual (analytic) clarity. If an author says “that’s true by definition” or “true in virtue of meaning,” they imply analyticity.
> **分析与综合（真理或陈述） (Analytic vs. Synthetic):** **分析**陈述仅凭词义即可判定为真，而**综合**陈述的真假则取决于世界的状态。*示例：*“所有单身汉都是未婚的”是分析的（只需拆解“单身汉”的定义）；而“所有单身汉都是不快乐的”是综合的（其真假取决于实际单身汉的生活状态）。这一区分源于康德，尽管现代哲学家对此存有争议（奎因曾著名的挑战过这一区分）。了解这一点很有用，因为“分析哲学”这一术语本身在一定程度上就呼应了对概念（分析）清晰性的关注。如果一位作者说“这在定义上是真的”或“凭借意义而为真”，他们指的就是分析性。
- **A priori vs. A posteriori:** **A priori** knowledge is knowable _prior to_ or independently of experience (e.g. math truths, “All bachelors are unmarried”), whereas **a posteriori** knowledge is knowable _only through_ experience (e.g. “Some bachelors are happy” – you’d have to check real cases). This pair often accompanies analytic/synthetic: traditionally, analytic truths are a priori and synthetic truths a posteriori, though philosophers like Kripke showed there can be necessary a posteriori truths. In reading, if someone says “we know this _a priori_,” they mean it can be known by reason or understanding alone, no empirical observation needed. “A posteriori” indicates empirical evidence is required.
> **先验与后验 (A priori vs. A posteriori):** **先验**知识是在经验之**前**或独立于经验即可获知的（例如数学真理、“所有单身汉都是未婚的”）；而**后验**知识**只能通过**经验获知（例如“某些单身汉很快乐”——你必须通过实际案例来验证）。这对概念通常与“分析/综合”伴随出现：传统观点认为，分析真理是先验的，综合真理是后验的；尽管克里普克等哲学家证明了可能存在“必然的后验真理”。在阅读中，如果有人说“我们先验地知道这一点”，他们意指这可以仅凭理性或理解力获知，无需经验观察。“后验”则表明需要经验证据。
- **Object-level vs. Meta-level:** Philosophers distinguish talking about objects in the world (object-level talk) from talking about _language or statements_ about those objects (meta-level talk). This connects with use/mention and de re/de dicto. For example, an object-level question is “Is the sky blue?” whereas a meta-level question is “What do we mean when we say ‘the sky is blue’?” or “Is the statement ‘the sky is blue’ true?” Analytic philosophy often operates at the meta-level, analyzing language, concepts, and arguments themselves. This is why you’ll see discussions _about_ sentences, definitions, and propositions (meta), not just about things like sky or cats directly.
> **对象平面与元平面 (Object-level vs. Meta-level):** 哲学家区分了谈论世界中的对象（对象平面对话）与谈论关于这些对象的**语言或陈述**（元平面对话）。这与“使用/提及”以及“从物/从言”密切相关。例如，对象平面的问题是“天空是蓝色的吗？”，而元平面的问题是“当我们说‘天空是蓝色的’时是什么意思？”或“‘天空是蓝色的’这一陈述是真的吗？”分析哲学通常在元平面运作，分析语言、概念和论证本身。这就是为什么你会看到关于句子、定义和命题（元平面）的讨论，而不仅仅是直接讨论天空或猫。
- **Truth-functional vs. Non-truth-functional:** A note on logical connectives: “and, or, not, if…then” are **truth-functional** (their truth-value depends in a fixed way on the truth of components). Connectors like “because” or “necessarily” are not truth-functional in the same simple sense. Recognizing if an argument is using plain logical structure or invoking modality/causality can clarify what’s being claimed. For example, “P and Q” vs “P because Q” – the latter introduces an explanation, not just a conjunction.
> **真值函数式与非真值函数式 (Truth-functional vs. Non-truth-functional):** 关于逻辑连接词的说明：“且（and）、或（or）、非（not）、如果…那么（if…then）”是**真值函数式**的（它们的整体真值以固定的方式取决于组成部分的真值）。像“因为”或“必然地”这样的连接词在简单意义上并不是真值函数式的。识别一个论证是在使用纯粹的逻辑结构，还是在调用模态/因果关系，可以澄清其主张。例如，“P 且 Q”对比“因为 P，所以 Q”——后者引入了解释，而不仅仅是合取。

_Being precise about these distinctions prevents many misunderstandings. For example, knowing use vs mention helps you avoid confusing a concept with instances of it. Recognizing de re/de dicto prevents you from thinking someone has contradicted themselves when really they mean something in two different ways. Modal distinctions clarify whether a philosopher is talking about what **must** be the case or just what **happens to be the case**. Always pay attention to the exact phrasing – a single word like “necessarily” or a pair of quotation marks can change the meaning dramatically_.

> 精确把握这些区分可以避免许多误解。例如，了解“使用与提及”有助于避免将概念与其具体实例混淆；识别“从物/从言”可以防止你误认为某人自相矛盾，而实际上他们只是在两种层面上表达意思；模态区分则澄清了哲学家谈论的是“**必须**如此”还是“**恰好**如此”。务必关注确切的措辞——哪怕是一个像“必然地”这样的词，或一对引号，都能戏剧性地改变含义。

### 5\. Logical Connectors and Idiomatic Phrases

Analytic philosophy employs a repertoire of **connective phrases** to organize arguments and guide readers. Mastering these phrases will improve both comprehension and writing flow. Below are categories of common logical connectors and phrases, with examples:

- **Introducing assumptions or hypothetical scenarios:**
  - “**Suppose (that)…**” (e.g., “Suppose that free will is an illusion…” – used to set up a scenario or assumption to explore.)
  - “**Assume for the sake of argument that…**” (e.g., “Assume, for the sake of argument, that the soul exists…” – a temporary assumption to see where it leads.)
  - “**Let us grant** …” or “**Granted, …**” (e.g., “Let us grant that the premises are true; we still need to examine the conclusion.” – often used to concede a point provisionally.)
  - “**Imagine…**” or “**Consider a case in which…**” (used to introduce a thought experiment or example: “Imagine a world where time travel is possible…”).
> **引入假设或假设性情景：**  
> “**Suppose (that)…**” （**假设……**）：（例如：“假设自由意志是一种错觉……”——用于设定一个待探索的情景或前提。）  
> “**Assume for the sake of argument that…**” （**为了论证起见，假定……**）：（例如：“为了论证起见，假定灵魂是存在的……”——这是一种临时性的假设，旨在观察其逻辑走向。）  
> “**Let us grant…**” 或 “**Granted, …**” （**即便承认/准予……**）：（例如：“即便承认前提是真的，我们仍需审查其结论。”——通常用于暂时性地让步于某个观点。）  
> “**Imagine…**” 或 “**Consider a case in which…**” （**想象……/考虑一种……的情况**）：（用于引入思想实验或案例：“想象一个时间旅行成为可能的世界……”）。

- **Drawing inferences or stating a conclusion:**
  - “**Therefore, …**” (e.g., “The mind cannot be identical to the brain. Therefore, dualism must be true.”)
  - “**Thus, …**” (e.g., “He has a right to liberty; thus, interfering with his choices is prima facie wrong.”)
  - “**It follows that …**” (e.g., “The premises are jointly inconsistent; it follows that at least one of them is false.”)
  - “**Hence, …**” (formal way to say _therefore_. E.g., “No prime number greater than 2 is even; hence, 5 is not even.”)
  - “**Consequently, …**” or “**As a result, …**” (e.g., “Not all causes have effects that we can observe; as a result, our knowledge of causation is indirect.”)
> **进行推论或陈述结论：**  
> “**Therefore, …**” （**因此……**）：（例如：“心灵不可能等同于大脑。因此，二元论必定为真。”）  
> “**Thus, …**” （**从而/于是……**）：（例如：“他拥有自由权；于是，干涉他的选择在初步看来（*prima facie*）是错误的。”）  
> “**It follows that …**” （**由此得出/由此可见……**）：（例如：“这些前提共同构成不自洽；由此得出，其中至少有一个是假的。”）  
> “**Hence, …**” （**因此/由此……**）：（*therefore* 的正式表达。例如：“没有大于 2 的质数是偶数；由此，5 不是偶数。”）  
> “**Consequently, …**” 或 “**As a result, …**” （**结果是/因此……**）：（例如：“并非所有的原因都有我们能观察到的结果；因此，我们对因果关系的认识是间接的。”）
- **Indicating logical relation or continuation:**
  - “**Moreover, …**” / “**Furthermore, …**” (to add a supporting point: “Killing is wrong because it deprives a future. Furthermore, it causes immense suffering to loved ones.”)
  - “**Additionally, …**” (another addition in the chain of reasoning.)
  - “**In addition, …**”
  - These signal that you’re piling up reasons or elaborating the argument step by step.
> **表示逻辑关联或递进：**  
> “**Moreover, …**” / “**Furthermore, …**” （**而且/此外……**）：（用于增加支持性论点：“杀生是错误的，因为它剥夺了未来。此外，它给亲人带来了巨大的痛苦。”）  
> “**Additionally, …**” （**另外……**）：（论证链条中的另一项补充。）  
> “**In addition, …**” （**除此之外……**）  
> 这些词标志着你在堆叠理由，或是在一步步地详述论证。
- **Contrasting or presenting counterpoints:**
  - “**However, …**” (standard pivot to a contrasting point or objection: “X seems convincing. However, Y raises a problem.”)
  - “**Nonetheless, …**” / “**Nevertheless, …**” (to concede something yet introduce a contrasting fact: “The theory is elegant; nevertheless, it fails to account for consciousness.”)
  - “**On the other hand, …**” (to present the other side of an argument or a different perspective: “Some argue free will is an illusion. On the other hand, our experience of choice suggests otherwise.”)
  - “**In contrast, …**” (explicitly comparing two positions or cases.)
> **对比或提出反论点：**  
> “**However, …**” （**然而……**）：（转向对比点或异议的标准枢纽：“X 似乎令人信服。然而，Y 提出了一个问题。”）  
> “**Nonetheless, …**” / “**Nevertheless, …**” （**尽管如此……**）：（用于在承认某事的同时引入对比性事实：“该理论很优雅；尽管如此，它未能解释意识问题。”）  
> “**On the other hand, …**” （**另一方面……**）：（展示论证的另一面或不同的视角：“一些人认为自由意志是错觉。另一方面，我们对选择的体验则表明事实并非如此。”）
“**In contrast, …**” （**相比之下……**）：（明确对比两个立场或案例。）
- **Introducing an example or evidence:**
  - “**For example, …**” (e.g., “Abstract objects are outside of space and time (for example, numbers or propositions).”)
  - “**For instance, …**” (same usage as _for example_.)
  - “**Consider …**” (often used to invite the reader to think of a particular case: “Consider a scenario in which an AI becomes self-aware…”. Or simply “Consider the case of a person who…”.)
  - “**As an illustration, …**”
> **引入示例或证据：**  
> “**For example, …**” （**例如……**）：（例如：“抽象对象位于时空之外（例如，数字或命题）。”）  
> “**For instance, …**” （用法同上，表示“**譬如……**”。）  
> “**Consider …**” （**考虑/试看……**）：（常用于引导读者思考特定案例：“考虑一种人工智能获得自我意识的情境……”或简单表述为“试看一个……的人的案例”。）  
> “**As an illustration, …**” （**作为说明/举例而言……**）
- **Restating or clarifying a point:**
  - “**In other words, …**” (rephrase for clarity: “In other words, freedom requires the ability to do otherwise.”)
  - “**That is, …**” or “**i.e., …**” (from Latin _id est_, meaning _that is_. Used to clarify or specify: “All and only bachelors (i.e., unmarried adult men) are male.”)
  - “**Namely, …**” (to specify: “Two values, namely _equality_ and _freedom_, come into conflict here.”)
  - “**To put it another way, …**” (another phrasing for restating an idea differently, useful if the concept is tricky.)
> **重述或澄清观点：**  
> “**In other words, …**” （**换句话说……**）：（为了清晰而改述：“换句话说，自由要求具备‘采取其他行动’的能力。”）  
> “**That is, …**” 或 “**i.e., …**” （源自拉丁语 *id est*，意为“**即/也就是说**”：用于澄清或具体化：“所有且仅有单身汉（即，未婚成年男性）是男性。”）  
> “**Namely, …**” （**即/也就是……**）：（用于精确说明：“两种价值，即‘平等’与‘自由’，在此产生了冲突。”）  
> “**To put it another way, …**” （**换种说法……**）：（另一种改述方式，在处理棘手概念时非常有用。）
- **Conceding a point (before countering it):**
  - “**Although …, …**” (e.g., “Although the opponent’s theory explains one case, it fails in others.”)
  - “**Even if …, …**” (e.g., “Even if we grant that consciousness _emerges_ from matter, it doesn’t follow that it’s reducible to matter.”)
  - “**Granted, …**” (as an adjective at the start: “Granted, this approach has intuitive appeal. **Still,** it encounters the following paradox…”)
  - “**Of course, …**” (used to acknowledge an obvious or agreed point: “Of course, correlation is not causation, but in this case the correlation is hard to explain otherwise.”)
> **让步（在反驳之前）：**  
> “**Although …, …**” （**虽然……，但是……**）：（例如：“虽然对手的理论解释了一个案例，但在其他案例中却失效了。”）  
> “**Even if …, …**” （**即便……，也……**）：（例如：“即便我们承认意识是从物质中‘涌现’出来的，也并不意味着它可以被还原为物质。”）  
> “**Granted, …**” （作为句首词：“**诚然……**”：“诚然，这种方法具有直觉上的吸引力。**然而（Still）**，它遇到了以下悖论……”）  
> “**Of course, …**” （**当然……**）：（用于承认一个显而易见或公认的观点：“当然，相关性并不等同于因果关系，但在这种情况下，若非因果，相关性将难以解释。”）
- **Introducing an objection or question:**
  - “**It might be objected that …**” (common way to introduce a potential objection: “It might be objected that this argument begs the question.”)
  - “**One might argue that …**” (to voice a possible counter-argument: “One might argue that personal identity requires memory continuity.”)
  - “**An opponent might say …**” or “**Critics claim that …**”
  - “**A possible objection is that …**”
  - These formulations allow the writer to **simulate a dialogue**, considering how someone might dispute their claims, which is then usually answered.
> **引入异议或质疑：**  
> “**It might be objected that …**” （**可能有人会反驳说……**）：（引入潜在异议的常用方式：“可能有人会反驳说，这个论证在乞题。”）  
> “**One might argue that …**” （**有人可能会论证道……**）：（表达一种可能的反论点：“有人可能会论证道，人格同一性需要记忆的连续性。”）  
> “**An opponent might say …**” 或 “**Critics claim that …**” （**对手可能会说……/批评者称……**）  
> “**A possible objection is that …**” （**一种可能的异议是……**）  
> 这些表述允许作者**模拟一场对话**，通过考虑他人可能如何质疑自己的主张（随后通常会给出回答）来完善论证。
- **Replying to objections:**
  - “**In reply, …**” or “**To this, I respond that …**” (e.g., “In reply, the dualist can argue that mental causation is of a different sort.”)
  - “**However, this objection fails because …**”
  - “**Granted X, but …**” (granting the objection’s premise but showing it doesn’t undermine the conclusion).
> **回应异议：**  
> “**In reply, …**” 或 “**To this, I respond that …**” （**作为回应对此，我的回答是……**）：（例如：“作为回应，二元论者可以辩称心理因果属于一种不同的类型。”）  
> “**However, this objection fails because …**” （**然而，这一异议之所以失效，是因为……**）  
> “**Granted X, but …**” （**承认 X，但是……**）：（承认异议的前提，但展示它并不会动摇结论。）
- **Summarizing or concluding sections:**
  - “**In summary, …**” / “**To sum up, …**” (to recap an argument’s main points.)
  - “**Thus far, we have seen that …**” (midway summary: “Thus far, we have seen that any theory of justice must balance liberty and equality.”)
  - “**Therefore, we can conclude that …**” (final conclusion of a section or paper.)
  - “**Overall, …**”
> **总结或段落结语：**  
> “**In summary, …**” / “**To sum up, …**” （**总而言之/综上所述……**）：（概括论证的核心要点。）  
> “**Thus far, we have seen that …**” （**到目前为止，我们已经看到……**）：（中期总结：“到目前为止，我们已经看到，任何正义理论都必须平衡自由与平等。”）  
> “**Therefore, we can conclude that …**” （**因此，我们可以得出结论……**）：（章节或论文的最终结论。）  
> “**Overall, …**” （**总的来看……**）

Using these connectors appropriately is crucial. They serve as **signposts** for the reader, indicating the role each sentence plays in the argument. For example, a sentence beginning with “However” immediately tells you to expect a contrast or objection. “Therefore” tells you a conclusion or inference is coming. “Suppose” signals a hypothetical setup.

> 恰当使用这些连接词至关重要。它们为读者提供了**路标（signposts）**，标示出每个句子在论证中所扮演的角色。例如，一个以“However”开头的句子立即告诉你要预期一个对比或异议；“Therefore”预示着结论或推论的到来；“Suppose”则标志着一个假设性设定的开始。

When reading, **pay attention to these words** – they reveal the structure: Are we adding support (“moreover”), considering a new case (“for example”), acknowledging a caveat (“although”), or drawing a conclusion (“hence”)? Marking the logical transitions can greatly aid comprehension of complex passages.

> **在阅读时，请高度关注这些词汇**——它们揭示了文本结构：是在增加支持（“moreover”），考虑新案例（“for example”），承认局限（“although”），还是得出结论（“hence”）？标记这些逻辑转换点能极大辅助你理解复杂的篇章。

When writing, using these phrases will make your arguments easier to follow. Instead of writing in a stream-of-consciousness style, analytic writing explicitly _guides_ the reader: “I have given reason A. **However**, one might worry B. **In fact**, B fails because… **Thus**, A still stands.” Each connector here (“however,” “in fact,” “thus”) steers the reader through your reasoning process.

> **在写作时，使用这些短语能让你的论证更易于遵循。** 分析性写作并非“意识流”，而是显式地**引导**读者：“我给出了理由 A。**然而（However）**，有人可能会担忧 B。**事实上（In fact）**，B 并不成立，因为……**因此（Thus）**，理由 A 依然成立。”这里的每个连接词都在引领读者穿越你的推理逻辑。

### 6\. Stylistic Conventions in Analytic Writing

Beyond logic and vocabulary, analytic philosophy has a distinctive style and tone. Adopting these conventions will make your writing sound at home in philosophical discourse:

> 除了逻辑和词汇，分析哲学还拥有一套独特的风格与语调。采纳这些惯例将使你的写作在哲学话语体系中显得地道：

- **Avoid Metaphorical or Figurative Language:** Analytic philosophers generally prefer literal clarity to poetic flourish. Metaphors are used sparingly and usually only to clarify an abstract point, not to obfuscate. In fact, students are often advised to _“avoid metaphor and analogy”_ in formal arguments – or use them only with clear explanatory purpose. For example, rather than saying “Justice is the lifeblood of society,” one would more directly say “Justice is essential for a well-functioning society.” The aim is to prevent the reader from having to interpret figurative language when precision is needed. (Of course, famous philosophers do occasionally use metaphors – Wittgenstein’s “beetle in a box” or Plato’s cave – but these are carefully introduced thought experiments, not flowery embellishments.)
> **避免隐喻或比喻性语言 (Avoid Metaphorical or Figurative Language)**：分析哲学家通常偏好字面上的清晰度，而非诗意的辞藻。隐喻被极少使用，且通常仅用于澄清某个抽象观点，而非为了故弄玄虚。事实上，学生常被建议在正式论证中“**避免隐喻和类比**”——或者仅在具有明确解释目的时才使用它们。*示例*：与其说“正义是社会的命脉”，不如更直接地表述为“正义对于功能良好的社会至关重要”。其目的是防止读者在需要精确性的地方不得不去解读比喻。（当然，著名哲学家偶尔也会使用隐喻——如维特根斯坦的“盒子里的甲虫”或柏拉图的“洞穴”——但这些都是经过谨慎引入的思想实验，而非华丽的修饰。）
- **Hedging and Precision:** It’s rare to see overconfident, absolute claims in good philosophical writing. Instead, philosophers **hedge** their claims to be precise and not overstate. Common hedging words/phrases include: “**it seems plausible that…**”, “**arguably, …**”, “**it is likely that…**”, “**perhaps…**”, “**in general…**”, “**to some extent…**”, “**one might say…**”, “**we have reason to believe…**”. For example: rather than “This _proves_ that mind and body are distinct,” a cautious philosopher might write “This **suggests** that mind and body are distinct,” unless it truly is a watertight proof. Hedging shows humility and precision – acknowledging exceptions, uncertainty, or that an argument provides evidence but not indisputable proof. Use hedging to avoid making claims stronger than your evidence warrants. Readers will trust a carefully phrased “_It seems, then, that under these assumptions, X is true_” more than a bombastic “_X is definitely true_” when X is debatable.
> **限制性表述与精确性 (Hedging and Precision)**：在优秀的哲学写作中，极少见到过度自信、绝对化的主张。相反，哲学家会通过**限制性表述（Hedging）**来确保精确且不夸大其词。常用的限制性词汇/短语包括：“**……似乎是合理的**”（it seems plausible that…）、“**可以论证的是……**”（arguably, …）、“**很可能……**”、“**或许……**”、“**通常情况下……**”、“**在某种程度上……**”、“**人们可能会说……**”、“**我们有理由相信……**”。*示例*：除非真的是无懈可击的证明，否则一位谨慎的哲学家不会写“这*证明了*心身是分离的”，而会写“这**暗示了（suggests）**心身是分离的”。**限制性表述体现了谦逊与精确**——它承认了例外、不确定性，或者承认某个论证只提供了证据而非不容置疑的判准。使用此类表述可以避免提出超出证据支撑范围的过强主张。面对有争议的话题，读者会更信任措辞谨慎的“*那么，在这些假设下，X 似乎是真的*”，而非夸大其词的“*X 绝对是真的*”。
- **Nominalization and Abstract Nouns:** Philosophical writing often uses **nominalizations** – turning verbs/adjectives into nouns – to discuss abstract concepts. For instance, instead of saying “when people are free,” one might discuss “**freedom**”; instead of “X is similar to Y,” you’ll see talk of “the **similarity** between X and Y”. This allows sentences to pack complex ideas as subjects or objects. Compare: _“We assume people are rational when making choices.”_ vs. _“Our account relies on an assumption of **rationality** in choice.”_ The second phrasing uses the nominalization “rationality” and sounds more at home in analytic prose. Nominalizations can make sentences denser but enable succinct reference to concepts. Don’t overdo it to the point of obscurity, but know that phrases like “the justification of belief” or “the permissibility of the action” are common, where everyday writing might use a clause (“that a belief is justified” or “that the action is permissible”).
> **名词化与抽象名词 (Nominalization and Abstract Nouns)**：哲学写作经常使用**名词化**——将动词/形容词转化为名词——来讨论抽象概念。*例如*：与其说“当人们自由时”，不如讨论“**自由（freedom）**”；与其说“X 与 Y 相似”，你会看到关于“X 与 Y 之间的**相似性（similarity）**”的探讨。这使得句子可以将复杂的思想作为主语或宾语。*对比*：“我们假设人们在做选择时是理性的。” vs. “我们的说明依赖于选择中的**理性（rationality）**假设。”后一种表述使用了名词化后的“理性”，在分析哲学散文中听起来更地道。名词化虽然会让句子变得稠密，但能实现对概念的简洁引用。不要过度使用到晦涩难懂的程度，但要意识到像“信念的证成（the justification of belief）”或“行为的可容许性（the permissibility of the action）”这类短语在哲学中非常普遍，而在日常写作中可能会使用从句（“一个信念是被证成的”或“该行为是可容许的”）。
- **Preference for Formality and Neutral Tone:** Write in a **neutral, impersonal tone**. Avoid colloquialisms, jokes (unless making a point with a light touch), or emotional language. For example, say “This view faces a serious challenge” rather than “This view is totally ridiculous.” Let the force of your logical argument, not colorful adjectives, do the persuading. Even when you disagree strongly, you’d write “However, this argument fails to account for XYZ” instead of “This argument is stupid.” The latter is inappropriate in scholarly discourse. The tone is **polite, measured, and analytic**. That said, _controlled_ use of first person (“I”) is absolutely fine (see next point).
> **偏好正式与中立的语调 (Preference for Formality and Neutral Tone)**：请使用**中立、非个人化**的语调写作。避免口语、笑话（除非是为了轻微地点明某个观点）或情感化的语言。*示例*：应说“这一观点面临严峻挑战”，而非“这一观点完全荒谬”。请让逻辑论证的力量（而非色彩鲜艳的形容词）去说服读者。即使你强烈反对某事，也应写道“然而，这一论证未能考虑到 XYZ”，而非“这个论证很愚蠢”。后者在学术话语中是不恰当的。这种语调是**礼貌、克制且具分析性的**。即便如此，*受控地*使用第一人称（“我”）是完全没问题的（见下一点）。
- **Use of First Person vs. Impersonal language:** Unlike some academic fields that discourage writing “I” or “we,” philosophy often encourages clear attribution of arguments to persons, including yourself. It’s common to see phrases like “**I will argue that…**”, “**I suggest…**”, or “**As we saw earlier,…**”. Using “I” can actually make writing clearer and more direct (“I have shown P; now I will consider an objection to P”). The old-fashioned impersonal style (“the author believes that…” or using passive voice like “it will be shown that…”) can make writing clunky. Many instructors prefer “I” for clarity. For example: “In this essay, **I argue** that personal identity is not what matters for survival.” This is more straightforward than “This essay will argue that personal identity is not what matters…”. Don’t overuse “I” to inject mere opinion (“I feel that…” is weak); use it to take ownership of claims (“I will prove that…”) or to guide the reader (“I will first define terms, then…”). Some writers use “we” to include the reader in the journey (“We have seen that…; now we must consider…”), which is also acceptable. Historically, novice philosophers sometimes avoided “I” (using “one” or a passive voice), but even established analytic philosophers use first person for clarity. The key is: be clear about who is claiming what – using first person can help in doing so.
> **第一人称与非人称语言的使用 (Use of First Person vs. Impersonal language)**：与某些不鼓励使用“我（I）”或“我们（we）”的学术领域不同，哲学通常鼓励将论证明确归于个人，包括作者自己。你经常会看到诸如“**我将论证……**”、“**我建议……**”或“**正如我们此前看到的……**”之类的短语。使用“我”实际上可以使写作更清晰、更直接（“我已经证明了 P；现在我将考虑一个针对 P 的异议”）。老式的非人称风格（如“作者认为……”或使用被动语态“……将被证明”）会使写作变得笨拙。许多导师更喜欢用“我”来确保清晰度。例如：“在本篇论文中，**我论证**人格同一性对于生存而言并非关键所在。”这比“本篇论文将论证人格同一性并非……”更为直接。注意不要滥用“我”来强加纯粹的个人观点（“我觉得……”这种表达很软弱）；要用它来表明对主张的所有权（“我将证明……”）或引导读者（“我将首先定义术语，然后……”）。有些作者使用“我们”来拉近读者，共同探索（“我们已经看到……；现在我们必须考虑……”），这也是可以接受的。关键在于：明确谁在主张什么——使用第一人称有助于实现这一点。
- **Explicit structure and signposting (again):** Stylistically, a well-written philosophy piece often **telegraphs its structure**. For instance: “**In Section 1, I explain the concept. In Section 2, I present an argument for it. In Section 3, I address objections.**” While you might not always write in sections, even in a paragraph you might say: “**First**, let’s define free will. **Second**, I will outline the determinist’s challenge. **Finally**, I will propose a reconciliation.” These signposts greatly aid readability, and instructors/readers appreciate them. They make the logical flow overt.
> **明确的结构与预示 (Explicit structure and signposting)**：在风格上，一篇优秀的哲学作品通常会**预告其结构**。例如：“**在第一部分，我解释该概念。在第二部分，我提出针对它的论证。在第三部分，我回应异议。**”即便不分章节，你在段落中也可以说：“**首先**，让我们定义自由意志。**其次**，我将概述决定论者的挑战。**最后**，我将提出一种调和方案。”这些预示极大地提高了可读性，导师和读者都很看重这一点。它们使逻辑流向变得透明化。
- **Honing definitions and distinctions:** A large part of analytic style is carefully **defining terms** and **disentangling distinct ideas**. You’ll often see sentences like “By **mind** I mean the subject of conscious experience, not merely brain activity,” or “It’s important to distinguish **justice** in the sense of fairness from justice as retribution.” Including definitions (especially for how _you_ intend a term in your argument) is expected. A tutorial like this one itself is full of those _‘In other words, X means…’_ constructions. In your own writing, don’t shy from explicitly saying “I will use the term **agency** to mean the capacity for intentional action.” That prevents confusion later.
> **磨炼定义与区分 (Honing definitions and distinctions)**：分析性风格的一个重要部分是仔细地**定义术语**并**厘清不同的想法**。你经常会看到这样的句子：“我所说的**心灵**是指意识经验的主体，而不仅仅是大脑活动，”或者“将公平意义上的**正义**与报应意义上的正义区分开来是很重要的。”提供定义（尤其是你在论证中打算如何使用某个术语）是预料之中的。像本教程本身就充满了“换句话说，X 意味着……”这类结构。在写作中，不要回避明确说明：“我将使用‘**主体性（agency）**’一词来指代进行意向性行动的能力。”这能防止后续的混淆。
- **Citation and engagement with sources:** Philosophers meticulously cite sources and often explicitly engage others’ arguments. You might see “As _Rawls (1971)_ argues, …” or “_Nagel’s_ position on consciousness can be summarized as follows…”. When you write, acknowledge whose idea you’re discussing (“According to Aristotle, …”). Analytic style values giving credit and also clearly delineating _your_ contributions versus someone else’s.
> **引用与对文献的深度参与 (Citation and engagement with sources)**：哲学家严谨地引用来源，并经常显式地参与他人的论证。你可能会看到“正如 *罗尔斯 (1971)* 所论证的那样……”或“*内格尔* 关于意识的立场可以总结如下……”。写作时，请注明你正在讨论谁的想法（“根据亚里士多德的观点……”）。分析性风格重视对他人的致敬，同时也重视清晰地划定**你自己的贡献**与他人的见解之间的界限。
- **Footnotes/endnotes:** In philosophy papers, it’s common to relegate some material to footnotes – often historical notes, tangential points, or references. A running joke is that some philosophy papers have more footnote text than main text! Use them judiciously: main text for core argument, footnotes for clarifications or asides. (For learning purposes, you don’t need to emulate excessive footnoting, but be aware when reading that juicy arguments or definitions might hide in footnotes.)
> **脚注/尾注 (Footnotes/endnotes)**：在哲学论文中，将部分内容移至脚注是非常普遍的做法——这些内容通常包括历史背景说明、次要论点或文献引用。哲学界有一个流传甚广的笑话：有些哲学论文的脚注字数甚至比正文还要多！**原则**：请审慎使用它们。正文应保留给核心论证，脚注则用于澄清或旁述。**阅读建议**：在学习过程中，你不需要刻意模仿那种过度的脚注风格，但在阅读时要留意，一些精彩的论辩或关键定义可能正隐藏在脚注里。
- **Consistency in terminology:** Once you define or use a term, stick to it. Don’t swap between synonyms if they might carry slight differences. If you start by talking about “happiness” in a theory, don’t later switch to “pleasure” unless you mean something different – if you do, clarify the distinction. Philosophers often introduce a term and then consistently capitalize it or use the same phrase every time for the specific concept (e.g., “the Principle of Utility (PoU)” and then use “PoU” throughout). This consistency helps the reader track exactly what you mean.
> **术语的一致性 (Consistency in terminology)**：一旦你定义或使用了一个术语，请始终坚持使用它。不要随意更换近义词，因为细微的词义差别可能会引发逻辑混乱。如果你在理论初期讨论的是“幸福（happiness）”，除非你另有所指，否则不要在后文切换成“快乐（pleasure）”——如果你确实要切换，请务必澄清两者之间的区别。哲学家通常会引入一个术语，随后在全文中一致地将其首字母大写，或始终使用相同的短语来指代该特定概念（例如：“功利原则（Principle of Utility, PoU）”，并在后文中统一使用“PoU”）。这种一致性有助于读者精准地追踪你的本意。
- **Avoiding rhetorical questions:** While not a strict rule, many philosophical writers avoid excessive rhetorical questions because they can confuse whether the author is actually asking the reader or making a point. Instead of “So, does personal identity matter for moral responsibility?” one might say “The question arises whether personal identity matters for moral responsibility. I will argue that it does not.” This way you pose the question and immediately indicate your stance or the next step, rather than leaving it hanging.
> **避免修辞性提问 (Avoiding rhetorical questions)**：虽然这不是一项硬性规定，但许多哲学作家会避免过度使用修辞性提问，因为这可能让读者困惑：作者究竟是在真诚发问，还是在陈述某个观点？与其问：“那么，人格同一性对于道德责任重要吗？”，不如说：“接下来的问题是，人格同一性对于道德责任是否重要。**我将论证它并不重要。**”这种方式提出了问题并立即表明了你的立场或下一步计划，而不是让问题悬而不决。
- **Charitable interpretation:** When writing about other philosophers, analytic convention is to interpret arguments **charitably** – meaning present others’ arguments in their strongest, most reasonable form (even if you will ultimately disagree). You’ll see phrases like “One might interpret Kant as claiming X… To be charitable, let’s assume he means Y, which is more defensible.” Adopting this tone in your own writing – not strawmanning opponents, but engaging their best version – is expected in the field.
> **同情式理解/宽容原则 (Charitable interpretation)**：当评述其他哲学家时，分析哲学的惯例是进行**同情式理解**（即“宽容原则”）。这意味着你要以对方最强有力、最合理的形式来呈现其论点（即使你最终并不同意）。你经常会看到这类措辞：“有人可能将康德的主张解释为 X……为了体现宽容，让我们假设他的意思是 Y，因为 Y 显然更站得住脚。”在写作中采用这种语调——**不把对手的观点“稻草人化”**（strawmanning），而是与其最完美的版本交锋——是该领域的基本专业素养。

_(In sum, the stylistic ideal is a piece of writing that reads like a clear, rational dialogue. It anticipates potential misunderstandings, heads them off with precise language and definitions, and leads the reader through a well-signposted path of argumentation. The prose is plain but not dull – it is **exact**. Where ordinary writing might leave implications unstated, analytic writing spells them out. Where everyday speech might exaggerate, analytic writing qualifies. This does make it dense, but that density carries information, not confusion.)_

> （总而言之，这种风格理想是创造出一种读起来像清醒、理性的**对话**的作品。它预判潜在的误解，通过精确的语言和定义将其化解，并引领读者走过一条标志清晰的论证路径。其散文朴实但不沉闷——它是**严丝合缝**的。日常写作可能对某些暗示点到为止，而分析性写作则将其逐一阐明；日常言语可能夸大其词，而分析性写作则谨慎限制。这确实使其显得稠密，但这种稠密承载的是信息，而非混乱。）

### Conclusion and Next Steps

With the above toolkit of vocabulary, distinctions, argument forms, and stylistic tips, you have what you need to _immediately start engaging_ with analytic philosophy texts. As you read, keep this guide handy: identify the logical connectors, note when a term is being defined or a distinction drawn, and dissect arguments into their form (is it a modus ponens? a reductio?). Practice writing short paragraphs on philosophical questions using these elements: define your terms, state a clear thesis, support it with a few logically connected sentences (“because…, therefore…”), consider an objection (“however, one might object that…”), and respond.

> 凭借上述词汇工具包、区分维度、论证形式和风格技巧，你已经具备了**立即开始参与**分析哲学文本讨论的所需条件。在你阅读时，请随手翻阅这份指南：识别逻辑连接词，留意术语何时被定义或区分何时被划定，并将论证剖析为具体的形式（这是肯定前件吗？还是归谬法？）。尝试利用这些元素就哲学问题撰写短小的段落：定义你的术语，陈述一个明确的论题，用几个逻辑相连的句子提供支持（“因为……，所以……”），考虑一个异议（“然而，有人可能会反驳说……”），并做出回应。

By focusing on this **20%** of language patterns, you’ll cover the majority of what appears in academic philosophy papers and books. Over time, you’ll expand your vocabulary (encountering terms like “supererogatory” or “epistemic injustice”), and you’ll see more nuanced argument moves. But the foundation will remain: **clarity, logical structure, and conceptual precision**.

> 通过专注于这 **20%** 的语言模式，你将涵盖学术哲学论文和著作中出现的大部分内容。随着时间的推移，你会扩大词汇量（遇到诸如“超义务的” [supererogatory] 或“认识不义” [epistemic injustice] 等术语），并见到更细微的论证手法。但其基础始终保持不变：**清晰性、逻辑结构和概念精确性**。

In analytic philosophy, _how_ you say something is almost as important as _what_ you say – because if the expression is not clear, the idea cannot be properly evaluated. Fortunately, the conventions of the field are all about making your ideas clear and arguments robust. As you apply this guide, you’ll gradually “think in analytic prose,” which will speed up both your reading comprehension and your ability to formulate your own philosophical thoughts rigorously.

> 在分析哲学中，“**如何说**”几乎与“**说什么**”同样重要——因为如果表达不清晰，思想就无法得到恰当的评估。幸运的是，该领域的惯例完全是为了让你的思想变得清晰、论证变得稳固。当你运用这份指南时，你会逐渐学会“用分析性散文思考”，这将同时提高你的阅读理解能力和严谨构建个人哲学思考的能力。
